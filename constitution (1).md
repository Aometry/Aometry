## PART 1: NAME, PURPOSES, AND FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES

### Rule 1: Name

(1) The name of the incorporated association is **Source Party Australia Incorporated** (the Association).

(2) The Association operates publicly as "Source Party" and may use state-specific branding including "Source Party Victoria", "Source VIC", or similar variations as approved by the Committee.

---

### Rule 2: Definitions

In this Constitution, unless the contrary intention appears:

**"AGM"** means Annual General Meeting.

**"Association"** means Source Party Australia Incorporated.

**"Committee"** means the National Committee of the Association elected under Rule 14.

**"Committee member"** means a member of the National Committee.

**"Eligible to Vote member"** means:

- (a) Any Paid Member in good standing (membership fees current and not suspended), OR
- (b) Any current Committee member (regardless of membership class).

**"Fee Unit"** means the amount fixed from time to time under section 7 of the Monetary Units Act 2004 (Cth). As at the date of adoption of this Constitution, one Fee Unit equals $16.81. All fees and financial thresholds in this Constitution are expressed in Fee Units and automatically adjust with statutory indexation.

**"Financial year"** means the 12-month period ending on 30 June.

**"Founding Member"** means one of the first 100 persons admitted as Paid Members of the Association.

**"Free Member"** means a member admitted under Rule 6(2) who has not paid membership fees but maintains registration for electoral roll purposes.

**"General Meeting"** means the Annual General Meeting or a Special General Meeting of the Association.

**"Member"** means a member of the Association admitted under Part 2.

**"NCAP"** means Negative Consent Approval Protocol, the decision-making framework specified in Rule 49 and Schedule B.

**"Paid Member"** means a member who has paid the applicable annual membership fee under Rule 8 and holds voting rights at General Meetings.

**"Public Officer"** means the person appointed under section 58 of the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic).

**"Root Axiom"** means the foundational principle of the Association:

> "Identify and eliminate systemic inefficiency and unnecessary suffering."

From this axiom derive the Association's core values and policy framework. All constitutional interpretation, policy development, and operational decisions must accord with the Root Axiom.

The Root Axiom may only be amended by special resolution under Rule 82(2)(a) (Class 1 Amendment: 90% affirmative vote of 75% of Eligible to Vote members).

**"Secretary"** means the person holding office as Secretary of the Association under Rule 16.

**"SGM"** means Special General Meeting.

**"State Branch"** means a branch of the Association established under Part 5 for a particular Australian state or territory.

**"Treasurer"** means the person holding office as Treasurer of the Association under Rule 17.

**"Value Gates"** means the interpretive principles derived from the Root Axiom:

- Transparency (elimination of information inefficiency)
- Evidence (elimination of ideological suffering)
- Efficiency (elimination of bureaucratic waste)
- Adaptability (elimination of rigid system failure)
- Fairness (elimination of distributive suffering)

As further specified in Schedule A.

**"Working Group"** means a delegated body established under Part 4 to execute specific functions with NCAP authority.

### Rule 3: Purposes

(1) The purposes of the Association are:

(a) To advance democratic governance in Australia through evidence-based policy development and systematic reduction of inefficiency and suffering;

(b) To contest elections at local, state, and federal levels and to support the election of candidates committed to the Root Axiom;

(c) To develop and promote public policy guided by the Root Axiom and Value Gates framework;

(d) To engage citizens in democratic participation through transparent, accessible, and technologically-enabled processes;

(e) To build community connections and empower individuals to effect systemic change;

(f) To maintain and operate as a political party registered under applicable Commonwealth and State electoral legislation;

(g) To promote political education and civic literacy among members and the broader community;

(h) To collaborate with aligned organizations, movements, and individuals in pursuit of the Root Axiom;

(i) To demonstrate innovative governance models including automated decision-making, open-source infrastructure, and high-velocity operations; and

(j) To do all things incidental or conducive to the attainment of these purposes.

(2) The Association is established exclusively for the purposes set out in subrule (1) and not for the profit or gain of individual members.

(3) The income and property of the Association must be applied solely towards the promotion of the purposes of the Association and no part may be paid or transferred directly or indirectly to members except:

(a) In good faith in return for services actually rendered or goods actually supplied;

(b) As reasonable remuneration to Committee members or staff under Rule 21;

(c) As interest at a rate not exceeding current bank rates for moneys lent; or

(d) As reasonable rent for premises let to the Association.

---

### Rule 4: Root Axiom Framework

(1) All activities, policies, decisions, and constitutional interpretations of the Association must advance the Root Axiom.

(2) When evaluating any proposal, policy, or decision, members and officers must consider:

(a) **Inefficiency Identification:** What systemic inefficiencies does this address or create?

(b) **Suffering Identification:** What unnecessary suffering does this reduce or cause?

(c) **Evidence Basis:** What empirical evidence supports this approach?

(d) **Alternatives Assessment:** Are there more efficient or less harmful alternatives?

(e) **Systemic Impact:** What second-order effects and feedback loops result?

(3) The Committee must publish an annual Root Axiom Impact Report assessing how the Association's activities advanced or hindered the Root Axiom during the financial year.

(4) Any member may challenge a decision, policy, or action as inconsistent with the Root Axiom by lodging a written objection with the Secretary. The Committee must consider such objections and respond with written reasoning within 14 days.

---

### Rule 5: Value Gates

(1) The Value Gates derived from the Root Axiom provide interpretive guidance for policy development and operational decisions.

(2) The five Value Gates are:

(a) **Transparency:** Information systems must be open, accessible, and auditable to eliminate information asymmetry and hidden inefficiencies.

(b) **Evidence:** Decisions must be grounded in empirical data, scientific research, and rigorous analysis to eliminate ideologically-driven suffering.

(c) **Efficiency:** Processes must minimize waste, bureaucracy, and resource consumption to maximize impact per unit of effort.

(d) **Adaptability:** Systems must incorporate feedback mechanisms, sunset provisions, and iterative improvement to eliminate rigid failure modes.

(e) **Fairness:** Resource distribution and opportunity access must reduce unjust suffering and systemic inequality.

(3) Schedule A provides detailed application guidelines for each Value Gate.

(4) When Value Gates conflict in a particular decision, the Committee must:

(a) Document the conflict explicitly;

(b) Assess which resolution best advances the Root Axiom overall;

(c) Publish reasoning for the prioritization chosen; and

(d) Review the decision annually to assess outcomes.

(5) The Value Gates may be refined or expanded by special resolution under Rule 82(2)(b) (Class 2 Amendment: 80% supermajority).

## PART 2 - MEMBERSHIP

### Rule 8: Membership Fees

(1) The annual membership fee for Paid Members is determined by membership tier based on the applicant's capacity to pay:

(a) **Student/Youth Tier:** 0.5 Fee Units per year (for full-time students or persons under 25);

(b) **Concession/Low-Waged Tier:** 1 Fee Unit per year (for concession card holders or low-income earners);

(c) **Standard Waged Tier:** 2 Fee Units per year (standard rate);

(d) **High Waged Tier:** 4 Fee Units per year (for persons whose individual annual income exceeds the median individual income in Australia as published by the Australian Bureau of Statistics); and

(e) **Solidarity Tier:** 6 or more Fee Units per year (voluntary higher contribution).

(2) The applicable tier rates in dollar terms are specified in Schedule C and automatically adjust with Fee Unit indexation.

(3) Applicants self-select their tier based on their circumstances. The Association operates on an honour system and does not verify income or eligibility.

(4) "Median individual income" for the purposes of subrule (1)(d) means the most recently published median employee earnings figure from the Australian Bureau of Statistics. The Committee must update the applicable income threshold annually and publish it on the Association website.

(5) Founding Members receive a 50% discount on their selected tier for the first year of membership only.

(6) Free Members pay no membership fee.

(7) **Payment Methods and Frequency:**

(a) Members may pay fees:

- (i) Annually in advance;
- (ii) Multi-annually in advance (2, 3, or 5 year terms) with no discount;
- (iii) Monthly by automated payment (annual fee divided by 12);
- (iv) Weekly by automated payment (annual fee divided by 52); or
- (v) At such other intervals as the Committee may approve.

(b) Members who pay monthly or weekly must maintain continuous payment. A missed payment triggers the conversion procedure under Rule 11(5).

(c) Members who pay in advance for multiple years lock in their tier rate at the Fee Unit value applicable at time of payment.

(8) The Committee may:

(a) Waive or reduce fees in cases of demonstrated financial hardship;

(b) Accept payment plans for members unable to pay fees at standard intervals; and

(c) Grant temporary Free Member status to Paid Members experiencing financial difficulty.

(9) Fees are non-refundable except:

(a) Where an application for membership is rejected;

(b) Where the Association dissolves before the membership period expires (prorated refund);

(c) Where a member paid in advance and resigns during the cooling-off period under Rule 12(5) (prorated refund); or

(d) At the Committee's discretion in exceptional circumstances.

(10) **Discretionary Donations:**

(a) Any person (including Free Members and non-members) may make discretionary donations to the Association in addition to or in lieu of membership fees.

(b) Discretionary donations do not confer membership rights.

(c) A Free Member who makes discretionary donations does not thereby become a Paid Member and does not acquire voting rights.

(d) To acquire voting rights, a Free Member must specifically pay the applicable membership fee under subrule (1), regardless of donation amount.

(e) All donations are acknowledged and receipted in accordance with electoral and taxation law.

### Rule 11: Duration and Renewal of Membership

(1) **Paid Membership Duration:**

(a) Paid Membership is valid for 12 months from the date of initial fee payment.

(b) Members who pay multi-annually in advance have Paid Membership valid for the full prepaid term (24, 36, or 60 months).

(c) Members who pay monthly or weekly have Paid Membership valid while payments remain current.

(2) **Free Membership Duration:**

Free Membership continues indefinitely until resigned under Rule 12 or terminated under Rule 13.

(3) **Renewal Notices:**

The Secretary must notify Paid Members at least 30 days before their membership renewal date, including:

(a) The renewal date;

(b) The applicable fee amount (in current Fee Unit value);

(c) Available payment methods and frequencies; and

(d) Consequences of non-payment.

(4) **Automatic Renewal for Ongoing Payment Plans:**

(a) Members paying monthly or weekly have their membership automatically renewed each year while payments continue.

(b) The monthly or weekly payment amount adjusts annually to reflect Fee Unit indexation.

(c) Members must be notified at least 30 days before any payment amount increase due to indexation.

(5) **Conversion to Free Membership for Non-Payment:**

(a) A Paid Member who fails to pay a scheduled fee renewal (annual, monthly, or weekly) enters a grace period.

(b) The grace period is **2 payment cycles** from the missed payment date:

- (i) For annual payments: 2 years (24 months);
- (ii) For monthly payments: 2 months (60 days);
- (iii) For weekly payments: 2 weeks (14 days).

(c) During the grace period:

- (i) The member retains Paid Member status and voting rights;
- (ii) The Secretary must send at least 2 reminder notices;
- (iii) The member may pay the overdue amount to remain in good standing.

(d) If payment is not received by the end of the grace period:

- (i) Paid Membership automatically converts to Free Membership;
- (ii) The member loses voting rights but retains all Free Member rights;
- (iii) The member is notified in writing of the conversion;
- (iv) No penalty or fee is imposed.

(e) A member converted to Free Membership under this rule is **not considered to have resigned** and remains a member of the Association.

(6) **Reinstatement of Paid Membership:**

(a) A Free Member (including one converted under subrule (5)) may become a Paid Member at any time by paying the applicable membership fee.

(b) Reinstatement is immediate upon payment and does not require reapplication.

(c) Founding Member status is retained only if Paid Membership is reinstated within 12 months of conversion. After 12 months, Founding Member benefits are permanently forfeited.

(7) **No Penalties for Late Payment:**

The Association does not charge late fees, penalties, or interest on overdue membership fees. The only consequence of non-payment is conversion to Free Membership under subrule (5).

### Rule 12: Resignation

(1) A member may resign from the Association by written notice (including electronic notice) to the Secretary.

(2) **Cooling-Off Period:**

(a) Resignation takes effect 30 days after the notice is received by the Secretary, unless the member specifies a later date.

(b) During the 30-day cooling-off period, the member:

- (i) Retains all membership rights;
- (ii) May withdraw the resignation by written notice to the Secretary;
- (iii) Remains subject to all membership obligations.

(c) If the resignation is withdrawn during the cooling-off period, it is treated as never having been submitted.

(3) **Effect of Resignation:**

A member whose resignation takes effect:

(a) **If Paid Member who paid annually or multi-annually in advance:**

- (i) Forfeits prepaid fees for the current year;
- (ii) If resignation occurs during cooling-off period after payment, receives prorated refund;
- (iii) If resignation occurs more than 30 days after payment, no refund.

(b) **If Paid Member on monthly/weekly payment plan:**

- (i) No refund for prior payments;
- (ii) Payments cease immediately upon resignation taking effect.

(c) **If Free Member:**

- (i) No financial impact.

(d) **All resigning members:**

- (i) Cease to have any rights as a member;
- (ii) Are removed from the Register of Members;
- (iii) May reapply for membership under Rule 7 at any time after resignation takes effect.

(4) Resignation does not affect any liability the member has to the Association arising before resignation.

(5) **Inadvertent or Administrative Resignations:**

The Committee may, at its discretion, treat a purported resignation as withdrawn if:

(a) The member indicates the resignation was submitted in error or under duress;

(b) The circumstances suggest the resignation was not genuinely intended; or

(c) The member requests reinstatement within the cooling-off period.

### Rule 13: Discipline and Termination of Membership

(1) The Committee may discipline a member (including suspension or termination of membership) if the member:

(a) Breaches this Constitution or Association policies;

(b) Engages in conduct contrary to the purposes of the Association;

(c) Acts in a manner inconsistent with the Root Axiom or Value Gates;

(d) Engages in misconduct including harassment, discrimination, violence, or unlawful activity; or

(e) Brings the Association into disrepute.

(2) **[Note: Fee non-payment is NOT grounds for discipline. Non-payment results in automatic conversion to Free Membership under Rule 11(5).]**

(3) Before disciplining a member, the Committee must:

(a) Notify the member in writing of the alleged breach or misconduct;

(b) Provide the member at least 14 days to respond in writing;

(c) Consider the member's response (if any); and

(d) Give the member an opportunity to be heard at a Committee meeting (in person or by electronic means) if the member requests within 7 days.

(4) After following the procedure in subrule (3), the Committee may by majority vote:

(a) Take no action;

(b) Issue a formal warning;

(c) Suspend the member's rights (including voting rights) for a specified period;

(d) Convert a Paid Member to Free Member status (with refund of prorated fees if suspension is for serious misconduct);

(e) Terminate the membership; or

(f) Impose other sanctions as appropriate.

(5) A decision to terminate membership requires a two-thirds majority of the Committee.

(6) The Secretary must notify the member in writing of the Committee's decision within 7 days, including:

(a) The decision and reasons;

(b) Any sanctions imposed;

(c) The effective date; and

(d) The member's right to appeal under subrule (7).

(7) A member may appeal a discipline decision to the next General Meeting by written notice to the Secretary within 14 days of receiving the decision.

(8) An appeal:

(a) Must be included on the agenda of the next General Meeting;

(b) Is decided by majority vote of Eligible to Vote members present;

(c) May overturn, modify, or uphold the Committee's decision; and

(d) Is final (no further appeal).

(9) Pending an appeal, the Committee's decision remains in effect unless the Committee or General Meeting decides otherwise.

(10) A member whose membership is terminated:

(a) Ceases to have any rights as a member from the effective date;

(b) Is removed from the Register of Members;

(c) Forfeits any prepaid membership fees; and

(d) May reapply for membership after 12 months if the termination decision specified this possibility, or otherwise may not reapply.

## PART 3: NATIONAL COMMITTEE

### Rule 14: Composition of the National Committee

(1) The National Committee consists of between 5 and 9 Paid Members elected at the Annual General Meeting.

(2) The exact number of Committee positions is determined by motion at each AGM, provided it remains within the range specified in subrule (1).

(3) All Committee members hold office for a term of one year, commencing at the conclusion of the AGM at which they are elected.

(4) Committee members may be re-elected for consecutive terms without limit.

(5) The Committee must include at a minimum:

(a) A Secretary (required by law); and

(b) A Treasurer (required by law).

(6) Other Committee positions are determined by the Committee and may include:

(a) Campaign Coordinator;

(b) Policy Coordinator;

(c) Communications Coordinator;

(d) Membership Coordinator;

(e) Technology Coordinator; or

(f) Such other roles as the Committee determines.

(7) A Committee member may hold multiple roles concurrently, including Secretary and Treasurer combined if the Committee so determines.

(8) Committee members need not be Paid Members at the time of election, but:

(a) Must become Paid Members (if not already) within 30 days of election; OR

(b) If unable to pay fees due to financial hardship, may serve as Free Members during their term with Committee voting rights under Rule 9(3); BUT

(c) Must convert to Paid Member status within 90 days of their term expiring to retain voting rights at General Meetings under Rule 9(4).

### Rule 15: Election of the Committee

(1) Elections for the Committee are conducted at each Annual General Meeting.

(2) **Nominations:**

(a) Any Paid Member may nominate for election to the Committee.

(b) Nominations must be:

- (i) In writing (including electronic form);
- (ii) Signed by the nominee (indicating consent);
- (iii) Submitted to the Secretary at least 7 days before the AGM; OR
- (iv) Made from the floor at the AGM with the nominee's consent.

(c) A nominee may nominate for specific officer roles (Secretary, Treasurer) or as a general Committee member.

(3) **Voting Method:**

(a) Elections are conducted by secret ballot of Eligible to Vote members present (in person or by proxy).

(b) Each voter may vote for up to the number of Committee positions available.

(c) Officer roles (Secretary, Treasurer) are elected first by separate ballot.

(d) Remaining Committee positions are elected by general ballot.

(e) The candidates receiving the highest number of votes are elected.

(f) In the event of a tie, the tied candidates draw lots to determine the result.

(4) **Unfilled Positions:**

(a) If insufficient nominations are received to fill all Committee positions, the positions remain vacant.

(b) The Committee may fill vacant positions under Rule 19.

(5) **Uncontested Positions:**

If the number of nominations equals the number of positions available, the nominees are declared elected without ballot.

(6) **Taking Office:**

Elected Committee members take office at the conclusion of the AGM at which they are elected, unless they are filling a casual vacancy under Rule 19.

### Rule 16: Secretary

(1) The Secretary is elected under Rule 15 and holds office for one year or until a successor is elected.

(2) The Secretary is responsible for:

(a) Maintaining the Register of Members under Rule 68;

(b) Maintaining custody of the Association's records, books, and documents (except financial records held by the Treasurer);

(c) Maintaining the common seal (if any) and ensuring its proper use;

(d) Convening Committee meetings and General Meetings in accordance with this Constitution;

(e) Preparing and distributing agendas and minutes of all meetings;

(f) Conducting correspondence on behalf of the Association as directed by the Committee;

(g) Receiving and processing membership applications under Rule 7;

(h) Lodging required returns with regulatory authorities including Consumer Affairs Victoria;

(i) Ensuring compliance with the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic) and other applicable legislation;

(j) Coordinating the annual constitutional review under Rule 83; and

(k) Such other duties as the Committee determines or this Constitution requires.

(3) The Secretary may delegate administrative tasks to staff, volunteers, or automated systems, but remains responsible for ensuring tasks are completed.

(4) If the Secretary is absent or unable to perform duties, the Committee may appoint another Committee member to act as Secretary temporarily.

---

### Rule 17: Treasurer

(1) The Treasurer is elected under Rule 15 and holds office for one year or until a successor is elected.

(2) The Treasurer is responsible for:

(a) Receiving all moneys paid to or received by the Association and issuing receipts;

(b) Maintaining accurate financial records and accounts;

(c) Making payments authorized by the Committee or this Constitution;

(d) Ensuring the Association's funds are held in financial institutions approved by the Committee;

(e) Preparing financial reports for Committee meetings and General Meetings;

(f) Coordinating the annual audit or review as required under Rule 56;

(g) Preparing the annual budget for approval at the AGM under Rule 54;

(h) Ensuring compliance with financial reporting requirements under the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic) and electoral legislation;

(i) Monitoring expenditure against approved budgets and NCAP spending thresholds;

(j) Processing membership fee payments and maintaining fee payment records; and

(k) Such other financial duties as the Committee determines.

(3) The Treasurer must:

(a) Keep financial records that correctly record and explain the financial transactions and financial position of the Association;

(b) Enable true and fair financial statements to be prepared;

(c) Enable financial statements to be audited or reviewed; and

(d) Retain financial records for at least 7 years.

(4) The Treasurer may delegate routine financial tasks to staff, volunteers, or automated systems, but remains responsible for financial accuracy and compliance.

(5) If the Treasurer is absent or unable to perform duties, the Committee may appoint another Committee member to act as Treasurer temporarily.

---

### Rule 18: Public Officer

(1) The Committee must appoint a Public Officer within 14 days of incorporation or within 14 days of a vacancy occurring.

(2) The Public Officer must:

(a) Be at least 18 years of age;

(b) Reside in Victoria; and

(c) Consent to the appointment in writing.

(3) The Public Officer may be the Secretary, Treasurer, or another Committee member or member of the Association.

(4) The Public Officer is responsible for:

(a) Ensuring the Association complies with the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic);

(b) Lodging required documents with Consumer Affairs Victoria; and

(c) Acting as the liaison between the Association and Consumer Affairs Victoria.

(5) The appointment or cessation of the Public Officer must be notified to Consumer Affairs Victoria within 14 days.

---

### Rule 19: Casual Vacancies

(1) A casual vacancy occurs if a Committee member:

(a) Dies;

(b) Resigns by written notice to the Secretary;

(c) Is absent from 3 consecutive Committee meetings without apology or leave of absence approved by the Committee;

(d) Ceases to be a member of the Association;

(e) Is convicted of an indictable offence;

(f) Becomes of unsound mind or a person whose person or estate is liable to be dealt with under the law relating to mental health;

(g) Is removed by special resolution of the Association at a General Meeting; or

(h) Would otherwise be prohibited from being a Committee member under the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic).

(2) The Committee may fill a casual vacancy by appointing a Paid Member to serve until the next AGM.

(3) An appointment under subrule (2) requires a majority vote of the remaining Committee members.

(4) If the vacancy is for the Secretary or Treasurer, the Committee must fill the position within 14 days.

(5) The Committee may continue to act despite a casual vacancy or vacancies, provided at least 3 Committee members remain (including at least one of Secretary or Treasurer).

(6) If the number of Committee members falls below 3, or if both the Secretary and Treasurer positions become vacant, the remaining Committee members must:

(a) Appoint sufficient members to bring the Committee to at least 3 members with a Secretary and Treasurer within 14 days; OR

(b) Convene a Special General Meeting to elect a new Committee within 28 days.

### Rule 20: Powers and Duties of the Committee

(1) Subject to this Constitution, the Committee has the power and duty to manage the affairs of the Association and may exercise all powers of the Association except those required by this Constitution or the Associations Incorporation Reform Act 2012 (Vic) to be exercised by General Meeting.

(2) Without limiting subrule (1), the Committee may:

(a) Acquire, hold, deal with, and dispose of property;

(b) Open bank accounts and authorize expenditures;

(c) Enter into contracts and agreements on behalf of the Association;

(d) Employ staff and engage contractors or service providers;

(e) Establish Working Groups and delegate authority under Part 4;

(f) Establish State Branches under Part 5;

(g) Develop and approve Association policies, procedures, and regulations consistent with this Constitution;

(h) Approve communications and public statements under Part 8;

(i) Coordinate election campaigns and endorse candidates under Part 11;

(j) Invest funds not immediately required in authorized investments;

(k) Insure the Association, Committee members, and staff against liability;

(l) Do all things necessary or convenient for carrying out the purposes of the Association; and

(m) Delegate any of its powers to Working Groups, officers, staff, or sub-committees subject to such conditions as the Committee thinks fit.

(3) The Committee must:

(a) Act in accordance with the Root Axiom and Value Gates;

(b) Act in the best interests of the Association and its members;

(c) Exercise powers for proper purposes;

(d) Avoid conflicts of interest and disclose potential conflicts when they arise;

(e) Keep full and accurate minutes of Committee meetings;

(f) Present financial reports, activity reports, and the annual Root Axiom Impact Report to each AGM;

(g) Ensure compliance with this Constitution and applicable laws;

(h) Conduct the annual constitutional review under Rule 83; and

(i) Maintain transparent and accessible governance in accordance with Rule 4.

(4) The Committee may exercise all the powers of the Association to borrow money, give security for debts, and issue debentures, subject to approval by special resolution at a General Meeting.

### Rule 21: Remuneration of Committee Members

(1) Committee members and officers serve in a voluntary capacity but may be remunerated from Association funds subject to this rule.

(2) The Committee may approve remuneration for Committee members, officers, and other members who perform substantial work for the Association, subject to:

(a) Annual budget allocation approved by Paid Members at the AGM;

(b) Transparent disclosure of all remuneration in the annual financial report;

(c) Market-rate justification demonstrating remuneration is reasonable for the work performed; and

(d) Compliance with any applicable electoral funding and disclosure laws.

(3) **Remuneration Priority:**

When Association funds permit remuneration, priority is given to:

(a) Secretary (due to administrative and compliance workload);

(b) Treasurer (due to financial management and reporting workload);

(c) Campaign Coordinators during election periods (due to intensive electoral work);

(d) Technology Coordinator (if governance infrastructure maintenance requires substantial time); and

(e) Other roles as the Committee determines based on workload and impact.

(4) Remuneration may take the form of:

(a) Hourly or daily rates for specific tasks;

(b) Fixed stipends for ongoing roles;

(c) Project-based payments for discrete work; or

(d) Reimbursement for out-of-pocket expenses incurred on behalf of the Association.

(5) A Committee member with a direct financial interest in a remuneration decision must:

(a) Disclose the interest to the Committee;

(b) Absent themselves from Committee deliberation on the matter; and

(c) Not vote on the decision.

(6) All remuneration decisions must be:

(a) Approved by majority vote of disinterested Committee members;

(b) Recorded in Committee meeting minutes;

(c) Included in the annual budget presented to the AGM; and

(d) Disclosed in the annual financial report with recipient names and amounts.

(7) Remuneration does not affect a Committee member's duties or liabilities under this Constitution or applicable law.

(8) Committee members are entitled to reimbursement for reasonable out-of-pocket expenses incurred in performing their duties, including:

(a) Travel and accommodation for Association business;

(b) Communications costs (phone, internet);

### Rule 21B: Role Definitions and Duty Documentation

(1) **Role Definition Principle:**

All Committee roles, officer positions, and Working Group coordinator positions must have documented role definitions. If a duty is not documented as part of a role, the person holding that role is not expected to perform it.

(2) **Mandatory Role Documentation:**

(a) Each Committee position must have a written role definition maintained by the Secretary, including:

- (i) Position title;
- (ii) Term length;
- (iii) Mandatory duties (constitutional requirements);
- (iv) Standard duties (expected regular responsibilities);
- (v) Time commitment estimate (hours per week/month);
- (vi) Required skills or qualifications (if any);
- (vii) Reporting relationships;
- (viii) Delegation authorities;
- (ix) Access to Association resources (funds, systems, information); and
- (x) Performance expectations.

(b) Role definitions must be:

- (i) Approved by Committee majority vote;
- (ii) Published in the Association's documentation portal;
- (iii) Provided to nominees before Committee elections;
- (iv) Reviewed annually during constitutional review under Rule 83; and
- (v) Updated whenever duties change.

(3) **Constitutional Officer Roles:**

(a) The Secretary and Treasurer roles have mandatory duties specified in Rules 16 and 17 respectively.

(b) Additional duties beyond those specified in Rules 16 and 17 must be documented in the role definition and approved by the Committee.

(c) Any duty currently performed by a Secretary or Treasurer that is not listed in Rules 16-17 or the approved role definition is considered a **voluntary contribution**, not a role requirement.

(4) **Voluntary Contributions vs. Role Duties:**

(a) A Committee member may voluntarily perform duties beyond their documented role definition.

(b) Voluntary contributions:

- (i) Are appreciated but not expected;
- (ii) Do not automatically become role requirements;
- (iii) Do not bind successor role-holders;
- (iv) Should be acknowledged in annual reports; and
- (v) May be formalized as role duties by updating the role definition under subrule (2)(b).

(c) Examples of voluntary contributions that should NOT become undocumented expectations:

- (i) A Secretary maintaining the Association website (unless website maintenance is documented in the Secretary role definition);
- (ii) A Treasurer performing graphic design for campaigns (unless this is documented);
- (iii) A Committee member moderating community Discord channels (unless this is documented);
- (iv) An officer taking on additional projects (unless formally delegated).

(5) **Formalizing Voluntary Contributions:**

(a) If a voluntary contribution proves valuable and should continue, the Committee may:

- (i) Add it to the existing role definition (with role-holder's consent);
- (ii) Create a new role or Working Group for the function;
- (iii) Delegate it formally under Part 4;
- (iv) Engage paid staff or contractors for the function; or
- (v) Automate it through governance infrastructure under Part 13.

(b) Formalization requires:

- (i) Committee motion approving the change;
- (ii) Updated role definition documentation;
- (iii) Notice to affected role-holders; and
- (iv) Adjustment to remuneration or time commitment estimates if applicable.

(6) **Role Handovers:**

(a) When a Committee member's term ends, they must provide a handover to their successor including:

- (i) Copy of the current role definition;
- (ii) List of completed and ongoing tasks;
- (iii) Access credentials and system logins;
- (iv) Contact information for key stakeholders;
- (v) Documentation of current projects and commitments;
- (vi) List of any voluntary contributions performed beyond role definition; and
- (vii) Recommendations for successor.

(b) The handover must be documented and provided to both the successor and the Secretary.

(c) The outgoing role-holder must clearly distinguish between:

- (i) **Required duties** (documented in role definition); and
- (ii) **Voluntary contributions** (not required of successor).

(d) The successor is only expected to perform duties documented in the role definition. They may choose to continue voluntary contributions but are not obligated to do so.

(7) **Scope Creep Prevention:**

(a) If a Committee member is asked to perform duties not in their role definition, they may:

- (i) Accept voluntarily (making clear this is not a role requirement);
- (ii) Decline without consequence;
- (iii) Request the duty be formally added to their role definition (with adjustment to remuneration or time commitment);
- (iv) Request the duty be assigned to another role or Working Group; or
- (v) Request the Committee create appropriate infrastructure (automation, staff, contractors) to handle the duty.

(b) No Committee member may be disciplined or criticized for declining to perform undocumented duties.

(c) If multiple Committee members decline an undocumented duty, this signals the Committee should either:

- (i) Formalize it as a documented role with appropriate support;
- (ii) Eliminate the expectation; or
- (iii) Find alternative solutions (automation, delegation, external engagement).

(8) **Default Role Definitions:**

(a) Upon adoption of this Constitution, the Committee must prepare initial role definitions for all positions within 60 days.

(b) Until role definitions are documented:

- (i) Officers are expected to perform only the mandatory duties specified in this Constitution (Rules 16-17);
- (ii) Committee members are expected to attend meetings and vote on Committee business;
- (iii) All other activities are voluntary contributions.

(c) The initial role definitions must be based on:

- (i) Constitutional requirements;
- (ii) Realistic time commitment estimates (avoiding burnout);
- (iii) Root Axiom alignment (eliminating unnecessary duties);
- (iv) Input from current and recent role-holders; and
- (v) Availability of automation or delegation options.

(9) **Working Group Roles:**

(a) Working Group coordinator positions must also have documented role definitions approved by the Committee.

(b) Working Group role definitions follow the same principles as Committee role definitions under this rule.

(c) Working Group members (non-coordinators) are expected only to:

- (i) Contribute to Working Group objectives;
- (ii) Participate in Working Group meetings or communications; and
- (iii) Complete specific tasks they volunteer for or are assigned with their consent.

The Secretary must maintain a standard role definition template including all elements specified in subrule (2)(a). The template should be accessible to all members and used for all new roles created.

(11) **Annual Role Review:**

(a) During the annual constitutional review under Rule 83, the Committee must:

- (iii) Assess whether duties should be formalized, eliminated, or redistributed;
- (iv) Update time commitment estimates based on actual experience;
- (v) Propose remuneration adjustments if duties have expanded significantly; and

- (iii) Disclose current remuneration (if any) for each role;
- (iv) Allow nominees to ask questions about role expectations; and
- (v) Encourage nominees to review handover documentation from previous role-holders.

(c) Transparency in role expectations reduces burnout and improves Committee retention.

(13) **Protection Against Exploitation:**

(a) This rule exists to prevent volunteer exploitation and burnout.

(b) The Root Axiom requires elimination of unnecessary suffering, which includes:

- (i) Unclear role expectations causing stress;
- (ii) Undocumented duties creating unfair workload;
- (iii) Voluntary contributions becoming mandatory through social pressure;

- (i) Document the issue in writing to the Committee;
- (ii) Request role definition review and adjustment;

### Rule 22: Establishment and Purpose of Working Groups

(1) **Working Groups as Delegated Bodies:**

Working Groups are autonomous operational units established by the Committee to execute specific functions with delegated authority under the Negative Consent Approval Protocol (NCAP).

(2) **Committee Authority to Establish:**

(a) The Committee may establish, modify, or dissolve Working Groups by majority vote.

(b) Each Working Group must have a documented charter approved by the Committee specifying:

- (i) Working Group name and purpose;
- (ii) Scope of delegated authority;
- (iii) NCAP spending limits (per-proposal and cumulative);
- (iv) Coordinator role definition under Rule 21B;
- (v) Membership structure (open, appointed, elected, or hybrid);
- (vi) Reporting requirements to Committee;
- (vii) Performance metrics and success criteria;
- (viii) Review and sunset provisions;
- (ix) Relationship to other Working Groups and State Branches.

(c) Working Group charters must be documented under Rule 21A and published in the Association's documentation portal.

(3) **Default Working Groups:**

Upon adoption of this Constitution, the following Working Groups are automatically established with charters specified in Schedule D:

(a) **Communications Working Group:** Public messaging, social media, media relations, brand management, and member communications;

(b) **Policy Working Group:** Policy development, research, Root Axiom alignment assessments, and policy review;

(c) **Campaigns Working Group:** Electoral strategy, candidate support, voter outreach, and campaign coordination;

(d) **Membership Working Group:** Member engagement, onboarding, retention, community building, and conversion pipeline management;

(e) **Technology Working Group:** Governance infrastructure maintenance, community space administration, website management, digital tools, and automation systems.

(4) **Additional Working Groups:**

The Committee may establish additional Working Groups as needed, including but not limited to:

- Fundraising Working Group
- Events and Community Engagement Working Group
- Research and Analysis Working Group
- Legal and Compliance Working Group
- Regional or issue-specific Working Groups
- Time-limited project teams for specific initiatives

(5) **Working Group Membership:**

(a) Each Working Group must include at least one National Committee member to maintain Committee oversight and accountability.

(b) The Committee member(s) in a Working Group may be:

- (i) Appointed by the Committee;
- (ii) Volunteers who choose to join;
- (iii) The Working Group Coordinator (if they are a Committee member).

(c) Committee members are NOT required to be members of all Working Groups.

(d) Working Group membership models (as specified in charter):

- (i) **Open membership:** Any member may join and participate;
- (ii) **Application-based:** Members apply and are approved by Coordinator or Committee;
- (iii) **Appointed:** Committee appoints specific members based on skills;
- (iv) **Hybrid:** Combination of the above.

(e) Free Members and Paid Members participate in Working Groups on equal terms (Working Groups are not restricted by membership class).

(6) **Working Group Coordinators:**

(a) Each Working Group must have at least one Coordinator responsible for:

- (i) Convening Working Group meetings and coordinating activities;
- (ii) Managing NCAP submissions and authorizations;
- (iii) Reporting to the Committee;
- (iv) Maintaining Working Group documentation under Rule 21A;
- (v) Onboarding new Working Group members;
- (vi) Ensuring activities align with Root Axiom and charter.

(b) Coordinators are:

- (i) Appointed by the Committee; OR
- (ii) Elected by Working Group members (if charter specifies); OR
- (iii) Self-nominated and approved via NCAP (if charter specifies).

(c) Coordinator role definitions must be documented under Rule 21B.

(d) Coordinators may be Committee members or general members.

(e) Coordinators serve until resignation, removal by Committee vote, Working Group dissolution, or term expiry (if charter specifies term limits).

(7) **Dissolution of Working Groups:**

(a) The Committee may dissolve a Working Group by majority vote if:

- (i) Its purpose has been fulfilled;
- (ii) It has become inactive or ineffective;
- (iii) Its functions are better served by other means;
- (iv) It operates contrary to Root Axiom or Association purposes;
- (v) Annual review recommends dissolution.

(b) Upon dissolution:

- (i) All documentation and assets transfer to the Committee;
- (ii) Delegated authorities are revoked;
- (iii) Members are notified;
- (iv) Ongoing projects are reassigned or concluded.

(8) **Multiple Working Group Participation:**

(a) Members may participate in multiple Working Groups simultaneously.

(b) **Coordinator exclusivity:**

- (i) A member may serve as Coordinator for only ONE Working Group at a time;
- (ii) This prevents excessive workload concentration and burnout;
- (iii) Exception: During brief transition periods when handing over coordination responsibilities (maximum 30 days);
- (iv) Committee may grant exception by vote in extraordinary circumstances (maximum 90 days).

(c) **General membership limits:**

- (i) Members are encouraged to focus participation on 2-4 Working Groups where they can contribute meaningfully;
- (ii) Membership in ALL Working Groups is discouraged but not prohibited;
- (iii) Members joining all Working Groups should be mindful of:
  - Diluting their effectiveness across too many areas;
  - Creating notification fatigue and reducing engagement quality;
  - Potential for spreading themselves too thin to contribute meaningfully;
  - Root Axiom principle of efficiency (focused effort > scattered effort).

(d) **Inactive membership management:**

- (i) Working Group Coordinators may remove members who have been inactive for 90 days without participation;
- (ii) Removed members may rejoin at any time by notifying Coordinator;
- (iii) Removal is administrative housekeeping, not disciplinary action;
- (iv) Purpose: Maintain accurate membership lists and reduce notification noise for disengaged members.

(e) **Committee member participation:**

- (i) Committee members must be members of at least ONE Working Group (to maintain oversight under Rule 22(5)(a));
- (ii) Committee members are encouraged to focus on 1-3 Working Groups aligned with their Committee responsibilities;
- (iii) Committee members joining all Working Groups risk burnout and should reconsider their capacity.

(f) **Workload distribution principle:**

The Association operates on the principle that:

- (i) **Depth > Breadth:** Better to contribute meaningfully to few Working Groups than superficially to many;
- (ii) **Sustainability:** Members should manage their involvement to prevent burnout;
- (iii) **Specialization:** Developing expertise in specific areas serves Association better than generalist involvement;
- (iv) **Trust in delegation:** Members should trust other Working Groups to handle their domains rather than feeling need to oversee everything.

(g) **Onboarding guidance:**

When new members join or existing members consider adding Working Groups:

- (i) Membership Working Group should provide guidance on sustainable participation levels;
- (ii) Suggest starting with 1-2 Working Groups and expanding if capacity allows;
- (iii) Explain that quality of contribution matters more than quantity of memberships;
- (iv) Encourage members to align Working Group participation with their skills, interests, and available time.

### Rule 22A: Flat Organizational Structure and Role Separation

(1) **Flat Organizational Principle:**

(a) The Association operates on a **flat organizational structure** where:

- (i) Roles are differentiated by function and responsibility, not hierarchical authority;
- (ii) Committee membership does not create superior status over general members;
- (iii) Working Group Coordinators have authority within their Working Group domain regardless of Committee membership;
- (iv) Expertise and contribution matter more than titles or positions;
- (v) Leadership is distributed across Working Groups and roles rather than concentrated in Committee.

(b) This structure advances the Root Axiom by:

- (i) **Eliminating inefficiency:** Hierarchical approval bottlenecks slow decision-making;
- (ii) **Eliminating suffering:** Hierarchical power dynamics create unnecessary stress, gatekeeping, and exclusion;
- (iii) **Enabling velocity:** NCAP and distributed authority allow rapid action without waiting for "permission from above."

(2) **Committee Members as Participants, Not Bosses:**

(a) Within Working Groups, Committee members participate as **Working Group members**, not as supervisors or bosses.

(b) A Working Group Coordinator who is not a Committee member has **full authority** to:

- (i) Lead Working Group meetings and coordination;
- (ii) Make NCAP submissions on behalf of Working Group;
- (iii) Assign tasks and coordinate volunteer efforts;
- (iv) Represent Working Group to Committee and other stakeholders;
- (v) Make decisions within delegated Working Group scope;
- (vi) Direct the work of Committee members who are Working Group members.

(c) **Example scenarios:**

_Scenario 1:_ Communications Working Group Coordinator (general Paid Member, not on Committee) assigns a social media task to a Committee member who is part of Communications WG.

- **Appropriate:** Committee member completes task or discusses alternatives with Coordinator as peer.
- **Inappropriate:** Committee member refuses because "I'm on Committee and you're not."

_Scenario 2:_ Policy Working Group Coordinator (not on Committee) objects to a Committee member's NCAP submission in Policy WG authorization channel because it conflicts with established policy framework.

- **Appropriate:** Objection is valid regardless of relative positions. Objection hearing proceeds normally.
- **Inappropriate:** Committee member overrules objection citing Committee authority.

_Scenario 3:_ Technology Working Group Coordinator (not on Committee) decides to migrate community space to new platform and submits NCAP authorization.

- **Appropriate:** Committee members in Tech WG vote like any other WG member. Committee members outside Tech WG may exercise oversight if concerned, but should trust WG expertise.
- **Inappropriate:** Committee unilaterally reverses decision because "we're the Committee."

(3) **Committee Oversight ≠ Hierarchical Control:**

(a) The Committee has **oversight authority** over Working Groups under Rule 24(3), but this is not hierarchical control.

(b) Oversight means:

- (i) Reviewing Working Group performance and outputs;
- (ii) Intervening if Working Group acts contrary to Root Axiom or beyond delegated scope;
- (iii) Resolving disputes or conflicts between Working Groups;
- (iv) Providing strategic guidance and resources;
- (v) Holding Working Groups accountable for results.

(c) Oversight does **not** mean:

- (i) Micromanaging Working Group decisions;
- (ii) Overruling Coordinators on operational matters within their scope;
- (iii) Requiring Committee pre-approval for routine Working Group activities;
- (iv) Committee members pulling rank in Working Group discussions;
- (v) Treating Working Groups as subordinates rather than autonomous operational units.

(d) If Committee believes Working Group is acting inappropriately:

- (i) Committee should engage with Working Group collaboratively first;
- (ii) If necessary, Committee may modify Working Group charter or delegated authorities;
- (iii) In severe cases, Committee may remove Coordinator or dissolve Working Group;
- (iv) BUT these are formal governance actions requiring Committee vote, not individual Committee members asserting authority.

(4) **Expertise and Contribution Over Titles:**

(a) The Association values:

- (i) Subject matter expertise regardless of formal position;
- (ii) Demonstrated contribution and execution;
- (iii) Alignment with Root Axiom in practice;
- (iv) Collaborative and respectful engagement;
- (v) Results delivered rather than titles held.

(b) A general member with deep expertise should be:

- (i) Respected and their input weighted appropriately;
- (ii) Empowered to lead in their domain (via Working Group Coordinator role or similar);
- (iii) Not dismissed or overruled by Committee members lacking equivalent expertise;
- (iv) Encouraged to contribute fully regardless of formal position.

(c) Committee members should:

- (i) Recognize limits of their own expertise;
- (ii) Defer to Working Group expertise on operational matters;
- (iii) Contribute as peers within Working Groups, not as authorities;
- (iv) Model humility and collaborative spirit.

(5) **Prevention of Hierarchy Creep:**

(a) The following behaviors constitute **hierarchy creep** and should be actively discouraged:

- (i) Committee members expecting deference or special treatment in Working Groups;
- (ii) Committee members overruling Working Group decisions without proper Committee process;
- (iii) Working Group Coordinators being undermined by Committee members in their Working Groups;
- (iv) "We're the Committee so we decide" rhetoric in contexts where Working Groups have delegated authority;
- (v) Creating approval layers or gatekeeping beyond what this Constitution requires;
- (vi) Treating Committee membership as status symbol rather than service role.

(b) If hierarchy creep is observed:

- (i) Any member may raise concern with Committee via Secretary;
- (ii) Committee should investigate and address issue promptly;
- (iii) Persistent hierarchy creep by Committee member may be grounds for removal under Rule 19(1);
- (iv) Committee should periodically assess organizational culture and flatten structures if hierarchy developing.

(6) **Distributed Leadership Model:**

(a) The Association's leadership is **distributed** across:

- (i) National Committee (governance, strategy, oversight);
- (ii) Working Group Coordinators (operational domain leadership);
- (iii) State Branch Coordinators (regional organizing and campaigns);
- (iv) Subject matter experts and key contributors (influence through expertise);
- (v) Membership as a whole (democratic decision-making via General Meetings).

(b) This model prevents:

- (i) Single points of failure (no individual is indispensable);
- (ii) Burnout from concentrated responsibility;
- (iii) Gatekeeping and bottlenecks;
- (iv) Cult of personality or authoritarian tendencies;
- (v) Exclusion of talented members who aren't on Committee.

(c) This model enables:

- (i) High velocity decision-making (NCAP and distributed authority);
- (ii) Scalability (more Working Groups = more capacity without Committee overload);
- (iii) Resilience (organization continues functioning if Committee members step down);
- (iv) Member empowerment and engagement;
- (v) Innovation and experimentation at Working Group level.

(7) **Committee Role Clarity:**

(a) The Committee's role is to:

- (i) Set strategic direction and priorities;
- (ii) Ensure constitutional compliance and Root Axiom alignment;
- (iii) Manage Association finances and resources;
- (iv) Coordinate across Working Groups and State Branches;
- (v) Handle matters requiring centralized decision-making;
- (vi) Represent Association externally on major matters;
- (vii) Support and resource Working Groups to succeed;
- (viii) Hold Working Groups accountable for results.

(b) The Committee's role is **not** to:

- (i) Make all decisions;
- (ii) Approve every action before it happens;
- (iii) Override Working Group expertise on operational matters;
- (iv) Micromanage day-to-day activities;
- (v) Create bureaucratic approval processes;
- (vi) Exercise hierarchical control over members or Working Groups.

(8) **Cultural Norms and Expectations:**

(a) The Association cultivates organizational culture where:

- (i) Everyone, regardless of position, is addressed respectfully as peer;
- (ii) Ideas are evaluated on merit, not source's position;
- (iii) "That won't work because..." is followed by reasoning, not appeals to authority;
- (iv) Disagreement is welcomed and handled constructively;
- (v) Mistakes are learning opportunities, not grounds for punishment;
- (vi) Success is celebrated collectively, not attributed to individual leaders;
- (vii) "How can we help you succeed?" replaces "You need our permission."

(b) Committee members should model these cultural norms:

- (i) Participating in Working Groups as contributors, not overseers;
- (ii) Asking questions and seeking to understand rather than directing;
- (iii) Supporting Working Group Coordinators' leadership;
- (iv) Sharing credit and acknowledging others' contributions;
- (v) Being transparent about Committee decision-making;
- (vi) Admitting uncertainty or lack of expertise gracefully.

(9) **Annual Organizational Culture Review:**

(a) During annual constitutional review under Rule 83, Committee must assess:

- (i) Whether flat organizational structure is being maintained;
- (ii) Signs of hierarchy creep or power concentration;
- (iii) Member feedback on organizational culture;
- (iv) Working Group Coordinator experiences with Committee oversight;
- (v) Whether NCAP is enabling or being subverted by hierarchical patterns;
- (vi) Recommendations for strengthening flat structure.

(b) If hierarchy creep identified:

- (i) Committee should acknowledge issue transparently;
- (ii) Develop action plan to address it;
- (iii) May include Committee restructuring, role redefinitions, or cultural interventions;
- (iv) Follow-up assessment in 6 months.

### Rule 23: Working Group Authority and NCAP Protocol

(1) **Delegated Authority:**

(a) The Committee delegates operational decision-making authority to Working Groups within scope defined in their charters, including:

- (i) Routine operational decisions within Working Group purpose;
- (ii) Public communications (Communications WG);
- (iii) Policy proposals and research (Policy WG);
- (iv) Campaign activities and candidate support (Campaigns WG);
- (v) Member engagement initiatives (Membership WG);
- (vi) Technology implementation and maintenance (Technology WG);
- (vii) Expenditures up to NCAP spending limits;
- (viii) Contracts within delegated scope and spending limits;
- (ix) Other functions as specified in charter.

(b) Working Groups may NOT exercise authority over:

- (i) Matters requiring affirmative Committee consent under Rule 45;
- (ii) Expenditures exceeding delegated spending limits;
- (iii) Constitutional amendments;
- (iv) Matters outside chartered scope;
- (v) Member discipline or termination;
- (vi) Establishment or dissolution of Working Groups;
- (vii) Committee member appointments or removals;
- (viii) Any matter Committee has specifically reserved.

(2) **NCAP Within Working Groups:**

(a) Working Groups exercise delegated authority through Negative Consent Approval Protocol (NCAP) as specified in Schedule B.

(b) NCAP voting participants:

- (i) All Working Group members (including Committee members in that Working Group);
- (ii) Any Committee member not in the Working Group may participate if they choose to exercise oversight;
- (iii) Committee members who do not vote are interpreted as delegating authority to the Working Group.

(c) This structure enables:

- (i) High-velocity autonomous Working Group operation;
- (ii) Committee oversight without micromanagement;
- (iii) Strategic Committee attention where most needed;
- (iv) Distributed governance with democratic accountability.

(3) **Authorization Channel Structure:**

(a) Each Working Group has a dedicated authorization channel in the governance infrastructure:

- `#auth-communications`
- `#auth-policy`
- `#auth-campaigns`
- `#auth-membership`
- `#auth-technology`
- `#auth-[name]` for additional Working Groups

(b) Authorization channels are:

- (i) Bot-only posting (NCAP submissions and status updates);
- (ii) Reaction-based voting only (no human text messages);
- (iii) Automatically enforced (human messages auto-deleted).

(c) Discussion occurs in:

- (i) Working Group discussion channels;
- (ii) Objection hearing threads (if objection raised);
- (iii) Committee meetings (if escalated).

(4) **NCAP Submission Process:**

(a) A Working Group member submits a proposal via governance infrastructure including:

- (i) Working Group name;
- (ii) Proposal description (content, action, purpose);
- (iii) Urgency level (standard 4h, urgent 2h, complex 6-8h);
- (iv) Estimated cost (if expenditure involved);
- (v) Root Axiom alignment rationale;
- (vi) Target authorization channel.

(b) Submission posts to appropriate Working Group authorization channel.

(c) All Working Group members AND all Committee members are notified.

(5) **NCAP Timer and Voting:**

(a) NCAP timers and voting operate according to the dynamic mechanisms defined in **Rule 49**.

(b) **Urgency Levels** (as defined in Rule 49(3)):

- **Standard:** Default urgency, calculated timer $T$;
- **Urgent:** Expedited timeline, timer scaled by factor $s$ (e.g., 0.5);
- **Complex:** Extended timeline, timer scaled by factor $s$ (e.g., 2.0).
- Specific initial timer values calculations are detailed in **Schedule B**.

(c) **Voting Actions:**

- **Approve:** Reduces timer (accelerates consensus) as per Rule 49;
- **Object:** Extends timer to Ceiling (Objection Gantry) as per Rule 49;
- **Silence:** Interpreted as consent.

(d) **Gantry Period:**

- As the timer approaches 0 (or Voting Floor), the proposal enters the **Gantry** state (Rule 49(5)).
- This provides a final window for objections or approvals.
- **Instant Resolution:** Additional votes during Gantry can trigger immediate Approval or Block (Rule 49(5)(c)-(d)).

(6) **Objection Handling:**

(a) Objections trigger an **Objection Gantry** (Rule 49(5)) effectively extending the timer to the Ceiling.

(b) This provides a final window for discussion and potential resolution:

- **Withdrawal:** Objector may withdraw objection, reducing timer;
- **Consensus:** Members may vote APPROVE to reduce timer and overcome objection (if sufficient participation);
- **Escalation:** If Objection Gantry expires without resolution, the proposal is **BLOCKED** and deemed escalated to Committee (or full vote).

(c) Rejected/Blocked proposals may be resubmitted with modifications.

(7) **Committee Oversight Rights:**

Committee members not in a Working Group retain full oversight rights but are NOT expected to review every NCAP submission. They should focus on:

- High-risk or high-impact proposals
- Root Axiom alignment concerns
- Proposals exceeding delegated authority
- Strategic matters requiring Committee input

(8) **Spending Limits:**

(a) Default NCAP spending limit per proposal: **60 Fee Units** (indexed automatically with Fee Unit value).

(b) Working Group charters may specify different per-proposal limits based on function.

(c) Charters may also specify cumulative spending limits (monthly or annual) monitored by Treasurer.

(d) Expenditures exceeding limits require affirmative Committee approval under Rule 45.

(e) All NCAP-authorized expenditures are:

- (i) Auto-logged by governance infrastructure;
- (ii) Tagged with Working Group, proposal ID, timestamp;
- (iii) Reviewed by Treasurer monthly;
- (iv) Reported to Committee quarterly;
- (v) Disclosed in annual financial reports by Working Group.

(9) **Emergency Actions:**

(a) In genuine emergencies (imminent harm, legal deadline, critical system failure, time-sensitive opportunity), a Coordinator may act without NCAP authorization.

(b) Emergency actions must be:

- (i) Documented immediately in writing;
- (ii) Reported to all Committee members within 24 hours with justification;
- (iii) Subject to Committee ratification at next meeting;
- (iv) Limited to actions strictly necessary for the emergency.

(c) Abuse of emergency authority may result in Coordinator removal or Working Group authority restrictions.

(10) **NCAP Transparency:**

(a) All NCAP activity is logged by governance infrastructure with complete audit trail.

(b) Public meta-channel displays summaries of authorized actions for member transparency.

(c) Members may review NCAP activity to assess Working Group productivity and Committee oversight patterns.

(d) NCAP protocol details in Schedule B may be updated by Committee vote without constitutional amendment.

### Rule 24: NCAP Scope and Communications Exemptions

(1) **Principle:**

Not all communications and activities require NCAP authorization. The Association distinguishes between public-facing communications (require NCAP), initial high-impact member communications (require NCAP), and routine internal communications (documented but exempt).

(2) **NCAP-Required Communications:**

(a) **Public communications:**

- Social media posts on official accounts
- Press releases and media statements
- Public blog posts or articles attributed to Association
- Media inquiry responses
- Public policy or political comments
- Advertising and promotional materials visible to non-members

(b) **Initial member announcements:**

- Major policy position announcements
- Strategic direction changes
- Candidate endorsements or campaign launches
- Significant organizational changes
- First communication about new initiative, survey, or program

(c) **High-impact member communications:**

- Constitutional amendment proposals
- AGM or SGM notices and agenda
- Disciplinary proceedings notifications
- Major fundraising appeals
- Urgent calls to action with time-sensitive deadlines

(3) **NCAP-Exempt Routine Internal Communications:**

(a) **Routine member services:**

- Membership renewal reminders
- Fee payment receipts and confirmations
- Password reset and account management
- Automated system notifications
- Event registration confirmations

(b) **Scheduled regular communications:**

- Monthly or weekly newsletters (if pre-approved template)
- Regular meeting reminders for recurring events
- Working Group updates to WG members
- Committee meeting minutes distribution
- Routine progress updates on approved initiatives

(c) **Follow-up and reminder communications:**

- Reminder emails for surveys (after initial NCAP-approved launch)
- Second/third reminders for event registrations
- Follow-ups to non-respondents for approved initiatives
- Deadline reminders for approved processes
- "Last chance" reminders for time-sensitive approved actions

(d) **Internal coordination:**

- Working Group meeting scheduling and agendas
- Committee member coordination emails
- Volunteer task assignments within approved projects
- Internal discussion threads
- Information sharing among members or Working Groups

(e) **Transactional and administrative:**

- Thank you emails for donations or volunteer contributions
- Welcome emails to new members
- Birthday or anniversary messages
- Access credentials and login information
- Technical support responses

(4) **Ambiguous Cases - Default to NCAP:**

When uncertain whether communication requires NCAP, the default is to submit for NCAP authorization. Examples of ambiguous cases that should use NCAP:

- First announcement of new survey (subsequent reminders exempt)
- Event with political/media implications (routine reminders exempt)
- Newsletter containing new policy position (regular format with standard updates exempt)
- Email that could be interpreted as speaking for Association publicly

(5) **Initial vs. Follow-Up Framework:**

(a) **Initial communications** require NCAP if they:

- Introduce new information, initiatives, or positions
- Request member action/feedback for first time
- Have potential for controversy
- Commit Association to course of action
- Carry reputational or political risk

(b) **Follow-up communications** are exempt if they:

- Relate to NCAP-approved initial communication
- Contain substantially same content with minor updates
- Serve as reminders or deadline notifications
- Don't introduce new positions or commitments
- Sent within reasonable timeframe (generally 90 days of initial authorization)

(c) **Example workflows:**

- **Survey:** Initial email requires NCAP, reminder emails exempt
- **Event:** Initial announcement requires NCAP, reminders exempt, major agenda changes require new NCAP
- **Newsletter:** First edition requires NCAP, subsequent editions exempt unless containing major new announcements

(6) **Documentation Requirements:**

Even NCAP-exempt communications must be documented under Rule 21A, including:

- Draft content and distribution list
- Purpose and exemption category
- Distribution timestamp
- Sender identity
- Link to original NCAP authorization (for follow-ups)
- Storage in communications archive

(7) **Working Group Authority:**

Working Groups may send NCAP-exempt communications without approval, subject to:

- Communications falling within documented exemption categories
- Documentation requirements
- Association brand guidelines and communication standards
- Root Axiom alignment
- Member communication preferences (frequency, topics)

(8) **Emergency Communications:**

In genuine emergencies (member safety, legal compliance, security breach), any Committee member or Coordinator may send immediate communications without NCAP, provided they report to Committee within 24 hours and document for record.

### Rule 25: Communication Infrastructure

(1) **Asynchronous-First Principle:**

The Association operates on asynchronous-first communication to:

- Eliminate unnecessary meetings and synchronous time requirements
- Enable participation across timezones and schedules
- Create transparent, searchable, permanent discussion records
- Reduce cognitive load and email overload
- Advance Root Axiom by eliminating communication inefficiency and participation barriers

(2) **Primary Community Space:**

(a) The Association maintains a primary community space (currently Discord) for member communication, coordination, and collaboration.

(b) The community space must be:

- Asynchronous-capable (members participate on own schedule)
- Organized by channels/topics for focused discussion
- Searchable and archivable
- Accessible via web, mobile, and desktop
- Capable of text, voice, and video communication
- Free or low-cost for members
- Compliant with data protection and privacy requirements
- Capable of role-based access control

(c) The Committee may migrate to alternative platforms if current platform becomes unsuitable, with 14 days notice to members and migration plan including data export and member training.

(3) **Membership Expectation to Join Community Space:**

(a) Joining the community space is a standard expectation for all members (Free and Paid).

(b) New members receive:

- Invitation link to community space
- Onboarding guide
- Appropriate role assignment
- Welcome message and introduction to active channels

(c) Members who don't join within 30 days receive reminder with support offer.

(d) Members who cannot or choose not to join:

- Remain full members with all constitutional rights
- Receive email summaries of major discussions
- May participate via alternative arrangements
- Are not penalized
- BUT acknowledge they may miss real-time discussions

(e) Technical barriers (disability, lack of device, internet access) must be addressed through alternative access methods, assistance, and multi-channel critical information distribution.

(4) **Public-First Community Space:**

(a) The community space is **publicly accessible and discoverable** to advance transparency, low-barrier entry, public visibility, and conversion pipeline.

(b) The community space must be:

- Listed publicly on platform's discovery features
- Searchable by relevant keywords
- Accessible via public invite link on website
- Joinable without requiring membership application first
- Welcoming to visitors and prospective members

(5) **Tiered Access Structure:**

(a) **Public access (anyone who joins):**

- Welcome and rules, about Association, how to join
- Public announcements, events, media releases
- General discussion, policy discussion, introductions
- Volunteer opportunities, community projects
- Feedback and suggestions

(b) **Member-only access (Free or Paid):**

- Membership administration
- Member-only announcements
- Working Group coordination channels
- NCAP authorization channels
- Draft policy documents
- Campaign strategy and planning
- Governance infrastructure logs

(c) **Paid member-only access:**

- Committee election forums
- Budget discussions and financial planning
- Constitutional amendment debates
- Candidate preselection discussions
- Strategic planning sessions
- Detailed financial reports

(d) **Committee and restricted access:**

- Committee confidential deliberations
- Legal matters
- Personnel matters
- Disciplinary proceedings
- Sensitive negotiations

(6) **Role Assignment and Verification:**

(a) New visitors receive @Visitor role (public channels only).

(b) When membership approved under Rule 7:

- Member notifies community space
- Technology WG verifies membership status
- @Visitor role removed
- @Free Member or @Paid Member role assigned
- Access to member-only channels granted

(c) Members who allow membership to lapse are automatically downgraded to @Visitor after grace period, losing member-only access but retaining public access.

(7) **Conversion Pipeline:**

The public community space serves as conversion funnel:

- **Discovery:** Person finds Association, joins as visitor
- **Engagement:** Participates in public discussions, asks questions
- **Interest:** Expresses interest in joining, reviews materials
- **Application:** Submits membership application
- **Onboarding:** Gains member role, accesses member channels, begins full participation

The Association should optimize pipeline through active welcoming public channels, prompt responses, highlighting benefits, reducing application friction, and tracking conversion metrics.

(8) **Email Usage Policy:**

(a) Email should be used for:

- Important announcements requiring immediate attention
- AGM/SGM formal notices (constitutional requirement)
- Time-sensitive action items
- Critical reminders
- Weekly/monthly digest of community space activity
- Links to major discussions
- External communications (media, government, vendors)

(b) Email should NOT be used for:

- Working Group coordination
- Policy development conversations
- Committee deliberation (except formal motions)
- Lengthy discussions (move to community space)
- File sharing and collaboration
- Social conversation

(c) When email thread becomes discussion (2-3+ replies), move to community space with summary and link.

(9) **Synchronous Meetings:**

(a) Synchronous meetings (voice/video) are permitted but should be:

- Scheduled in advance with agenda
- Time-limited (default 60 minutes max)
- Recorded when feasible (with consent)
- Summarized in writing afterward
- Supplemented by asynchronous follow-up

(b) Appropriate for:

- Complex discussions requiring real-time dialogue
- Brainstorming and creative collaboration
- Conflict resolution
- Urgent crisis response
- Social connection
- Committee meetings as required

(c) Should NOT be:

- Primary mode of coordination
- Required for routine decisions
- Scheduled without timezone consideration
- Substitute for documentation
- Exclusive forums for important decisions

(d) After meetings:

- Key decisions posted to community space
- Minutes/summary shared within 48 hours
- Recordings shared with access controls
- Asynchronous input opportunity for non-attendees
- Follow-up discussion continues in community space

(10) **Working Group Coordination Requirements:**

(a) Working Groups must use community space as primary coordination platform.

(b) Activities that must occur in community space:

- Discussion of proposals before NCAP
- Project coordination and task assignments
- Sharing drafts, research, work products
- Meeting scheduling and agendas
- Post-meeting follow-up and action tracking
- Knowledge sharing and documentation

(c) Supplementary tools allowed (video calls, collaborative documents, project management software) but must link back to community space with notifications, links, and documented outcomes.

(11) **Documentation and Transparency:**

(a) Community space discussions constitute informal documentation under Rule 21A.

(b) Formal documentation still required for final decisions, meeting minutes, policy drafts, financial transactions, and member actions.

(c) Community space discussions should be searchable, archived permanently, referenced in formal documentation, and summarized when complex.

(d) Confidential matters in restricted channels must be marked, have documented access restrictions, and be summarized publicly with redactions when appropriate.

(12) **Root Axiom Alignment:**

The asynchronous-first, community-space-primary model advances Root Axiom by:

**Eliminating inefficiency:**

- Reduces unnecessary synchronous meetings
- Enables focused work without interruptions
- Creates searchable, reusable knowledge base
- Prevents duplicate discussions across email threads
- Allows timezone-flexible participation

**Eliminating unnecessary suffering:**

- Reduces stress from scheduling conflicts and meeting overload
- Accommodates diverse circumstances (caregiving, work, disabilities)
- Prevents information exclusion
- Reduces email overload anxiety
- Enables participation without travel

**Advancing Value Gates:**

- **Transparency:** All discussions searchable and visible
- **Efficiency:** Async coordination maximizes productivity
- **Adaptability:** Platform can change if better options emerge
- **Fairness:** Equal information access for all members
- **Evidence:** Documented discussions create audit trail

## PART 5: STATE BRANCHES

### Rule 28: State Branch Framework

(1) **National Association with State Branches:**

(a) Source Party Australia Incorporated is a national association that may establish State Branches in any Australian state or territory to coordinate regional activities and electoral campaigns.

(b) State Branches are not separate legal entities. All assets, liabilities, and legal responsibilities remain with the national Association.

(c) State Branches operate under this Constitution and the authority of the National Committee, with delegated autonomy for state-specific matters.

(2) **Purposes of State Branches:**

State Branches serve to:

- (i) Build regional membership and community connections;
- (ii) Coordinate state election campaigns and candidate support (once registered with State Electoral Commission);
- (iii) Develop state-specific policy positions consistent with national policy framework and Root Axiom;
- (iv) Engage with state and local media;
- (v) Organize local events, activities, and community organizing;
- (vi) Adapt national messaging to regional contexts and concerns;
- (vii) Recruit and train local volunteers and potential candidates;
- (viii) Represent the Association in state political forums and coalitions;
- (ix) Work toward state electoral registration when membership thresholds are reached.

(3) **Relationship to National Committee:**

(a) State Branches are subordinate to the National Committee.

(b) National Committee retains authority over:

- (i) Constitutional interpretation and amendments;
- (ii) National policy positions and platform;
- (iii) Root Axiom and Value Gates framework;
- (iv) Financial management and budgets (including State Branch allocations);
- (v) Candidate endorsements for federal elections;
- (vi) National communications strategy and branding;
- (vii) Establishment and dissolution of State Branches;
- (viii) Approval of state electoral registration applications;
- (ix) Resolution of disputes between State Branches or between Branch and National;
- (x) Any matter affecting the Association as a whole.

(c) State Branches have delegated authority over:

- (i) State-specific policy positions (must be consistent with national policy and Root Axiom);
- (ii) State election candidate selection and endorsement (once registered with State Electoral Commission);
- (iii) State campaign strategy and tactics (within approved budgets and national framework);
- (iv) Local events, community engagement, and organizing activities;
- (v) State media relations (coordinated with Communications Working Group);
- (vi) Allocation of State Branch budget within approved limits;
- (vii) Internal State Branch governance and procedures;
- (viii) Other operational matters as delegated by National Committee.

(4) **At-Large Members:**

(a) Members residing in states without established State Branches are "at-large members."

(b) At-large members are coordinated directly by National Committee and national Working Groups.

(c) At-large members may:

- (i) Participate in all national Working Groups and activities;
- (ii) Propose establishment of State Branch in their state under Rule 29;
- (iii) Form informal regional organizing groups;
- (iv) Attend events and activities in other states' branches;
- (v) Stand as candidates in federal elections;
- (vi) Participate fully in Association governance and decision-making.

### Rule 29: Establishment of State Branches

(1) **Activation Criteria:**

(a) A State Branch may be established when:

- (i) At least **50 Paid Members** reside in that state or territory; AND
- (ii) The National Committee approves establishment by majority vote.

(b) Paid Members are counted based on their primary residential address in the membership register.

(c) The 50 Paid Member threshold may be waived by National Committee vote if:

- (i) Significant organizing activity exists in the state;
- (ii) State election opportunity is imminent and strategic;
- (iii) Strong core group of committed organizers exists (minimum 10 Paid Members);
- (iv) Committee determines provisional branch status is appropriate;
- (v) Clear pathway to reaching 50 Paid Members within 12 months.

(2) **Proposal and Establishment Process:**

(a) When activation criteria are met, any member residing in that state may propose State Branch establishment by written submission to the National Committee via the Secretary.

(b) The proposal must include:

- (i) Evidence of 50+ Paid Members in state (or justification for waiver);
- (ii) Names and consent of at least 3 Paid Members willing to serve on initial State Branch Committee;
- (iii) Proposed initial priorities and activities (6-12 month plan);
- (iv) Assessment of state electoral landscape and opportunities;
- (v) Resource requirements and budget request;
- (vi) Timeline for working toward state electoral registration (if applicable);
- (vii) Demonstrated alignment with Root Axiom and Association values.

(c) National Committee considers the proposal and may:

- (i) Approve establishment immediately by majority vote;
- (ii) Request additional information, planning, or consultation;
- (iii) Approve provisional status with conditions and review date;
- (iv) Defer until membership threshold or organizing capacity increases;
- (v) Decline if not strategically appropriate with written reasons.

(d) Upon approval, the National Committee must:

- (i) Appoint interim State Branch Committee (3-7 members from proposed group);
- (ii) Set date for first State Branch General Meeting (within 90 days);
- (iii) Allocate initial budget for Branch establishment and operations;
- (iv) Coordinate creation of state-specific channels in community space with Technology Working Group;
- (v) Announce State Branch establishment to full membership;
- (vi) Provide onboarding support and resources to interim Committee.

(3) **No Automatic Establishment:**

(a) No State Branch is automatically established upon adoption of this Constitution.

(b) State Branches are established organically as membership and organizing capacity develop in each jurisdiction.

(c) The first State Branch will be established when criteria in subrule (1) are met and proposal approved under subrule (2).

(d) Given the Association's founding base, organizing efforts, and electoral opportunities, Victoria is likely to be the first state to meet establishment criteria, but this is not constitutionally guaranteed or mandated.

(4) **Branch Creation Distinguished from Electoral Registration:**

(a) Establishing a State Branch is an **internal organizational decision** for coordination, community building, and campaign preparation.

(b) Registering with a State Electoral Commission is a **separate legal process** governed by state electoral law with different membership thresholds (see Rule 30).

(c) A State Branch may exist and operate productively without being registered with the State Electoral Commission, including:

- (i) Building membership toward electoral registration threshold;
- (ii) Developing policy positions and campaign infrastructure;
- (iii) Organizing community events and issue advocacy;
- (iv) Training volunteers and potential candidates;
- (v) Supporting federal campaigns and candidates in that state;
- (vi) Building public profile and brand recognition.

(d) Until registered with the State Electoral Commission, a State Branch:

- (i) Cannot endorse candidates for state elections under the Association's name;
- (ii) Cannot appear on state election ballot papers as a party;
- (iii) Cannot receive state electoral funding or other benefits of registration;
- (iv) May support individuals running as independents or under other arrangements;
- (v) May engage in issue advocacy, community organizing, and political education;
- (vi) Should actively work toward achieving electoral registration if state elections are a strategic priority.

### Rule 30: State Electoral Registration

(1) **Electoral Registration Threshold:**

(a) The Association should pursue registration with a State Electoral Commission when total membership (Free Members + Paid Members) in that state reaches **150% of the minimum legal requirement** for party registration in that jurisdiction.

(b) State-specific thresholds as of Constitution adoption (subject to change if electoral laws amended):

| State/Territory                              | Legal Minimum | Target Threshold (150%) |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------- | ----------------------- |
| Victoria (VEC)                               | 500 members   | 750 members             |
| New South Wales (NSWEC)                      | 750 members   | 1,125 members           |
| Queensland (ECQ)                             | 500 members   | 750 members             |
| South Australia (ECSA)                       | 200 members   | 300 members             |
| Western Australia (WAEC)                     | 500 members   | 750 members             |
| Tasmania (TEC)                               | 100 members   | 150 members             |
| Northern Territory (NTEC)                    | 200 members   | 300 members             |
| Australian Capital Territory (Elections ACT) | 100 members   | 150 members             |

(c) "Members" for electoral registration purposes includes **both Free Members and Paid Members** who:

- (i) Reside in the state or territory;
- (ii) Are eligible to enroll to vote in that jurisdiction's elections;
- (iii) Provide explicit written consent to being included in party registration application;
- (iv) Are verified as real, unique individuals (not duplicates or fraudulent entries);
- (v) Have current contact details in the membership register.

(d) The National Committee must review and update target thresholds within 90 days of any change to State Electoral Commission registration requirements or processes.

(2) **Rationale for 150% Threshold:**

(a) The 150% buffer ensures:

- (i) Registration application succeeds on first attempt (avoiding rejection, delay, and wasted resources);
- (ii) Natural membership attrition or lapses don't drop membership below legal minimum;
- (iii) Buffer for members who decline consent or are found ineligible during verification;
- (iv) Confidence in member verification processes and data quality;
- (v) Organizational capacity to contest multiple electorates effectively;
- (vi) Strong foundation for sustained electoral presence and compliance obligations.

(b) This threshold advances the Root Axiom by:

- (i) Eliminating inefficiency of failed applications and resubmissions;
- (ii) Reducing suffering caused by dashed electoral hopes and wasted volunteer effort;
- (iii) Demonstrating evidence-based planning (learning from other parties' registration experiences).

(c) The National Committee may adjust the threshold (up or down) by two-thirds majority vote if:

- (i) Experience demonstrates a different buffer is more appropriate;
- (ii) Urgent electoral opportunity justifies accepting higher risk;
- (iii) State-specific factors (strict vs. lenient Electoral Commission, verification requirements, etc.) affect the calculation;
- (iv) Legal or procedural changes alter the risk profile.

(3) **Registration Readiness Assessment:**

(a) When a State Branch approaches the target threshold (within 10% or 100 members, whichever is less), the State Branch Committee must:

- (i) Commission independent member verification audit;
- (ii) Assess organizational capacity for registered party obligations;
- (iii) Develop electoral registration strategy and timeline;
- (iv) Identify potential registered officer, party agent, and compliance personnel;
- (v) Prepare budget for registration application and compliance costs;
- (vi) Consult with legal advisors on state electoral law requirements.

(b) When the target threshold is reached, State Branch Committee must:

- (i) Present comprehensive registration readiness report to National Committee;
- (ii) Recommend whether to proceed with registration application;
- (iii) Provide member verification audit results;
- (iv) Demonstrate compliance systems and capacity;
- (v) Present electoral strategy and candidate pipeline;
- (vi) Request National Committee approval and funding.

(4) **National Committee Registration Decision:**

(a) National Committee considers State Branch recommendation and assesses:

- (i) Member verification audit quality and results (sufficient eligible, consenting members);
- (ii) Organizational capacity to fulfill registered party obligations (compliance, reporting, accountability);
- (iii) Financial capacity to support registration costs and ongoing compliance;
- (iv) Strategic electoral opportunities and timing;
- (v) Legal and compliance readiness (systems, personnel, expertise);
- (vi) Risk assessment (probability of success, consequences of failure);
- (vii) Alignment with Association strategic priorities and Root Axiom.

(b) National Committee may:

- (i) Approve proceeding with electoral registration application by two-thirds majority vote;
- (ii) Defer until additional criteria met or risks mitigated;
- (iii) Request further preparation, verification, or planning;
- (iv) Provide conditional approval with specific requirements to be met before application;
- (v) Decline if not strategically appropriate, with written reasons and pathway to reconsideration.

(c) Electoral registration approval requires **two-thirds majority vote** of the National Committee due to:

- (i) Significant financial and compliance obligations;
- (ii) Reputational risk if registration fails or obligations not met;
- (iii) Long-term strategic commitment;
- (iv) Need for strong Committee consensus and support.

(5) **Pre-Registration Requirements:**

Before submitting electoral registration application, the State Branch must demonstrate:

(a) **Organizational Maturity:**

- (i) Established State Branch Committee operating for at least **6 months**;
- (ii) Regular Branch meetings and documented activities;
- (iii) Active membership engagement and participation;
- (iv) Effective coordination with National Committee and Working Groups.

(b) **Member Verification:**

- (i) Independent audit confirming member eligibility for registration;
- (ii) Explicit written consent from all members to be included in application;
- (iii) Verification of residential addresses and eligibility to vote in state;
- (iv) Removal of duplicates, fraudulent entries, and ineligible members;
- (v) Membership list accuracy and currency confirmed.

(c) **Documentation:**

- (i) Party constitution (this Constitution or state-specific adaptation if legally required);
- (ii) Registered officer and party agent identified and consenting;
- (iii) Party name, abbreviation, logo, and colors finalized and approved;
- (iv) Any additional documentation required by state electoral law.

(d) **Compliance Infrastructure:**

- (i) Financial management systems for electoral funding and disclosure;
- (ii) Personnel or contractors responsible for compliance and reporting;
- (iii) Processes for candidate nomination, endorsement, and accountability;
- (iv) Understanding of and capacity to meet ongoing registration obligations;
- (v) Legal and accounting support available as needed.

(e) **Electoral Readiness:**

- (i) Campaign infrastructure and volunteer base developed;
- (ii) Candidate recruitment and training pipeline established;
- (iii) Policy positions and messaging framework prepared;
- (iv) Media strategy and communications capacity;
- (v) Realistic assessment of electorates to contest and electoral goals.

(f) **Financial Commitment:**

- (i) National Committee approval of registration application budget;
- (ii) Funding secured for application fees and legal costs;
- (iii) Ongoing budget for compliance, reporting, and electoral activities;
- (iv) Contingency funds for unexpected costs or issues.

(6) **Registration Application Process:**

(a) Upon National Committee approval, the State Branch Coordinator (or designated registered officer) submits registration application to the State Electoral Commission including:

- (i) Completed application forms as required by state electoral law;
- (ii) Membership list with required member details and declarations;
- (iii) Party constitution (this Constitution or adapted version if required);
- (iv) Registered officer and party agent details, consents, and statutory declarations;
- (v) Party name, abbreviation, logo, and any other branding elements;
- (vi) Application fee (if required);
- (vii) Any supplementary documentation required by Electoral Commission.

(b) During the application process:

- (i) National Committee and State Branch Committee monitor progress closely;
- (ii) Respond promptly and comprehensively to any Electoral Commission requests or queries;
- (iii) Address deficiencies or objections with urgency and professionalism;
- (iv) Engage legal counsel if complex issues arise;
- (v) Keep membership informed of status via regular updates;
- (vi) Prepare for public objection period if applicable (respond to any objections filed).

(c) If application is **successful**:

- (i) Announce and celebrate achievement with membership and publicly;
- (ii) Activate full electoral campaign infrastructure;
- (iii) Commence intensive candidate recruitment and preselection processes;
- (iv) Establish compliance reporting systems and calendars;
- (v) Update all Association records, documentation, and communications;
- (vi) Fulfill any post-registration obligations to Electoral Commission;
- (vii) Begin electoral campaigning in accordance with state electoral law.

(d) If application is **rejected or withdrawn**:

- (i) Conduct thorough analysis of reasons for rejection;
- (ii) Identify and address all deficiencies systematically;
- (iii) Rebuild membership if necessary;
- (iv) Strengthen compliance and verification processes;
- (v) Develop remediation plan with timeline;
- (vi) Communicate transparently with membership about setback and path forward;
- (vii) Reapply when ready, incorporating lessons learned.

(7) **Maintaining Electoral Registration:**

(a) Once registered, the State Branch must maintain registration by:

- (i) Keeping membership above legal minimum at all times (target: **125% of legal minimum** as ongoing buffer);
- (ii) Submitting all required annual returns, financial disclosures, and updates to Electoral Commission on time;
- (iii) Complying with all state electoral funding, donation, and disclosure laws;
- (iv) Maintaining current registered officer and party agent appointments (notify Electoral Commission of changes within required timeframe);
- (v) Updating Electoral Commission promptly of any material changes (party name, constitution amendments, contact details, etc.);
- (vi) Conducting regular member verification audits (at least annually);
- (vii) Fulfilling any other ongoing obligations under state electoral law.

(b) If membership falls below legal minimum:

- (i) State Branch must immediately notify National Committee;
- (ii) Launch emergency membership recruitment drive;
- (iii) National Committee must allocate resources, support, and attention to rebuild membership urgently;
- (iv) State Branch reports weekly to National Committee on progress;
- (v) If membership remains below minimum for period specified in electoral law, Electoral Commission may cancel registration;
- (vi) Association must notify Electoral Commission of membership status and corrective actions as required by law.

(c) Loss of state electoral registration is a serious matter requiring:

- (i) Immediate National Committee investigation of root causes (membership decline, administrative failure, strategic misalignment);
- (ii) Assessment of State Branch viability and leadership effectiveness;
- (iii) Consideration of State Branch suspension or dissolution under Rule 33 if systematic failures or negligence identified;
- (iv) Transparent communication to membership about what occurred and accountability measures;
- (v) Development of comprehensive plan to prevent similar failures in other jurisdictions.

(8) **Multiple State Registrations:**

(a) The Association may be registered as a political party with multiple State Electoral Commissions simultaneously.

(b) Each state registration is independent legally and does not affect registration status in other jurisdictions.

(c) Federal electoral registration (if pursued) is separate from all state registrations and has different requirements under Commonwealth Electoral Act 1918.

(d) Pursuing multiple registrations requires National Committee to:

- (i) Prioritize states based on electoral opportunities, membership strength, and strategic value;
- (ii) Ensure sufficient financial and organizational capacity to support compliance in all registered jurisdictions;
- (iii) Maintain consistent branding and messaging across jurisdictions;
- (iv) Coordinate electoral activities to avoid resource conflicts;
- (v) Learn from each registration experience to improve subsequent applications.

(9) **Party Name and Branding Consistency:**

(a) The Association should use consistent party names and branding across all jurisdictions where possible to:

- (i) Build unified national brand recognition;
- (ii) Reduce voter confusion;
- (iii) Leverage campaign resources across jurisdictions;
- (iv) Demonstrate coherent national presence.

(b) **Preferred party name and variations:**

- (i) **Primary name:** "Source Party Australia" or "Source Party"
- (ii) **State variations (if required by state law):** "Source Party Victoria", "Source Party NSW", "Source Party Queensland", etc.
- (iii) **Abbreviations:** "Source" or "SRC" (if available in jurisdiction)
- (iv) **Logo and colors:** Consistent across all jurisdictions

(c) If preferred name is unavailable in a jurisdiction (already registered by another party or prohibited for other reasons), National Committee must:

- (i) Investigate possibility of negotiating with existing party for name release or variation;
- (ii) Consider alternative names that maintain brand coherence ("Source Australia", "The Source Party", "Australian Source Party", etc.);
- (iii) Assess strategic impact of name variation on brand recognition and electoral viability;
- (iv) Consult with State Branch Committee and Communications Working Group;
- (v) Approve alternative name by **two-thirds majority vote**;
- (vi) Document reasoning and strategic considerations.

(d) Regardless of any name variations required by law, the Association must maintain:

- (i) Consistent visual branding (logo, colors, design language);
- (ii) Consistent messaging and policy framework;
- (iii) Clear communication to voters about connection between differently-named state registrations;
- (iv) Unified digital presence (website, social media) explaining any name variations.

(10) **Electoral Registration Reporting:**

(a) State Branch Coordinator (or designated compliance officer) must report to National Committee:

- (i) **Quarterly:** Membership progress toward registration threshold, verification audit updates, organizing activities;
- (ii) **Monthly (once registered):** Compliance status, membership count, upcoming deadlines, issues or risks;
- (iii) **Immediately:** Any Electoral Commission correspondence, inquiries, or compliance concerns;
- (iv) **Annually:** Comprehensive review of registration maintenance, lessons learned, recommendations.

(b) National Committee must report to Association AGM on electoral registration status including:

- (i) Current state registrations and membership levels in each;
- (ii) States approaching registration thresholds and readiness assessments;
- (iii) Registration applications in progress or planned;
- (iv) Compliance status across all registrations and any issues or risks;
- (v) Strategic priorities for new registrations in coming year;
- (vi) Costs and benefits of state registrations (electoral performance, compliance burden, resource allocation).

### Rule 31: State Branch Governance

(1) **State Branch Committee:**

(a) Each State Branch is governed by a State Branch Committee of **3-7 members** elected by State Branch members.

(b) State Branch Committee composition:

- (i) **Branch Coordinator:** Primary liaison to National Committee, leads Branch activities, chairs Branch meetings;
- (ii) **Branch Treasurer:** Manages Branch budget, tracks expenditures, ensures compliance with financial procedures (may be combined with Coordinator role in small branches);
- (iii) **Committee Members:** 1-5 additional members as determined by Branch size and needs.

(c) State Branch Committee members must:

- (i) Be Paid Members of the Association;
- (ii) Reside primarily in that state or territory;
- (iii) Be elected at State Branch General Meetings;
- (iv) Serve one-year terms commencing at conclusion of Branch General Meeting;
- (v) Be eligible for re-election without limit.

(d) State Branch Committee members may concurrently serve on National Committee, other State Branch Committees (if they relocate), or as Working Group Coordinators subject to workload sustainability principles under Rule 22(8).

(e) Branch Committee role definitions must be documented under Rule 21B and published to Branch members.

(2) **State Branch General Meetings:**

(a) Each State Branch must hold at least one General Meeting per year (**Branch AGM**).

(b) Branch AGM must occur:

- (i) Within **90 days after** the Association's AGM; OR
- (ii) At such other time as Branch Committee determines in consultation with National Committee; BUT
- (iii) No more than **15 months** after the previous Branch AGM.

(c) Branch AGM agenda must include:

- (i) Branch Coordinator report on activities, achievements, and challenges during the year;
- (ii) Branch Treasurer financial report on income, expenditures, and budget status;
- (iii) Election of Branch Committee for coming year;
- (iv) Any constitutional or policy matters referred by National Committee for Branch consultation;
- (v) Branch strategic priorities and goals for coming year;
- (vi) Other business as determined by Branch Committee or proposed by members.

(d) Notice of Branch AGM must be:

- (i) Given to all Branch members (those residing in that state) at least **14 days in advance**;
- (ii) Distributed via community space announcement and email;
- (iii) Including agenda, Coordinator and Treasurer reports, and any motions to be considered;
- (iv) Including details of how to participate (in-person location, online link, proxy voting if permitted).

(e) Quorum for Branch General Meetings: **10% of Paid Members** residing in that state, or **10 Paid Members**, whichever is **lower**.

(f) If quorum is not met within 30 minutes of scheduled start time:

- (i) Meeting is adjourned to same time 7 days later;
- (ii) If quorum not met at adjourned meeting, those present constitute quorum;
- (iii) Members must be notified of adjournment and new date.

(g) **Special Branch General Meetings** may be called by:

- (i) Branch Committee by majority vote;
- (ii) National Committee directive;
- (iii) Written petition of **20% of Branch Paid Members** or **20 Branch Paid Members**, whichever is **lower**.

(h) Special Branch General Meetings require same notice period and quorum as Branch AGM.

(3) **State Branch Membership:**

(a) All Association members are automatically members of the State Branch in the state where they reside (if a Branch exists in that state).

(b) "Residing in a state" is determined by the member's primary residential address in the membership register.

(c) Members may update their address with the Secretary to change Branch affiliation (e.g., if they move to a different state).

(d) Members residing in states without established Branches are at-large members under Rule 28(4).

(e) **Paid Members** in a State Branch may:

- (i) Vote at State Branch General Meetings;
- (ii) Nominate for State Branch Committee;
- (iii) Vote in State Branch candidate preselection processes;
- (iv) Move and second motions at Branch meetings;
- (v) Access detailed Branch financial reports;
- (vi) Participate in all Branch governance decisions.

(f) **Free Members** in a State Branch may:

- (i) Attend State Branch meetings and events;
- (ii) Speak at meetings (subject to meeting procedures);
- (iii) Participate in Branch discussions and activities;
- (iv) Volunteer for Branch campaigns and projects;
- (v) Access Branch channels in community space;
- (vi) Contribute to Branch organizing and community building.

(g) Free Members do **not** have:

- (i) Voting rights at Branch General Meetings;
- (ii) Eligibility to serve on Branch Committee;
- (iii) Voting rights in candidate preselections;
- (iv) Ability to move or second motions.

(4) **State Branch Decision-Making:**

(a) State Branch Committees may use **NCAP** for operational decisions within their delegated authority, following the same protocol as Working Groups under Part 4.

(b) Each State Branch has dedicated authorization channel(s) in governance infrastructure:

- `#auth-branch-vic` (Victoria, when established)
- `#auth-branch-nsw` (New South Wales, when established)
- `#auth-branch-qld` (Queensland, when established)
- `#auth-branch-[state abbreviation]` (others as established)

(c) **NCAP voting participants** for State Branch authorizations:

- (i) All State Branch Committee members;
- (ii) Any National Committee member may participate to exercise oversight (not required to be Branch Committee member);
- (iii) National Committee members who do not vote are interpreted as delegating authority to the Branch Committee.

(d) **NCAP spending limits** for State Branches:

- (i) Default per-proposal limit: **60 Fee Units** (same as Working Groups);
- (ii) May be adjusted by National Committee based on Branch budget allocation and activity level;
- (iii) Cumulative monthly and annual spending limits set by Branch budget approved at Association AGM;
- (iv) Treasurer monitors cumulative spending and alerts Branch Committee when approaching limits.

(e) **Matters requiring National Committee approval** (not delegated to NCAP):

- (i) Expenditures exceeding per-proposal NCAP limits;
- (ii) State-specific policy positions that conflict with, contradict, or significantly extend national policy;
- (iii) Major strategic decisions with reputational or financial risk to Association as a whole;
- (iv) Legal commitments, contracts, or agreements binding the Association;
- (v) Candidate endorsements for state elections (requires National Committee approval even after state electoral registration);
- (vi) Any matter National Committee has specifically reserved or that falls outside Branch charter.

(f) **Escalation process** when Branch Committee is uncertain:

- (i) Branch Coordinator submits written question or proposal to National Committee via Secretary;
- (ii) National Committee discusses at next meeting or via NCAP if urgent;
- (iii) National Committee provides guidance or decision within 14 days;
- (iv) Decision is binding on Branch and documented.

(5) **Coordination with National Committee and Working Groups:**

(a) State Branch Coordinators must:

- (i) Submit **monthly written reports** to National Committee (similar to Working Group reporting under Rule 26);
- (ii) Attend National Committee meetings when invited or when agenda includes Branch-relevant matters;
- (iii) Coordinate major announcements, media appearances, and public communications with Communications Working Group;
- (iv) Consult Policy Working Group when developing state-specific policy positions;
- (v) Collaborate with Campaigns Working Group on electoral strategy and candidate support;
- (vi) Work with Membership Working Group on recruitment and engagement;
- (vii) Seek National Committee guidance proactively on ambiguous, risky, or strategic matters;
- (viii) Report significant issues, opportunities, risks, or crises immediately.

(b) National Committee may:

- (i) Provide guidance, direction, and strategic input to State Branches;
- (ii) Request additional reports, information, or briefings on specific matters;
- (iii) Review and approve (or modify) State Branch budgets and financial plans;
- (iv) Attend or observe State Branch meetings;
- (v) Review NCAP authorization patterns and spending;
- (vi) Intervene if Branch activities are contrary to Root Axiom, Association purposes, or constitutional requirements;
- (vii) Modify, expand, or restrict delegated authorities based on Branch performance and capacity;
- (viii) Resolve disputes between Branches, or between Branch and National Committee or Working Groups;
- (ix) Provide resources, support, training, and capacity building;
- (x) Suspend or dissolve State Branches under Rule 33.

(c) State Branches should operate with **autonomy within delegation** - National Committee oversight should be strategic and supportive rather than micromanaging, preserving Branch operational velocity while ensuring accountability and alignment.

### Rule 32: State Branch Electoral Activities

(1) **Candidate Selection Authority:**

(a) State Branches have delegated authority to select and recommend candidates for:

- (i) State lower house elections (Legislative Assembly, House of Assembly, etc.);
- (ii) State upper house elections (Legislative Council, etc.);
- (iii) Local government elections in that state or territory.

(b) **National Committee retains final approval authority** over all candidate endorsements, even for state and local elections, exercised by:

- (i) NCAP authorization (for uncontroversial endorsements meeting standard criteria);
- (ii) Affirmative Committee vote (for contested preselections, controversial candidates, or high-profile races).

(c) National Committee retains exclusive authority over candidate endorsements for:

- (i) Federal House of Representatives;
- (ii) Federal Senate;
- (iii) Any election where Association is registered as federal party.

(d) National Committee may delegate federal candidate selection to State Branches on a case-by-case basis by explicit Committee resolution.

(2) **Candidate Preselection Process:**

(a) State Branch Committee determines the specific preselection process for state and local candidates, which must include at minimum:

- (i) **Open call for nominations:** Publicized to all Paid Members in the electorate or state with reasonable notice period (minimum 14 days);
- (ii) **Eligibility vetting:** Verification that nominees meet legal requirements, are Paid Members, and pass basic background checks;
- (iii) **Alignment assessment:** Evaluation of nominee's demonstrated alignment with Root Axiom, Value Gates, and Association policy positions;
- (iv) **Presentation opportunity:** Nominees present to Branch members (in-person, online, or written statement) explaining their motivations, qualifications, and campaign plans;
- (v) **Democratic vote:** Paid Members in the state (or electorate if multi-candidate preselection) vote on nominees;
- (vi) **Branch Committee recommendation:** Based on vote results and assessment, Branch Committee recommends candidate(s) to National Committee;
- (vii) **National Committee approval:** National Committee considers recommendation and approves (or declines) endorsement.

(b) Preselection should prioritize candidates who demonstrate:

- (i) Deep alignment with Root Axiom and commitment to eliminating inefficiency and unnecessary suffering;
- (ii) Understanding of and fluency in Association policy framework and Value Gates;
- (iii) Competence and capacity to campaign effectively (communication skills, work ethic, resilience);
- (iv) Local connections, knowledge of electorate, and community credibility;
- (v) Diversity of backgrounds, perspectives, and lived experiences (to build representative, effective team);
- (vi) Electability and viability (realistic assessment of ability to win or perform strongly);
- (vii) Integrity and character (background checks, public record, reputation).

(c) Preselection disputes or challenges are resolved by:

- (i) Branch Committee review and decision (first instance);
- (ii) Appeal to National Committee (final decision, binding on all parties).

(d) Preselection processes must be documented under Rule 21A and transparent to participating members.

(3) **Candidate Requirements and Vetting:**

(a) All candidates endorsed by the Association must:

- (i) Be Paid Members in good standing;
- (ii) Reside in or have strong connection to the electorate they seek to represent;
- (iii) Meet all legal eligibility requirements for the office;
- (iv) Pass background checks (criminal history, financial probity, public statements);
- (v) Sign candidate agreement committing to Association policy positions, campaign coordination, and post-election accountability;
- (vi) Participate in candidate training and preparation provided by Association;
- (vii) Disclose any potential conflicts of interest, controversial history, or opposition research vulnerabilities.

(b) State Branch Committee (with National Committee support) must conduct due diligence on all candidates before endorsement including:

- (i) Criminal background check;
- (ii) Financial probity check (bankruptcy, tax issues, significant debts);
- (iii) Social media and public statements review;
- (iv) Professional and personal reference checks;
- (v) Assessment of potential vulnerabilities or opposition research risks;
- (vi) Interview or extended conversation to assess character and commitment.

(c) If vetting reveals disqualifying issues, the candidate may be declined endorsement with reasons provided confidentially.

(4) **Campaign Coordination:**

(a) State Branches coordinate state and local election campaigns in close cooperation with:

- (i) **Campaigns Working Group:** National campaign strategy, resources, training, and best practices;
- (ii) **Communications Working Group:** Messaging, media strategy, branding, and public communications;
- (iii) **Policy Working Group:** Policy platform, position development, and research support;
- (iv) **Technology Working Group:** Digital tools, campaign infrastructure, data management;
- (v) **Other State Branches:** Sharing learnings, resources, volunteers, and mutual support;
- (vi) **National Committee:** Oversight, approval of major decisions, strategic guidance, and resource allocation.

(b) State Branch campaigns must:

- (i) Align with national brand, messaging framework, and policy platform;
- (ii) Use approved Association branding, logos, colors, and campaign materials;
- (iii) Comply strictly with all state electoral laws, funding rules, and reporting requirements;
- (iv) Operate within approved campaign budgets and seek approval for expenditures exceeding limits;
- (v) Coordinate media appearances, press releases, and major announcements with Communications Working Group;
- (vi) Report campaign activities, expenditures, and progress regularly to National Committee;
- (vii) Maintain high ethical standards and conduct campaigns consistent with Root Axiom and Value Gates.

(c) Campaign budgets:

- (i) Proposed by State Branch Committee based on electoral opportunities and fundraising capacity;
- (ii) Reviewed and approved by National Committee as part of annual budget or via special allocation;
- (iii) May be adjusted during campaign based on performance, fundraising, and strategic priorities;
- (iv) Must comply with state electoral funding limits and disclosure requirements.

(d) Campaign expenditures:

- (i) Follow NCAP protocol for amounts within Branch spending limits;
- (ii) Require National Committee approval for larger expenditures;
- (iii) Must be documented, receipted, and compliant with electoral law;
- (iv) Reviewed by Treasurer and reported regularly.

(5) **Campaign Compliance and Accountability:**

(a) All candidates and State Branch Committees are responsible for strict compliance with state electoral law including:

- (i) Candidate nomination and registration procedures;
- (ii) Electoral funding, donation, and expenditure rules;
- (iii) Disclosure and reporting requirements;
- (iv) Advertising and campaign material authorization statements;
- (v) Polling day procedures and scrutineering;
- (vi) Post-election reporting and financial returns.

(b) Association Treasurer and State Branch Treasurer must work together to:

- (i) Establish compliant financial management systems for campaigns;
- (ii) Track all campaign income and expenditure accurately;
- (iii) Ensure timely lodgment of all required returns and disclosures;
- (iv) Maintain records for required retention periods;
- (v) Respond to any Electoral Commission queries or audits.

(c) Compliance failures or legal breaches are serious matters that may result in:

- (i) Fines, penalties, or legal consequences for Association;
- (ii) Removal of candidate endorsement;
- (iii) Suspension or removal of State Branch Committee members;
- (iv) Suspension or dissolution of State Branch under Rule 33;
- (v) Damage to Association reputation and future electoral prospects.

(6) **Post-Election Accountability:**

(a) Within **30 days** of any election in which Association candidates stood, the State Branch Committee must submit comprehensive post-election report to National Committee including:

- (i) **Electoral results:** Votes received, percentages, seats won/lost, comparison to targets and expectations;
- (ii) **Campaign financial summary:** Total income, expenditure by category, compliance with budget and legal limits;
- (iii) **Volunteer and member engagement:** Number of volunteers, campaign team, member participation, capacity building;
- (iv) **Media coverage:** Summary of media appearances, coverage tone and reach, key messages delivered;
- (v) **Lessons learned:** What worked, what didn't, unexpected challenges, innovations, mistakes;
- (vi) **Recommendations:** Strategic insights for future campaigns, resource needs, process improvements;
- (vii) **Candidate feedback:** Reflections from candidates on their experience and suggestions.

(b) National Committee reviews post-election reports and:

- (i) Acknowledges and celebrates successes and strong performances;
- (ii) Identifies systemic issues or patterns across campaigns;
- (iii) Incorporates learnings into national campaign strategy;
- (iv) Assesses State Branch performance and capacity;
- (v) Determines resource allocation for future campaigns;
- (vi) May commission more detailed analysis or review if needed.

(c) Post-election reports are shared with Campaigns Working Group and used to continuously improve electoral capacity and performance.

### Rule 33: State Branch Suspension and Dissolution

(1) **Grounds for Suspension or Dissolution:**

National Committee may suspend or dissolve a State Branch if:

- (i) Paid Membership in state falls below **25** for more than **6 consecutive months** (50% of establishment threshold);
- (ii) Branch becomes inactive with no meetings, activities, or communications for **6 months**;
- (iii) Branch operates in a manner contrary to Root Axiom, Association purposes, or constitutional requirements;
- (iv) Branch engages in serious or repeated financial mismanagement, fraud, or non-compliance;
- (v) Branch fails repeatedly to comply with constitutional requirements, National Committee directions, or electoral law;
- (vi) Branch creates significant legal, financial, or reputational risk to Association;
- (vii) Branch loses state electoral registration due to negligence or mismanagement (as distinct from natural membership decline);
- (viii) Branch Committee requests dissolution.

(2) **Suspension Process:**

(a) Before suspending a State Branch, National Committee must:

- (i) Notify Branch Committee in writing of specific concerns, issues, or deficiencies;
- (ii) Provide at least **28 days** for Branch to respond, remedy issues, or provide explanation;
- (iii) Consider Branch response in good faith and with appropriate weight;
- (iv) Offer support, guidance, resources, or intervention to help Branch address issues if possible.

(b) Suspension requires **two-thirds majority vote** of National Committee.

(c) During suspension period:

- (i) Branch Committee's delegated authorities and NCAP privileges are revoked;
- (ii) Branch expenditures require explicit National Committee approval;
- (iii) National Committee may appoint administrator or interim Committee to manage Branch affairs;
- (iv) Branch members retain all membership rights and may participate in national activities;
- (v) Suspension continues until National Committee votes to lift suspension or proceed to dissolution.

(d) Suspension should be **remedial, not punitive** - the goal is to address issues and restore Branch to good standing, not to punish.

(3) **Dissolution Process:**

(a) Dissolution requires **two-thirds majority vote** of National Committee.

(b) Branch members must be notified of proposed dissolution at least **28 days in advance** and given opportunity to:

- (i) Be heard at National Committee meeting (in person, by representative, or written submission);
- (ii) Propose alternative remedies or plans to address issues;
- (iii) Appeal decision based on procedural grounds or substantive errors.

(c) Upon dissolution:

- (i) Branch Committee is formally disbanded;
- (ii) All Branch assets, funds, records, and liabilities transfer to National Committee;
- (iii) Branch-specific channels in community space are archived (not deleted - preserved for historical record);
- (iv) Members in that state become at-large members under Rule 28(4);
- (v) If Branch was registered with State Electoral Commission, National Committee must:
  - Notify Electoral Commission of Branch dissolution;
  - Fulfill any outstanding compliance or reporting obligations;
  - Consider whether to maintain state registration with National Committee directly managing it, or cancel registration;
- (vi) Branch may be re-established in future if criteria met and issues that led to dissolution resolved.

(d) Dissolution decision should be documented with:

- (i) Specific grounds and evidence;
- (ii) Summary of attempts at remediation;
- (iii) Consideration of member submissions;
- (iv) Reasoning for decision;
- (v) Pathway for potential re-establishment.

(4) **Voluntary Dissolution:**

(a) A State Branch may request voluntary dissolution by:

- (i) Vote of **two-thirds of Branch Paid Members** at a Branch General Meeting convened for that purpose; OR
- (ii) **Unanimous Branch Committee vote** with written notification to all Branch members and opportunity for member feedback.

(b) Voluntary dissolution request should include:

- (i) Reasons for requesting dissolution (declining membership, lack of capacity, strategic re-assessment, etc.);
- (ii) Branch financial statement and asset inventory;
- (iii) Plan for transitioning members, assets, and activities;
- (iv) Recommendations for whether to pursue re-establishment in future.

(c) National Committee should approve voluntary dissolution requests unless:

- (i) Compelling strategic reason to maintain Branch despite low activity;
- (ii) Electoral opportunity imminent that justifies preserving Branch;
- (iii) Dissolution appears to be result of resolvable conflict or temporary issue;
- (iv) National Committee believes Branch can be revitalized with support.

(d) Even if declining voluntary dissolution, National Committee should work collaboratively with Branch to address underlying issues rather than forcing unwilling continuation.

## PART 6: GENERAL MEETINGS

### Rule 35: Types of General Meetings

(1) **General Meetings:**

The Association conducts General Meetings for democratic decision-making by Eligible to Vote members on matters reserved to membership under this Constitution.

(2) **Types of General Meetings:**

(a) **Annual General Meeting (AGM):** Held once per year to:

- (i) Receive reports from Committee and Working Groups;
- (ii) Approve annual financial statements and audit/review;
- (iii) Elect National Committee for coming year;
- (iv) Approve annual budget;
- (v) Consider constitutional amendments;
- (vi) Conduct other business as required by this Constitution or proposed by members.

(b) **Special General Meeting (SGM):** Held as needed to:

- (i) Consider urgent matters that cannot wait until next AGM;
- (ii) Address specific motions or decisions requiring member vote;
- (iii) Resolve appeals from member discipline decisions;
- (iv) Consider matters referred by National Committee;
- (v) Consider matters petitioned by members.

(3) **Eligible Voters:**

(a) The following may vote at General Meetings:

- (i) All Paid Members in good standing (membership fees current, not suspended);
- (ii) All current National Committee members (regardless of whether they joined as Free or Paid Members).

(b) Free Members who are not Committee members may attend and speak at General Meetings but have no voting rights.

### Rule 36: Annual General Meeting

(1) **Timing of AGM:**

(a) The Association must hold an AGM within **5 months** after the end of each financial year (by November 30 each year, given June 30 financial year end).

(b) The first AGM must be held within **18 months** after incorporation.

(c) AGMs in subsequent years must be held no more than **15 months** after the previous AGM.

(2) **Notice of AGM:**

(a) The Secretary must give all members at least **21 days written notice** of the AGM.

(b) Notice must be distributed via:

- (i) Announcement in community space (pinned in appropriate channel);
- (ii) Email to all members at their registered email addresses;
- (iii) Posted to Association website.

(c) Notice must include:

- (i) Date, time, and location (physical and/or online);
- (ii) Agenda and order of business;
- (iii) Details of how to participate (in-person, online, proxy);
- (iv) Motions to be considered (including constitutional amendments);
- (v) Nominations for Committee positions (if received by notice deadline);
- (vi) Annual reports from Committee, Treasurer, and Working Groups;
- (vii) Annual financial statements;
- (viii) Proposed budget for coming year;
- (ix) Deadline for additional motions, nominations, and proxy appointments.

(d) Members may submit motions for AGM agenda by written notice to Secretary at least **14 days before AGM**.

(3) **AGM Agenda:**

The agenda for the AGM must include at minimum:

(a) **Opening and formalities:**

- (i) Welcome and acknowledgment of Country (if appropriate);
- (ii) Apologies and confirmation of quorum;
- (iii) Appointment of chairperson for meeting;
- (iv) Approval of minutes from previous AGM.

(b) **Reports:**

- (i) Secretary report on Association activities and governance;
- (ii) Treasurer report and presentation of annual financial statements;
- (iii) Auditor or reviewer's report (if applicable under Rule 56);
- (iv) National Committee report on strategic priorities and achievements;
- (v) Working Group annual reports (may be presented by Coordinators or summarized by Secretary);
- (vi) State Branch reports (if any Branches established);
- (vii) Root Axiom Impact Report under Rule 4(3).

(c) **Financial matters:**

- (i) Consideration and approval of annual financial statements;
- (ii) Consideration and approval of budget for coming year;
- (iii) Appointment of auditor or reviewer for coming year (if required).

(d) **Elections:**

- (i) Election of National Committee (Secretary, Treasurer, and Committee members);
- (ii) Announcement of results and incoming Committee.

(e) **Constitutional and policy matters:**

- (i) Consideration of proposed constitutional amendments (if any);
- (ii) Consideration of member motions and resolutions;
- (iii) Constitutional review report under Rule 83.

(f) **Other business:**

- (i) Recognition and thanks to outgoing Committee members, volunteers, and contributors;
- (ii) Strategic priorities for coming year;
- (iii) General business and member questions;
- (iv) Closing remarks.

(4) **Quorum for AGM:**

(a) Quorum for AGM is **20% of Eligible to Vote members** or **20 Eligible to Vote members**, whichever is **lower**.

(b) For purposes of calculating quorum:

- (i) Members present in person count toward quorum;
- (ii) Members present online/virtually count toward quorum;
- (iii) Members voting by proxy count toward quorum;
- (iv) Members who have lodged votes on motions in advance (if permitted) count toward quorum.

(c) If quorum is not present within **30 minutes** of scheduled start time:

- (i) Meeting is adjourned to same time **7 days later** at same or similar venue;
- (ii) All members must be notified of adjournment and new date/time;
- (iii) If quorum not present at adjourned meeting, those present constitute quorum and meeting may proceed.

(5) **Voting at AGM:**

(a) Each Eligible to Vote member has **one vote** on each motion or election.

(b) Voting methods:

- (i) **In-person:** Show of hands, voice vote, or ballot as determined by chairperson;
- (ii) **Online:** Electronic voting via governance infrastructure or video conference platform (reactions, polls, chat);
- (iii) **Proxy:** As provided under Rule 37;
- (iv) **Advance voting:** If permitted by Committee for specific motions (e.g., constitutional amendments), electronic voting open for period before AGM.

(c) Unless this Constitution specifies otherwise, motions are decided by **simple majority** of votes cast (50% + 1).

(d) Tied votes fail (motion not passed).

(e) Voting is not secret unless:

- (i) Motion requests secret ballot and is approved by majority;
- (ii) Election contest with multiple candidates (elections conducted by secret ballot);
- (iii) Sensitive matter where chairperson determines secret ballot appropriate.

(6) **Chairperson of AGM:**

(a) The AGM is chaired by:

- (i) The Secretary; OR
- (ii) Another Committee member appointed by the Secretary; OR
- (iii) A member elected by the meeting if Secretary absent or delegates role.

(b) Chairperson's role:

- (i) Ensure meeting follows agenda and procedures;
- (ii) Manage speaking times and debate;
- (iii) Determine voting method and count votes (or appoint scrutineers);
- (iv) Rule on points of order and procedural matters;
- (v) Maintain order and respectful discourse;
- (vi) Ensure minutes are taken accurately.

(c) Chairperson has both deliberative vote (as a member) and casting vote (to break ties) if necessary.

### Rule 37: Proxy Voting

(1) **Proxy Voting Permitted:**

Eligible to Vote members who cannot attend a General Meeting may appoint a proxy to vote on their behalf.

(2) **Proxy Appointment:**

(a) A proxy must be appointed by written notice to the Secretary in the form approved by the Committee.

(b) The proxy appointment must:

- (i) Be signed by the member appointing the proxy (appointing member);
- (ii) Specify the proxy holder's name (the person authorized to vote);
- (iii) Specify which meeting the proxy is for (AGM or specific SGM);
- (iv) Be received by Secretary at least **24 hours before** the meeting.

(c) A proxy appointment may be:

- (i) **General:** Proxy holder may vote as they choose on all matters;
- (ii) **Specific:** Proxy holder directed to vote in particular way on specified motions (voting instructions must be clear);
- (iii) **Hybrid:** Proxy holder directed on some matters, discretion on others.

(3) **Proxy Holder Requirements:**

(a) A proxy holder must be an Eligible to Vote member.

(b) A proxy holder may hold proxies from multiple members, but no member may hold proxies for more than **5% of total Eligible to Vote members** or **5 members**, whichever is **greater**.

(c) This limit prevents concentration of proxy votes and ensures democratic participation.

(d) Committee members should be cautious about holding large numbers of proxies to avoid perception of vote-stacking.

(4) **Proxy Voting Procedure:**

(a) Proxy holders must identify themselves and the members they represent at the start of the meeting.

(b) For each vote, proxy holder votes according to:

- (i) Specific instructions if provided in proxy appointment;
- (ii) Their own judgment if general proxy;
- (iii) They must announce which votes are proxies vs. their own vote if asked.

(c) Proxy votes count toward quorum and vote tallies exactly as if the appointing member were present.

(5) **Revocation of Proxy:**

(a) A member may revoke a proxy at any time before it is exercised by:

- (i) Written notice to Secretary;
- (ii) Attending the meeting in person (presence automatically revokes proxy);
- (iii) Appointing a new proxy (replaces previous proxy).

(b) If appointing member attends meeting after proxy lodged, the proxy is void and member votes directly.

(6) **Proxy Restrictions:**

Proxy voting is **not permitted** for:

- (i) Elections to National Committee (must vote directly in person or online);
- (ii) Matters where personal attendance is required by law or this Constitution;
- (iii) Votes requiring two-thirds or higher majorities unless specifically permitted.

Committee may permit proxy voting for constitutional amendments and other supermajority votes if it determines democratic participation is better served by allowing proxies.

### Rule 38: Special General Meetings

(1) **Calling a Special General Meeting:**

(a) A Special General Meeting (SGM) may be called by:

- (i) **National Committee** by majority vote at any Committee meeting;
- (ii) **Secretary** on direction of the Committee;
- (iii) **Member petition** signed by at least **20% of Eligible to Vote members** or **20 Eligible to Vote members**, whichever is **lower**.

(b) When members petition for SGM, the petition must:

- (i) Be in writing and signed by required number of Eligible to Vote members;
- (ii) State the purpose and proposed motions for the SGM clearly;
- (iii) Be submitted to the Secretary.

(c) Upon receiving valid member petition:

- (i) Secretary must verify signatures and eligibility of petitioners;
- (ii) If valid, Secretary must call SGM within **21 days** of receiving petition;
- (iii) SGM must be held within **60 days** of receiving petition;
- (iv) If Secretary fails to call SGM within required timeframe, any petitioner may call the meeting directly.

(2) **Notice of SGM:**

(a) Secretary must give all members at least **14 days written notice** of SGM.

(b) Notice must be distributed via:

- (i) Announcement in community space;
- (ii) Email to all members;
- (iii) Posted to Association website.

(c) Notice must include:

- (i) Date, time, and location (physical and/or online);
- (ii) Purpose of the SGM (specific matters to be considered);
- (iii) Motions to be voted on with full text;
- (iv) Details of how to participate (in-person, online, proxy);
- (v) Any supporting documents or reports relevant to the motions.

(d) Notice period may be shortened to **7 days** if:

- (i) Urgent matter requiring immediate attention;
- (ii) Committee unanimously agrees shortened notice is necessary; AND
- (iii) Reasonable efforts made to notify all members.

(3) **SGM Agenda:**

(a) SGM agenda is limited to:

- (i) Matters specified in the notice calling the meeting;
- (ii) Procedural matters (appointment of chairperson, approval of minutes from previous SGM if applicable);
- (iii) No other business unless all Eligible to Vote members present consent.

(b) This ensures members attend knowing what will be decided and prevents surprise motions.

(4) **Quorum and Voting at SGM:**

(a) Quorum for SGM is **15% of Eligible to Vote members** or **15 Eligible to Vote members**, whichever is **lower** (lower threshold than AGM to facilitate urgent decision-making).

(b) If quorum not present within **30 minutes**, meeting is adjourned to **7 days later**. If quorum not present at adjourned meeting, those present constitute quorum.

(c) Voting procedures, chairperson, and proxy rules same as AGM (Rules 36 and 37).

(5) **Common Uses of SGM:**

Special General Meetings are typically called for:

- (i) Urgent constitutional amendments that cannot wait until next AGM;
- (ii) Member appeals of discipline decisions under Rule 13(7);
- (iii) Major strategic decisions requiring member approval (e.g., major asset purchases, significant partnerships);
- (iv) Removal of Committee member by member petition under Rule 19(1)(g);
- (v) Dissolution of Association under Rule 88;
- (vi) Other urgent or extraordinary matters requiring member decision.

### Rule 39: General Meeting Procedures

(1) **Conduct of Meetings:**

(a) General Meetings must be conducted in accordance with:

- (i) This Constitution;
- (ii) Common law principles of meeting procedure;
- (iii) Procedures adopted by the Committee for Association meetings;
- (iv) Chairperson's reasonable rulings on procedural matters.

(b) Meetings should be:

- (i) Accessible to all members (hybrid in-person and online strongly preferred);
- (ii) Conducted in respectful, inclusive, and democratic manner;
- (iii) Efficient and focused on agenda items;
- (iv) Documented with accurate minutes.

(2) **Motions and Debate:**

(a) Motions at General Meetings must be:

- (i) Moved by an Eligible to Vote member;
- (ii) Seconded by another Eligible to Vote member;
- (iii) Stated clearly in writing or verbally for the record;
- (iv) Relevant to the meeting agenda (or accepted by chairperson as in order).

(b) Debate on motions:

- (i) Mover speaks first to explain and advocate for the motion;
- (ii) Other members may speak for or against;
- (iii) Chairperson manages speaking list and time limits fairly;
- (iv) Mover has right of reply at end of debate;
- (v) Debate closes when no members wish to speak or chairperson calls vote.

(c) Amendments to motions:

- (i) May be proposed during debate;
- (ii) Must be seconded;
- (iii) Voted on before main motion;
- (iv) If passed, motion is amended and then voted on as amended.

(3) **Points of Order and Procedure:**

(a) Any member may raise **point of order** at any time if they believe:

- (i) Meeting procedures are not being followed;
- (ii) Debate has strayed from the motion;
- (iii) Speaker is out of order or disrespectful;
- (iv) Constitution is being violated.

(b) Chairperson must rule on point of order immediately.

(c) Chairperson's ruling may be challenged by motion from the floor:

- (i) "That the chairperson's ruling be disagreed with";
- (ii) Requires seconder and simple majority to overturn;
- (iii) If overturned, chairperson's ruling is reversed.

(4) **Participation Options:**

(a) General Meetings should accommodate participation via:

- (i) **In-person attendance:** Physical venue with capacity for anticipated attendance;
- (ii) **Online attendance:** Video conference platform allowing real-time audio/video participation and voting;
- (iii) **Hybrid model preferred:** Simultaneous in-person and online to maximize accessibility.

(b) Online participants have full rights to:

- (i) See and hear meeting proceedings;
- (ii) Speak and move/second motions;
- (iii) Vote on all matters;
- (iv) Raise points of order.

(c) Technology failures that prevent participation or voting should result in:

- (i) Adjournment if quorum lost;
- (ii) Delay of votes until issue resolved;
- (iii) Alternative voting method offered.

(5) **Minutes of General Meetings:**

(a) Secretary (or appointed minute-taker) must take accurate minutes including:

- (i) Date, time, location, and format of meeting;
- (ii) Names of attendees, apologies, and proxy holders;
- (iii) Confirmation of quorum;
- (iv) All motions proposed, movers/seconders, and results of votes;
- (v) Vote tallies for significant motions;
- (vi) Election results;
- (vii) Key points of debate on major motions (summary, not verbatim);
- (viii) Reports presented (can be attached rather than transcribed);
- (ix) Time of adjournment.

(b) Draft minutes must be:

- (i) Prepared within **14 days** of the meeting;
- (ii) Published to members in community space and via email;
- (iii) Approved at next General Meeting (with corrections if needed);
- (iv) Signed by chairperson once approved.

(c) Approved minutes are the authoritative record of the meeting under Rule 21A.

(6) **Recording of Meetings:**

(a) General Meetings may be recorded (audio or video) if:

- (i) Committee approves recording in advance;
- (ii) All attendees are notified at start of meeting that recording is occurring;
- (iii) Recording is for internal record-keeping or publishing to members who couldn't attend.

(b) Recordings must not be:

- (i) Edited to misrepresent what occurred;
- (ii) Published publicly without Committee approval (to protect member privacy);
- (iii) Used for purposes other than Association business.

(c) Any member may request recording be stopped for discussion of sensitive personal matter. Chairperson should accommodate reasonable requests.

### Rule 40: Voting Thresholds and Requirements

(1) **Simple Majority (Default):**

Unless this Constitution specifies otherwise, all motions at General Meetings are decided by **simple majority** (more than 50%) of votes cast by Eligible to Vote members present (in person, online, or by proxy).

(2) **Supermajority Thresholds:**

The following matters require supermajority votes as specified:

(a) **Two-thirds majority (66.67%):**

- (i) Removal of Committee member under Rule 19(1)(g);
- (ii) Approval of State Branch electoral registration under Rule 30(4)(b);
- (iii) Dissolution of Association under Rule 88(1);
- (iv) Class 3 constitutional amendments (Structural Governance) under Rule 82(2)(c);
- (v) Other matters specifically requiring two-thirds majority in this Constitution.

(b) **80% supermajority:**

- (i) Class 2 constitutional amendments (Core Values and Purposes) under Rule 82(2)(b).

(c) **90% of 75% of Eligible to Vote members (effectively 67.5% of all members):**

- (i) Class 1 constitutional amendments (Root Axiom) under Rule 82(2)(a).

(3) **Quorum Requirements:**

Quorum requirements for supermajority votes:

(a) **Class 1 amendments (Root Axiom):** Require 75% of all Eligible to Vote members to participate (in person, online, or proxy), and 90% of those participating must vote yes.

(b) **Class 2 amendments (Core Values):** Require 50% quorum of Eligible to Vote members.

(c) **Class 3 amendments (Structural Governance):** Require 33% quorum of Eligible to Vote members.

(d) **Class 4 amendments (Operational Procedures):** Require 20% quorum of Eligible to Vote members (same as general AGM quorum).

(e) **Other supermajority votes:** Use standard quorum for meeting type (AGM 20%, SGM 15%) unless otherwise specified.

(4) **Calculating Vote Thresholds:**

(a) Percentages are calculated based on **votes cast**, not total Eligible to Vote members, except for Class 1 amendments which use total members as base.

(b) Abstentions are not counted as votes cast.

(c) Fractions are rounded up (e.g., two-thirds of 10 votes = 7 votes required, not 6.67).

(d) Tied votes fail (motion is not passed).

(5) **Special Voting Procedures for Constitutional Amendments:**

(a) Due to importance of constitutional amendments, the following enhanced procedures apply:

**(i) Extended notice:** Proposed amendments must be circulated to all members at least **21 days before** the meeting (or **28 days** for Class 1-2 amendments).

**(ii) Full text:** Notice must include complete text of proposed amendment, not just summary.

**(iii) Rationale:** Proposer should provide written explanation of why amendment is needed and how it advances Root Axiom.

**(iv) Committee analysis:** Committee may provide independent analysis of amendment's implications, costs, benefits, and alignment with Association purposes.

**(v) Advance voting:** For Class 1-2 amendments, Committee should enable advance electronic voting for members who cannot attend the meeting, open for at least **7 days** before meeting.

**(vi) Debate:** Amendments should have adequate debate time at the meeting before vote is called.

(b) These procedures ensure informed democratic decision-making on constitutional matters.

### Rule 41: Electronic and Asynchronous General Meetings

(1) **Electronic General Meetings:**

(a) The Committee may determine that a General Meeting be held entirely electronically (no physical venue) if:

- (i) Circumstances make physical gathering impractical (pandemic, geographic dispersion, cost);
- (ii) Technology allows full member participation (audio/video, real-time voting);
- (iii) All members are given clear instructions on how to participate;
- (iv) Technical support is available during the meeting.

(b) Electronic-only General Meetings must meet same requirements as hybrid meetings (notice, quorum, voting, minutes).

(2) **Asynchronous Voting for Specific Motions:**

(a) For specific motions that do not require real-time debate (e.g., uncontroversial constitutional amendments, election of unopposed Committee members), the Committee may enable **asynchronous voting** where:

- (i) Motion and all supporting materials distributed to members;
- (ii) Voting period open for at least **7 days**;
- (iii) Members vote electronically at their convenience during voting period;
- (iv) Discussion occurs asynchronously in community space;
- (v) Results announced at conclusion of voting period.

(b) Asynchronous voting may increase participation by accommodating diverse schedules and timezones.

(c) Motions appropriate for asynchronous voting:

- (i) Class 4 constitutional amendments (operational, non-controversial);
- (ii) Election of single candidate running unopposed;
- (iii) Routine approvals (financial statements where no concerns raised);
- (iv) Motions where Committee believes debate has already occurred sufficiently in community space.

(d) Motions NOT appropriate for asynchronous voting:

- (i) Contested elections (multiple candidates);
- (ii) Controversial constitutional amendments;
- (iii) Member discipline appeals;
- (iv) Complex motions requiring deliberation;
- (v) Matters where real-time debate is valuable.

(3) **Hybrid AGM Model:**

(a) The Association should default to **hybrid AGM model** combining:

- (i) Real-time meeting (in-person and/or online) for reports, elections, debate, and time-sensitive votes;
- (ii) Asynchronous voting period for routine or non-controversial motions that opened before the meeting and closes after;
- (iii) Community space discussion threads for each major motion, open for weeks before AGM.

(b) This model maximizes participation while preserving value of synchronous gathering for community building, elections, and debate.

## PART 7: COMMITTEE MEETINGS

### Rule 42: Committee Meeting Requirements

(1) **Frequency of Committee Meetings:**

(a) The National Committee must meet at least **once per month** during the calendar year.

(b) The Committee may meet more frequently as needed to conduct Association business effectively.

(c) Meetings may be scheduled:

- (i) On a regular recurring schedule (e.g., first Saturday of each month);
- (ii) Ad hoc as called by Secretary or Committee resolution;
- (iii) Emergency meetings as needed (see Rule 42(5)).

(d) The Committee should avoid meeting during:

- (i) Extended periods (more than 6 weeks) without any meetings;
- (ii) Critical periods (election campaigns, major initiatives) without adequate meeting frequency.

(2) **Committee Meeting Formats:**

(a) Committee meetings may be held:

- (i) **In-person:** Physical gathering at agreed location;
- (ii) **Online:** Video conference or voice call using agreed platform;
- (iii) **Hybrid:** Simultaneous in-person and online participation;
- (iv) **Asynchronous:** For specific routine decisions, using community space discussion and NCAP-style voting.

(b) **Asynchronous Committee meetings** are appropriate for:

- (i) Routine administrative decisions;
- (ii) Time-sensitive matters between scheduled meetings;
- (iii) Simple approvals that don't require debate;
- (iv) When synchronous meeting is not possible due to scheduling conflicts.

(c) Asynchronous meetings are **not appropriate** for:

- (i) Complex strategic decisions requiring discussion;
- (ii) Sensitive personnel or discipline matters;
- (iii) Budget planning or significant financial decisions;
- (iv) Conflict resolution or contentious issues;
- (v) Matters requiring immediate back-and-forth deliberation.

(3) **Notice of Committee Meetings:**

(a) The Secretary must give Committee members at least **7 days notice** of scheduled Committee meetings.

(b) Notice must include:

- (i) Date, time, and location (or online meeting link);
- (ii) Agenda items to be discussed;
- (iii) Any papers, reports, or documents to be considered;
- (iv) Instructions for submitting additional agenda items.

(c) Notice may be shortened to **48 hours** for:

- (i) Urgent matters requiring prompt Committee attention;
- (ii) Emergency meetings under Rule 42(5).

(d) Notice is deemed given when:

- (i) Posted in Committee channel in community space; AND
- (ii) Emailed to all Committee members' registered email addresses.

(4) **Agenda Setting:**

(a) The Secretary prepares the meeting agenda in consultation with the Coordinator (if position exists) or Committee members.

(b) Agenda must include:

- (i) Apologies and confirmation of quorum;
- (ii) Approval of minutes from previous meeting;
- (iii) Matters arising from previous minutes (action items);
- (iv) Reports (Secretary, Treasurer, Working Groups, State Branches);
- (v) Correspondence requiring Committee attention;
- (vi) Specific business items requiring decisions;
- (vii) Strategic discussion items;
- (viii) General business and member matters;
- (ix) Date and time of next meeting.

(c) Committee members may request agenda items by notifying Secretary at least **3 days before** the meeting (or shorter time if urgent).

(d) Agenda should be circulated with meeting notice at least **3 days before** meeting to allow Committee members to prepare.

(5) **Emergency Committee Meetings:**

(a) Emergency Committee meetings may be called with **24 hours notice** or less if:

- (i) Urgent crisis requiring immediate Committee decision (legal threat, safety issue, major reputational risk);
- (ii) Time-sensitive opportunity that cannot wait for next scheduled meeting;
- (iii) Compliance deadline or legal requirement.

(b) Emergency meetings may be called by:

- (i) Secretary on their own initiative;
- (ii) Any two Committee members jointly requesting Secretary to call meeting;
- (iii) Majority of Committee members.

(c) Notice of emergency meeting must:

- (i) State clearly why emergency meeting is necessary;
- (ii) Specify the specific urgent matter(s) to be considered;
- (iii) Use all reasonable means to reach Committee members (email, phone, community space, SMS).

(d) Emergency meeting agenda is limited to:

- (i) The urgent matter(s) specified in notice;
- (ii) Procedural matters necessary to conduct the meeting;
- (iii) No other business unless all Committee members present consent.

(6) **Standing Items:**

The following should be standing agenda items at most Committee meetings:

- (i) Review of NCAP authorizations since last meeting (summary report from governance infrastructure);
- (ii) Financial report from Treasurer (expenditures, income, budget status);
- (iii) Working Group updates (brief reports or flag significant issues);
- (iv) State Branch updates (if any established);
- (v) Membership status (new members, resignations, conversions);
- (vi) Upcoming deadlines or compliance requirements;
- (vii) Action items from previous meetings (progress check).

### Rule 43: Quorum and Voting at Committee Meetings

(1) **Quorum for Committee Meetings:**

(a) Quorum for Committee meetings is **50% of Committee members** (rounded up).

(b) If Committee has 5 members, quorum is 3.
If Committee has 7 members, quorum is 4.
If Committee has 9 members, quorum is 5.

(c) Committee members participating in meeting via any format (in-person, online, phone) count toward quorum.

(d) If quorum is not present within **15 minutes** of scheduled start time:

- (i) Meeting is adjourned to time and date determined by Secretary;
- (ii) All Committee members notified of adjournment;
- (iii) If urgent, Secretary may call emergency meeting with shorter notice.

(e) If quorum is lost during meeting (members leave before conclusion):

- (i) No further binding decisions can be made;
- (ii) Discussion may continue for information sharing;
- (iii) Any decisions requiring votes must be deferred to next meeting or asynchronous vote.

(2) **Voting at Committee Meetings:**

(a) Each Committee member has **one vote** on each motion.

(b) Decisions are made by **simple majority** (more than 50%) of votes cast, unless this Constitution requires higher threshold.

(c) Chairperson has both:

- (i) **Deliberative vote:** As a Committee member; AND
- (ii) **Casting vote:** To break ties (may vote twice if necessary to resolve tied vote).

(d) Alternatively, chairperson may choose to use only deliberative vote and allow tied votes to fail.

(e) Abstentions:

- (i) Committee members may abstain from votes;
- (ii) Abstentions are not counted as votes cast;
- (iii) Committee members should abstain when they have conflict of interest under Rule 20(3)(d).

(3) **Supermajority Votes at Committee Meetings:**

The following Committee decisions require **two-thirds majority**:

- (i) Approval of State Branch electoral registration applications under Rule 30(4)(b);
- (ii) Termination of membership under Rule 13(4);
- (iii) Suspension or dissolution of State Branch under Rules 33(2)(b) and 33(3)(a);
- (iv) Other matters specifically requiring two-thirds majority in this Constitution.

(4) **Unanimous Votes at Committee Meetings:**

The following decisions require **unanimous Committee approval**:

- (i) Shortening AGM notice period below 21 days (emergency AGM);
- (ii) Other matters specifically requiring unanimity in this Constitution.

(5) **Asynchronous Committee Voting:**

(a) For matters that can be decided without real-time discussion, the Committee may vote asynchronously via:

- (i) Discussion thread in Committee channel in community space;
- (ii) Electronic poll or voting tool;
- (iii) Email thread (discouraged per Rule 25, but permitted for formal votes).

(b) Asynchronous vote must:

- (i) Clearly state the motion or decision being made;
- (ii) Remain open for at least **48 hours** to allow all Committee members opportunity to vote;
- (iii) Be documented with results and included in next meeting minutes.

(c) Asynchronous votes require same quorum and majority thresholds as synchronous meetings.

(d) If asynchronous vote does not achieve quorum or majority within voting period, matter is deferred to next Committee meeting.

### Rule 44: Committee Meeting Procedures

(1) **Chairperson of Committee Meetings:**

(a) Committee meetings are chaired by:

- (i) The Secretary;
- (ii) A Committee member appointed by the Secretary to chair specific meeting;
- (iii) A Committee member elected by the Committee at the meeting if Secretary absent.

(b) The chairperson's role:

- (i) Open meeting and confirm quorum;
- (ii) Guide meeting through agenda;
- (iii) Manage discussion and ensure all voices heard;
- (iv) Call votes and determine results;
- (v) Rule on procedural matters and points of order;
- (vi) Maintain focus and time management;
- (vii) Ensure respectful and productive discourse;
- (viii) Exercise casting vote to break ties if necessary.

(2) **Motions and Decisions:**

(a) Committee decisions may be made by:

- (i) **Formal motion:** Moved and seconded, then debated and voted on;
- (ii) **Consensus:** If all Committee members agree, decision can be recorded without formal vote;
- (iii) **Asynchronous vote:** As described in Rule 43(5).

(b) Significant decisions should be formalized as motions for clarity and documentation.

(c) Routine or administrative matters may be decided by consensus or chair's ruling if no objection.

(3) **Conflicts of Interest:**

(a) Committee members must disclose conflicts of interest when they arise, including:

- (i) Financial interest in matter being decided;
- (ii) Personal relationship with person affected by decision;
- (iii) Dual role creating potential conflict (e.g., deciding on Working Group budget when serving as WG Coordinator);
- (iv) Any other circumstance where impartiality might be questioned.

(b) When conflict disclosed, Committee member should:

- (i) Explain nature of conflict;
- (ii) Abstain from vote on that matter unless Committee agrees conflict is immaterial;
- (iii) Consider leaving meeting during discussion if conflict is significant;
- (iv) Recuse themselves entirely if conflict is severe or Committee requests.

(c) Failure to disclose material conflict of interest may be grounds for removal under Rule 19(1).

(d) Committee members should err on side of disclosure rather than concealment - transparency builds trust.

(4) **Committee Discussion and Debate:**

(a) Committee meetings should foster:

- (i) Open and honest discussion of issues;
- (ii) Constructive debate and diverse perspectives;
- (iii) Questioning of assumptions and proposals;
- (iv) Collaborative problem-solving;
- (v) Respectful disagreement without personal attacks.

(b) Committee members should:

- (i) Come prepared (read agenda materials in advance);
- (ii) Participate actively in discussions;
- (iii) Listen to others' perspectives;
- (iv) Speak concisely and stay on topic;
- (v) Focus on what best serves Association and Root Axiom, not personal preferences.

(c) Chairperson should ensure:

- (i) All Committee members have opportunity to speak;
- (ii) No single member dominates discussion;
- (iii) Quieter members are encouraged to contribute;
- (iv) Debate remains focused and productive;
- (v) Time is managed appropriately.

(5) **Time Management:**

(a) Committee meetings should have reasonable time limits:

- (i) Regular meetings: 1.5 to 2 hours default (may extend if necessary);
- (ii) Strategic planning sessions: 3-4 hours with breaks;
- (iii) Emergency meetings: As brief as possible to address urgent matter.

(b) Chairperson should:

- (i) Allocate time to agenda items based on importance;
- (ii) Keep discussions moving when adequate debate has occurred;
- (iii) Defer less urgent matters if running over time;
- (iv) Schedule follow-up meeting if critical business cannot be completed.

(c) Committee should regularly assess:

- (i) Whether meetings are too long or too frequent (burnout risk);
- (ii) Whether meetings are too short or infrequent (inadequate governance);
- (iii) Whether asynchronous decision-making could reduce meeting time.

(6) **Attendance and Apologies:**

(a) Committee members are expected to:

- (i) Attend all Committee meetings unless unable due to genuine conflict;
- (ii) Provide apology (advance notice of absence) if cannot attend;
- (iii) Review minutes and catch up on decisions made in their absence.

(b) Apologies should be sent to Secretary as soon as possible before the meeting.

(c) Consistent non-attendance:

- (i) Absence from **3 consecutive meetings** without apology or leave creates casual vacancy under Rule 19(1)(c);
- (ii) Committee should proactively address attendance issues with struggling members (support, workload reduction, role changes) before invoking vacancy provisions;
- (iii) Root Axiom requires eliminating suffering - if Committee member struggling, help them rather than punish.

(7) **Confidentiality:**

(a) Committee discussions and deliberations are **confidential** unless:

- (i) Committee explicitly decides to make information public;
- (ii) Disclosure required by law;
- (iii) Information is already public knowledge;
- (iv) Transparency requires disclosure (see Rule 44(8)).

(b) Committee members must not:

- (i) Share confidential Committee discussions outside Committee;
- (ii) Disclose sensitive information about members, staff, or third parties;
- (iii) Use confidential information for personal benefit;
- (iv) Share information that could harm Association if made public.

(c) Confidentiality obligations continue after Committee member's term ends.

(d) Breach of confidentiality is serious matter that may result in:

- (i) Formal reprimand;
- (ii) Removal from Committee;
- (iii) Termination of membership in severe cases;
- (iv) Legal action if breach causes significant harm.

(8) **Transparency and Member Communication:**

(a) While Committee deliberations are confidential, Committee **decisions and outcomes** should be transparent to members.

(b) After each Committee meeting, Secretary should publish:

- (i) Summary of key decisions made;
- (ii) Actions assigned to Committee members or Working Groups;
- (iii) Information members need to know (policy changes, upcoming events, strategic priorities);
- (iv) Rationale for significant decisions (Root Axiom alignment, Value Gates application).

(c) Summary may be published via:

- (i) Announcement in community space;
- (ii) Included in newsletter or regular communications;
- (iii) Posted to website.

(d) Information that should **not** be shared publicly:

- (i) Confidential member information or discipline matters;
- (ii) Commercially sensitive negotiations or contracts;
- (iii) Legal advice or litigation strategy;
- (iv) Personnel matters;
- (v) Information that could harm Association if disclosed prematurely.

(e) When in doubt, Committee should err toward transparency while protecting legitimate confidentiality interests.

### Rule 47: Committee Meetings and Root Axiom Alignment

(1) **Root Axiom Integration:**

Committee meetings should systematically apply the Root Axiom framework by:

(a) **Efficiency assessment:** Asking for each significant decision:

- (i) What inefficiencies does this address or create?

(c) **Evidence requirement:** Asking for each significant decision:

- (i) What evidence supports this approach?
- (ii) Have we considered alternatives with evidence basis?
- (iii) How will we measure success or failure?
- (iv) What assumptions are we making and are they valid?

(2) **Regular Root Axiom Check-Ins:**

(a) At each Committee meeting, include standing agenda item:

- "Root Axiom Alignment Check: Are our recent decisions and activities advancing the Root Axiom?"

(b) Quarterly, Committee should conduct deeper review:

- (i) Which activities are most aligned with Root Axiom?
- (ii) Which activities are questionable or misaligned?
- (iii) What inefficiencies have we identified in our own operations?
- (iv) What unnecessary suffering have we observed in our community or processes?
- (v) What changes should we make based on Root Axiom analysis?

(c) Annual Root Axiom Impact Report (Rule 4(3)) should draw heavily from these Committee discussions.

(3) **Continuous Improvement Culture:**

(a) Committee should foster culture of:

- (i) Questioning established practices;
- (ii) Welcoming constructive criticism;
- (iii) Experimenting with new approaches;
- (iv) Learning from failures;
- (v) Iterating based on evidence.

(b) Committee should model Adaptability value gate by:

- (i) Regularly reviewing and refining Committee processes;
- (ii) Adopting better tools and systems when available;
- (iii) Eliminating procedures that no longer serve purpose;
- (iv) Documenting lessons learned and sharing with membership.

## PART 8: DECISION-MAKING PROTOCOLS

### Rule 48: NCAP Philosophy and Principles

(1) **High-Velocity Decision-Making:**

(a) Traditional governance bottlenecks:

- Decisions require meetings (scheduling delay);
- Decisions require quorum (availability constraint);
- Decisions require active approval (coordination overhead);
- Decisions wait for next meeting cycle (artificial delays);
- Small decisions consume same process as large ones (inefficiency).

(b) **NCAP eliminates these bottlenecks:**

- Decisions made asynchronously (no meeting required);
- Decisions proceed with participation (no quorum requirement);
- **Silence equals consent** (no active approval needed, just no objection);
- Decisions resolve continuously (no waiting for meeting cycles);
- Decision speed scales to significance and consensus.

(c) **Root Axiom alignment:**

- NCAP eliminates **inefficiency** of meeting overhead, scheduling delays, and coordination friction;
- NCAP eliminates **suffering** of volunteers waiting for permission, leaders bottlenecked by approvals, and momentum lost to bureaucracy;
- NCAP enables rapid iteration, experimentation, and adaptation based on evidence.

(2) **Negative Consent Principle:**

(a) **Negative consent (opt-out) vs. positive consent (opt-in):**

**(i) Positive consent (traditional):**

- Decision requires active approval ("say yes to proceed");
- Silence or absence blocks action;
- Bias toward inaction (default is "no");
- Requires coordination to gather approvals.

**(ii) Negative consent (NCAP):**

- Decision proceeds unless objection raised ("say no to stop");
- **Silence equals approval** (everyone had opportunity to object, didn't, therefore consents);
- Bias toward action (default is "yes");
- Requires attention only when something's wrong.

(b) **When negative consent appropriate:**

- Routine operational decisions (communications posts, minor spending, standard processes);
- Decisions within established policy and authority (not creating new precedent);
- Situations where inaction is worse than imperfect action (time-sensitive opportunities);
- Contexts where trust and competence established (Working Groups operating within scope);
- Decisions that are reversible or low-risk (can be undone if problems emerge).

(c) **When negative consent NOT appropriate:**

- Major strategic decisions (constitutional amendments, large budget allocations);
- Decisions requiring deliberation (complex policy choices, controversial issues);
- High-stakes or irreversible decisions (terminating members, dissolving Working Groups);
- Decisions outside established authority (creating new precedent, exceeding scope);
- Situations where active consent demonstrates commitment (candidate endorsements, coalition agreements).

(3) **The "Moving Walkway" Analogy:**

(a) To understand NCAP, imagine a **moving walkway at an airport**:
  - The default state is **forward movement** toward the destination (the decision).
  - You are moving toward the destination even if you stand still (**Silence = Consent**).

(b) **Accelerating (Approve):**
  - If you want to get there faster, you walk forward.
  - The more people walking (approving), the faster the group arrives at the destination.

(c) **Emergency Stop (Object):**
  - If you see a problem (a safety hazard, a loose shoelace), you hit the **Emergency Stop** button.
  - This pauses or slows the walkway to allow the problem to be addressed.
  - **The Requirement:** If you hit the stop button, you are **required to explain to everyone else exactly why** you stopped the walkway. You cannot just stop it and walk away.
  - Once the problem is fixed or explained, the walkway restarts.

(4) **Dynamic Timer as Consensus Mechanism:**

(a) **The timer represents deliberation window:**

- Initial timer T set based on decision significance;
- Timer counts down continuously from submission;
- When timer expires, action is authorized (silence = consent);
- **But timer is dynamic** - votes change its duration.

(b) **Votes affect timer duration:**

**(i) APPROVE vote:**

- Signals active support ("I want this to happen faster");
- Reduces deliberation window (accelerates decision);
- Multiple approvals compound effect (strong consensus = rapid resolution).

**((ii) OBJECT vote:**

- Signals concern ("I need more time to think/discuss");
- **Mandatory Reason:** Objector must explain *why* they are objecting (e.g., "Budget is too high," "Need more details," "Conflicts with policy");
- Extends deliberation window (slows decision for discussion);
- Multiple objections compound effect (controversy = longer deliberation);
- **Objection is not veto** - just forces more time for resolution.

(c) **Timer mechanics scale with participation:**

- Timer adjusts based on **participation rate** (votes / pool size);
- Small participation = small effect (prevents tyranny of minority);
- Large participation = large effect (genuine consensus speeds or blocks);
- Pool size naturally affects how many votes needed to trigger gantry.

(4) **Gantry: Final Call Before Resolution:**

(a) **Gantry triggered when timer hits bounds:**

- **Floor (T/2):** Timer reduced to minimum by approvals → Approved Gantry;
- **Ceiling (2T):** Timer extended to maximum by objections → Objection Gantry;
- Gantry is brief final period (last chance to vote before outcome finalizes).

(b) **Approved Gantry (floor):**

- Strong approval participation has accelerated decision;
- Gantry period (default 15 minutes, may scale with T) is final chance to object;
- If objection raised during gantry: Exit gantry, timer recalculated (extends based on new objection);
- If gantry expires: **Decision APPROVED**, action authorized.

(c) **Objection Gantry (ceiling):**

- Strong objection participation has blocked rapid approval;
- Gantry period is final chance to show support;
- If approvals come in: May reduce timer back below ceiling, exit gantry;
- If gantry expires: **Decision BLOCKED**, escalates to Committee for formal discussion.

(d) **Gantry can be cancelled:**

- Opposite votes during gantry may push timer back into normal range;
- Example: Approved Gantry + objection → timer extends above floor → gantry cancelled, deliberation continues;
- Prevents premature finalization if new concerns emerge.

(5) **Objection Escalates, Doesn't Veto:**

(a) **Objection is not unilateral block:**

- One person objecting doesn't kill decision;
- Objection extends time for discussion and resolution;
- If objections dominate (hit ceiling), decision escalates to Committee;
- Committee makes final determination through formal discussion.

(b) **Purpose of objection:**

- Surface concerns early (before decision finalizes);
- Force deliberation on controversial issues;
- Prevent "steamrolling" by small active minority;
- Create space for persuasion, clarification, or revision.

(c) **Resolving objections:**

- Proposer and objector discuss (community space, direct message, facilitated conversation);
- Proposer may revise proposal to address concerns;
- Objector may withdraw objection if satisfied;
- Other members may approve to counterbalance objection;
- If unresolvable, Committee deliberates and decides.

(6) **NCAP Transparency and Accountability:**

(a) **All NCAP activity is logged:**

- Every submission, vote, timer change, outcome recorded;
- Complete audit trail with timestamps;
- Searchable archive for accountability and learning;
- Members can review patterns (who objects to what? what types of decisions move quickly?).

(b) **NCAP activity is visible:**

- Active NCAP requests posted in designated channel (e.g., #ncap-requests);
- Timer displayed in real-time (members can see countdown);
- Votes visible (who approved? who objected?);
- Transparency enables informed participation.

(c) **NCAP enables retrospective review:**

- Committee can analyze NCAP patterns quarterly or annually;
- Identify: What's working? What causes objections? Are timers appropriate?
- Adjust: Timer defaults, approval pools, categories based on evidence;
- Learn: Build institutional wisdom about decision-making.

### Rule 49: NCAP Core Mechanics

(1) **NCAP Authority Scope:**

(a) NCAP is used for decisions that:

- Are within established Working Group or Committee authority;
- Are routine or operational (not strategic or constitutional);
- Can be described clearly in written submission;
- Are reversible or low-risk if problems emerge;
- Benefit from speed over deliberation.

(b) NCAP is NOT used for:

- Constitutional amendments (require General Meeting);
- Major budget allocations beyond delegated authority;
- Termination of membership (requires Committee hearing under Rule 13);
- Decisions explicitly requiring Committee vote or General Meeting approval;
- Matters where law or constitution requires formal process.

(2) **Submitting NCAP Request:**

(a) Any member may submit NCAP authorization request for actions within their scope or for Association-wide decisions under NCAP authority.

(b) **NCAP submission must include:**

- (i) Clear description of what's being authorized;
- (ii) **Designated approver pool** (Working Group and/or Committee);
- (iii) **Proposer cannot be sole approver** - minimum pool size is 2 (independent oversight required);
- (iv) Initial timer duration T (hours until auto-approval if no votes);
- (v) Spending amount if financial authorization;
- (vi) Rationale and Root Axiom alignment if significant decision;
- (vii) Links to relevant documents or context.

(c) **Approver pool selection:**

- Working Group decisions: Approver pool = entire Working Group;
- Cross-WG decisions: Approver pool = relevant Working Groups;
- Association-wide decisions: Approver pool = Committee;
- Committee may expand pool for specific decisions (e.g., add State Branch);
- **Minimum pool size = 2** to maintain independent oversight (no self-approval).

(d) **Timer selection guidance:**

- Urgent/routine: T = 4-12 hours (communications posts, minor expenses);
- Standard: T = 24-48 hours (most operational decisions);
- Significant: T = 48-72 hours (policy drafts, larger spending, important decisions);
- Major: T = 72+ hours (strategic decisions, large budget items);
- Committee may establish default timers by category in Schedule B.

(3) **Dynamic Timer Calculation:**

(a) **Timer adjusts based on participation rate:**

The current timer is calculated continuously as:

```
approval_rate = (number of APPROVE votes) / (approver pool size)
objection_rate = (number of OBJECT votes) / (approver pool size)

timer_modifier = 1 - (0.5 × approval_rate) + (1.0 × objection_rate)

current_timer = initial_timer_T × timer_modifier
```

**(i) Approval effect:**

- Each approval reduces timer by proportion of pool participation;
- Maximum reduction: 50% (if <75% participation);
- **Exception:** 75% Approval triggers **Supermajority Bypass** (Rule 49(5)(e));
- Small pool participation = small reduction (prevents minority rushing decision);
- Large pool participation = large reduction (genuine consensus speeds decision).

**(ii) Objection effect:**

- Each objection extends timer by proportion of pool participation;
- Maximum extension: 100% (if 100% of pool objects, timer = T × 2.0 = ceiling);
- Objections have stronger effect than approvals (bias toward caution);
- Multiple objections can push decision to ceiling (escalation to Committee).

**(iii) Cumulative participation:**

- Timer reflects _current total participation_, not sequence of votes;
- Example: 3 approvals in 5-person pool = 60% approval rate → timer = T × (1 - 0.5×0.6) = T × 0.7;
- Prevents gaming by timing votes strategically;
- Intuitive: "Higher participation = stronger signal."

(b) **Timer bounds (floor and ceiling):**

**(i) Floor = T / 2 (minimum timer):**

- Timer cannot go below half of initial timer due to votes;
- If calculated timer ≤ floor, clamp to floor and trigger **Approved Gantry**;
- Floor prevents instant approval without any deliberation time.

**(ii) Ceiling = 2T (maximum timer):**

- Timer cannot go above double initial timer;
- If calculated timer ≥ ceiling, clamp to ceiling and trigger **Objection Gantry**;
- Ceiling triggers final objection window to see if consensus is salvageable.

(c) **Example calculations:**

**Example 1: Small pool, moderate consensus**

- Pool = 5 members, T = 48 hours
- 2 APPROVE votes (40% participation)
- Timer = 48h × (1 - 0.5×0.4) = 48h × 0.8 = 38.4 hours
- Still above floor (24h), continues countdown.

**Example 2: Large pool, small minority approves**

- Pool = 30 members, T = 72 hours
- 3 APPROVE votes (10% participation)
- Timer = 72h × (1 - 0.5×0.1) = 72h × 0.95 = 68.4 hours
- Minimal effect - needs much more participation to accelerate.

**Example 3: Strong consensus, Supermajority Bypass**

- Pool = 10 members, T = 48 hours
- 8 APPROVE votes (80% participation > 75% threshold)
- **80% Approval triggers Supermajority Bypass (Rule 49(5)(e))**
- **Decision APPROVED IMMEDIATELY** (Timer and Gantry skipped).

**Example 4: Mixed votes (approvals + objections)**

- Pool = 8 members, T = 24 hours
- 4 APPROVE votes (50%), 2 OBJECT votes (25%)
- Timer = 24h × (1 - 0.5×0.5 + 1.0×0.25) = 24h × (1 - 0.25 + 0.25) = 24h × 1.0 = 24 hours
- Net effect: Approvals and objections cancel out (timer unchanged).

**Example 5: Objections dominate, hits ceiling**

- Pool = 6 members, T = 48 hours
- 0 APPROVE, 6 OBJECT votes (100% objection)
- Timer = 48h × (1 + 1.0×1.0) = 48h × 2.0 = 96 hours
- **Hits ceiling → Objection Gantry triggered.**

(4) **Voting on NCAP Requests:**

(a) **Who can vote:**

- Any member of the approver pool can cast one vote;
- Vote options: APPROVE or OBJECT;
- Cannot vote both (choose one);
- Cannot change vote after casting (prevents gaming);
- **Silence is approval** - not voting = implicit consent when timer expires.

(b) **How to vote:**

- Via NCAP system (Discord bot reaction, web form, command);
- Vote recorded with timestamp and member ID;
- Vote immediately recalculates timer (displayed in real-time);
- Voter notified of vote confirmation and new timer value.

(c) **Vote effects:**

- APPROVE: Signals active support, reduces timer based on pool participation;
- OBJECT: Signals concern, extends timer based on pool participation;
- Multiple votes compound effect via participation rate calculation.

(d) **Preventing self-approval:**

- Proposer cannot vote on their own NCAP submission;
- System validates: `if voter_id == proposer_id: raise ValidationError("Cannot vote on own proposal")`;
- Ensures independent oversight (minimum two sets of eyes).

(5) **Gantry Period:**

(a) **Gantry Triggers and Durations:**

**(i) Natural Approval Gantry:**

- Trigger: Timer naturally counts down to **0.25 × T** (25% remaining) without sufficient votes to hit floor;
- Duration: Remaining time (typically 0.25 × T);
- Function: "Final Call" notification to ensure silence is intentional.

**(ii) Voted Approval Gantry:**

- Trigger: Approvals reduce timer to **Floor (0.5 × T)**;
- Duration: Remaining time at floor (0.5 × T);
- Function: Accelerates decision but maintains minimum safety window.

**(iii) Objection Gantry:**

- Trigger: Objections extend timer to **Ceiling (2.0 × T)**;
- Duration: **0.25 × T** (fixed additional window);
- Function: Final chance to resolve objections before escalation.

(b) **Scaling Logic:**

- Standard scaling uses fractions of T (0.25, 0.5) as defined above;
- **Schedule B Flexibility:** Committee may adopt non-linear scaling (e.g., square root functions) in Schedule B to optimize gantry durations for extreme timer values (very short or very long T);
- Principle: Shorter decisions need relative urgency, longer decisions need proportionate warning.

(c) **During Approved Gantry (Natural or Voted):**

- Decision approaching outcome;
- Final chance to raise objections;
- If **APPROVE** vote cast: **Decision APPROVED IMMEDIATELY** (Instant Completion);
- If **OBJECT** vote cast: Timer increases (may exit Gantry state);
- If Gantry expires: **Decision APPROVED**.

(d) **During Objection Gantry:**

- Decision approaching failure (escalation);
- Final chance to show support;
- If **APPROVE** vote cast: Timer reduces (may pull below ceiling, exiting Gantry);
- If **OBJECT** vote cast: **Decision BLOCKED IMMEDIATELY** (Instant Dismissal);
- If Gantry expires: **Decision BLOCKED** (Escalates).

(e) **Supermajority Bypass:**
  - If **>=75% of the Approver Pool** votes APPROVE at any time:
  - Decision is **APPROVED IMMEDIATELY**;
  - Remaining timer and Gantry period are bypassed/cancelled;
  - No "final call" required as overwhelming consensus is established;
  - Action may proceed instantly.

(f) **Gantry Notifications:**

- **"Final Call"** alert when entering any Gantry state;
- Visible countdown with distinct status indicator;
- Notifications sent to non-voting pool members.

(6) **NCAP Outcomes:**

(a) **Automatic approval (timer expires, no gantry):**

- Initial timer T expires with no votes or insufficient votes to trigger gantry;
- Silence = consent, everyone had opportunity to object;
- **Decision APPROVED**, action authorized;
- Logged in NCAP system with outcome timestamp.

(b) **Accelerated approval (Approved Gantry expires):**

- Sufficient approvals reduced timer to floor;
- Gantry period expired with no objections;
- **Decision APPROVED**, action authorized faster than initial timer;
- Logged with approval participation rate and time to resolution.

(c) **Escalation to Committee (Objection Gantry expires):**

- Sufficient objections extended timer to ceiling;
- Gantry period expired with objections still dominating;
- **Decision BLOCKED**, cannot proceed via NCAP;
- Added to next Committee meeting agenda for formal discussion;
- Committee reviews: proposal, objections, discussion, context;
- Committee decides by majority vote whether to approve (possibly with modifications), reject, or request revision.

(d) **Withdrawn by proposer:**

- Proposer may withdraw NCAP request before finalization;
- Useful if objections reveal problems or better approach emerges;
- Withdrawn request logged (not deleted - preserves record for learning);
- Proposer may submit revised request as new NCAP.

### Rule 50: NCAP Financial Authorizations

(1) **NCAP Spending Authority:**

(a) NCAP may authorize expenditures within delegated limits:

- Working Group Coordinators: Up to $X per transaction, $Y per month (amounts set by Committee in Schedule B);
- Committee members: Up to $Z per transaction for their area of responsibility;
- Amounts above limits require Committee approval via meeting vote.

(b) **Financial NCAP requirements:**

- Include exact amount or estimated range;
- Describe what's being purchased and why;
- Provide quote or link if available;
- State budget category (which line item);
- Confirm funds available in budget.

(c) **Receipt and reconciliation:**

- All NCAP-authorized spending requires receipt;
- Receipt uploaded to financial system within 7 days;
- Linked to NCAP authorization ID;
- Treasurer reconciles monthly NCAP spending against budget.

(2) **Financial Approver Pool:**

(a) For Working Group spending within delegated budget:

- Approver pool = entire Working Group;
- WG Coordinator cannot approve own spending (must be approved by other WG members or Committee).

(b) For Committee spending:

- Approver pool = full Committee;
- Spending Committee member cannot approve own expense.

(c) For major expenditures (above NCAP limits):

- Requires formal Committee vote at meeting (not NCAP);
- Or Special General Meeting if exceeds Committee authority.

(3) **Financial NCAP Audit Trail:**

(a) Every financial authorization via NCAP creates audit trail:

- NCAP submission with amount and rationale;
- Approvals/objections with timestamps;
- Final authorization outcome;
- Linked receipt when expenditure made;
- Budget tracking (amount applied to budget line item).

(b) Monthly financial reports include:

- Total NCAP-authorized spending by category;
- Comparison to budget allocations;
- List of individual NCAP financial authorizations;
- Any objections or escalations.

(c) Treasurer and Committee review:

- Treasurer monitors NCAP spending for budget compliance;
- Flags when approaching limits or budget concerns;
- Committee reviews NCAP financial patterns quarterly;
- Adjusts limits, processes, or budgets based on evidence.

### Rule 51: NCAP Categories and Timers

(1) **NCAP Decision Categories:**

To provide guidance and consistency, NCAP requests are categorized by type. Committee maintains category definitions and default timers in Schedule B.

(a) **Example categories:**

**(i) Communications (Urgent):**

- Social media posts, press releases, public statements;
- Default timer: 4-12 hours;
- Approver pool: Communications Working Group;
- Rationale: Time-sensitive, benefits from rapid response.

**(ii) Operations (Routine):**

- Minor expenditures, administrative tasks, routine processes;
- Default timer: 24 hours;
- Approver pool: Relevant Working Group or Committee;
- Rationale: Low-risk, benefits from speed.

**(iii) Policy (Significant):**

- Policy drafts, position papers, strategic documents;
- Default timer: 48-72 hours;
- Approver pool: Policy Working Group or Committee;
- Rationale: Important to get right, warrants deliberation.

**(iv) Financial (Variable):**

- Expenditures within delegated authority;
- Default timer: 24-48 hours depending on amount;
- Approver pool: Working Group or Committee based on amount;
- Rationale: Accountability for spending.

**(v) Governance (Major):**

- Process changes, significant organizational decisions;
- Default timer: 72+ hours;
- Approver pool: Committee;
- Rationale: Affects entire Association, needs broad input.

(b) **Category flexibility:**

- Categories are guidance, not rigid rules;
- Proposer may choose different timer if justified;
- Committee reviews category definitions and timers periodically;
- Updates Schedule B based on experience and feedback.

(2) **Default Timers by Significance:**

(a) Committee establishes default timers in Schedule B as starting point:

- **Urgent:** 4-12 hours (comms, rapid response, time-sensitive opportunities);
- **Routine:** 12-24 hours (minor spending, administrative, standard operations);
- **Standard:** 24-48 hours (most Working Group decisions, moderate spending);
- **Significant:** 48-72 hours (policy, strategy, larger spending, cross-WG coordination);
- **Major:** 72+ hours (governance changes, strategic direction, significant budget).

(b) Proposer selects appropriate timer based on:

- Urgency (how time-sensitive?);
- Significance (how important/risky?);
- Complexity (how much deliberation needed?);
- Controversy (how likely to generate objections?);
- Pool size (larger pools may need longer timers to ensure adequate participation window).

(c) If uncertain, err toward longer timer (allows adequate deliberation, can still accelerate via approvals).

(3) **Adjusting Timers Based on Evidence:**

(a) Committee reviews NCAP patterns quarterly or annually:

- Which categories generate most objections?
- Are timers too short (frequent escalations) or too long (unnecessary delays)?
- Are approver pools appropriate (too narrow/broad)?
- What's the median time-to-resolution by category?

(b) Based on data, Committee may:

- Adjust default timers in Schedule B;
- Refine category definitions;
- Change approver pool recommendations;
- Add new categories or merge existing ones.

(c) Changes to NCAP framework documented and communicated to membership.

### Rule 52: NCAP Limitations and Exclusions

(1) **What NCAP Cannot Authorize:**

The following decisions **cannot** be made via NCAP and require formal Committee vote or General Meeting:

(a) **Constitutional matters:**

- Amendments to Constitution;
- Interpretation of constitutional provisions (Committee decides, but through formal discussion not NCAP);
- Creation or dissolution of structural bodies (new Working Groups may use NCAP, but dissolution requires Committee).

(b) **Membership matters:**

- Termination of membership (requires hearing under Rule 13);
- Discipline beyond warning (suspension, restrictions);
- Challenges to membership eligibility.

(c) **Major financial matters:**

- Budget adoption or major amendments (requires Committee vote);
- Expenditures above NCAP limits (Committee authorization needed);
- Debt, loans, or financial obligations beyond current budget;
- Contracts or agreements committing Association beyond one year.

(d) **Electoral and political:**

- Candidate endorsements (Committee decides through discussion);
- Major electoral strategy decisions (preference deals, coalition agreements);
- Taking controversial political positions not already in policy.

(e) **Legal and compliance:**

- Decisions with legal risk or implications (consult Committee first);
- Responses to legal demands, investigations, or litigation;
- Changes to legal structure (incorporation, registration).

(f) **Personnel (if Association employs staff):**

- Hiring, firing, or discipline of employees;
- Employment contracts or compensation changes;
- Delegation of authority to staff.

(2) **When to Escalate Beyond NCAP:**

(a) Proposer should escalate to Committee (not use NCAP) if:

- Decision is outside established policy or precedent (creates new precedent);
- Decision is controversial and will likely generate objections (save time, go straight to discussion);
- Decision has significant risk or is irreversible;
- Decision affects multiple Working Groups or State Branches and requires coordination;
- Proposer is uncertain whether NCAP is appropriate (ask Committee).

(b) Committee member or Working Group Coordinator may advise proposer to withdraw NCAP and escalate if:

- Request exceeds NCAP authority;
- Request would benefit from formal discussion;
- Context has changed making NCAP inappropriate;
- Legal or compliance concerns arise.

(c) If NCAP request is inappropriate for NCAP, anyone in approver pool can object with reason "Outside NCAP authority" which should trigger escalation.

(3) **Emergency Override:**

(a) In genuine emergencies (safety threats, legal crises, immediate reputational risk), Committee members may act immediately without NCAP:

- Take necessary immediate action;
- Document action and rationale within 24 hours;
- Submit retrospective NCAP or Committee notification explaining emergency action;
- Committee reviews at next meeting to confirm action was appropriate.

(b) Emergency override is for extreme situations, not routine expediency:

- "Emergency" = immediate harm if we wait even 4 hours;
- Not emergency = "this is important and I want to move fast";
- Abuse of emergency override may be grounds for discipline;
- Pattern of emergencies suggests broken processes (fix root cause).

### Rule 54: Affirmative Consent Requirements Affirmative Consent Requirements

(1) **Affirmative Consent Principle:**

(a) Affirmative consent requires **explicit approval** by Committee vote or General Meeting vote before action may be taken.

(b) Silence or inaction is **not** interpreted as approval under affirmative consent.

(c) Affirmative consent ensures democratic deliberation and accountability for high-stakes decisions.

(2) **Matters Requiring Committee Affirmative Consent:**

The following decisions require **Committee vote** with simple majority unless otherwise specified:

(a) **Financial decisions:**

- (i) Expenditures exceeding 60 Fee Units (or Working Group/State Branch NCAP spending limit);
- (ii) Annual budget approval for submission to AGM;
- (iii) Contracts or agreements binding Association;
- (iv) Loans, borrowing, or providing security (requires General Meeting approval under Rule 20(4));
- (v) Major asset purchases or disposals above threshold set by Committee;
- (vi) Remuneration of Committee members, officers, or staff under Rule 21.

(b) **Governance decisions:**

- (i) Establishment, charter modification, or dissolution of Working Groups;
- (ii) Establishment, suspension, or dissolution of State Branches;
- (iii) Appointment of Committee members to fill casual vacancies;
- (iv) Removal of Committee members (requires two-thirds majority under Rule 19);
- (v) Approval of State Branch electoral registration applications (requires two-thirds majority under Rule 30);
- (vi) Modifications to governance infrastructure architecture or NCAP protocol (operational refinements via Committee vote, substantive changes via constitutional amendment);
- (vii) Calling of Special General Meetings.

(c) **Policy and strategy decisions:**

- (i) Adoption or significant modification of major Association policy positions;
- (ii) Strategic plans and multi-year initiatives;
- (iii) Electoral strategy frameworks and candidate endorsements;
- (iv) Partnerships, coalitions, or affiliations with other organizations;
- (v) Public statements on highly controversial or sensitive matters;
- (vi) Responses to major political events or crises.

(d) **Personnel and membership decisions:**

- (i) Hiring of staff or engagement of contractors for significant roles;
- (ii) Termination of staff employment;
- (iii) Member discipline decisions (suspension, termination) under Rule 13 (two-thirds majority for termination);
- (iv) Appeals of membership application rejections.

(e) **Legal and compliance decisions:**

- (i) Initiating or defending legal proceedings;
- (ii) Responses to government inquiries or regulatory actions;
- (iii) Electoral registration applications (state or federal);
- (iv) Compliance breaches or remediation plans;
- (v) Engagement of legal counsel for major matters.

(3) **Matters Requiring General Meeting Affirmative Consent:**

The following decisions require **General Meeting vote** (AGM or SGM):

(a) **Annual approvals at AGM:**

- (i) Annual financial statements and audit/review report;
- (ii) Annual budget for coming year;
- (iii) Election of National Committee;
- (iv) Appointment of auditor or reviewer (if applicable).

(b) **Constitutional amendments:**

- (i) All constitutional amendments under Rule 82, with tiered thresholds:
  - Class 1 (Root Axiom): 90% of 75% of Eligible to Vote members
  - Class 2 (Core Values): 80% supermajority
  - Class 3 (Structural): 66% supermajority
  - Class 4 (Operational): 60% majority

(c) **Major organizational decisions:**

- (i) Borrowing money, giving security, or issuing debentures (special resolution);
- (ii) Dissolution of Association (two-thirds majority under Rule 88);
- (iii) Sale or disposal of major Association assets (if Constitution or law requires);
- (iv) Other matters specifically reserved to General Meeting by this Constitution.

(d) **Member appeals:**

- (i) Appeals of Committee discipline decisions under Rule 13(7);
- (ii) Appeals of other Committee decisions affecting individual member rights.

(4) **Affirmative Consent Process - Committee:**

(a) For Committee affirmative consent decisions:

- (i) Matter must be included on Committee meeting agenda;
- (ii) Supporting papers, analysis, or recommendations circulated in advance;
- (iii) Adequate time allocated for discussion and questions;
- (iv) Motion proposed, seconded, debated, and voted on;
- (v) Result recorded in Committee meeting minutes;
- (vi) Decision documented under Rule 21A.

(b) Affirmative consent may also be sought via:

- (i) **Asynchronous Committee vote** under Rule 43(5) for non-urgent matters that don't require real-time deliberation (48-hour voting window minimum);
- (ii) **Emergency Committee meeting** under Rule 42(5) for urgent matters requiring immediate decision.

(c) Committee members who have conflict of interest must:

- (i) Disclose conflict under Rule 44(3);
- (ii) Abstain from vote;
- (iii) Consider leaving discussion if conflict is significant.

(5) **Affirmative Consent Process - General Meeting:**

(a) For General Meeting affirmative consent decisions:

- (i) Matter must be included in meeting notice (21 days for AGM, 14 days for SGM);
- (ii) Full text of constitutional amendments or major proposals included in notice;
- (iii) Committee analysis or recommendations provided if applicable;
- (iv) Adequate debate time allocated at meeting;
- (v) Vote conducted with appropriate threshold (simple majority, supermajority, or special requirements);
- (vi) Result recorded in meeting minutes and announced to membership.

(b) For constitutional amendments requiring high thresholds (Class 1-2):

- (i) Extended notice period (28 days recommended);
- (ii) Advance electronic voting enabled for members unable to attend;
- (iii) Community space discussion threads for deliberation;
- (iv) Root Axiom impact assessment included in proposal.

(6) **Emergency Affirmative Consent:**

(a) In genuine emergencies where:

- (i) Immediate Committee decision required but Committee cannot convene in time;
- (ii) Waiting for next scheduled meeting would cause significant harm; AND
- (iii) Matter requires affirmative consent (not delegable to NCAP or officer);

The Secretary or Treasurer may seek **emergency affirmative consent** via:

- (i) Email or community space message to all Committee members clearly stating the urgent decision;
- (ii) Request for explicit approval votes within specified short timeframe (minimum 6 hours, preferably 24 hours);
- (iii) Decision proceeds if majority of Committee members affirmatively approve;
- (iv) Decision must be ratified at next Committee meeting;
- (v) Emergency process documented with full justification.

(b) Emergency affirmative consent should be rare and used only for genuine emergencies, not routine convenience.

### Rule 54: Annual Budget Process

(1) **Budget Preparation:**

(a) The Treasurer, in consultation with the Committee, Working Groups, and State Branches, must prepare annual budget for the coming financial year.

(b) Budget preparation process:

- (i) **3-4 months before financial year end:** Treasurer requests budget proposals from Working Groups and State Branches;
- (ii) **2-3 months before year end:** Working Groups and State Branches submit proposed budgets with justification and activity plans;
- (iii) **2 months before year end:** Treasurer consolidates proposals, identifies gaps or conflicts, and prepares draft budget;
- (iv) **6 weeks before year end:** Committee reviews draft budget, provides feedback, requests adjustments;
- (v) **1 month before year end:** Committee approves final budget to present to AGM;
- (vi) **At AGM (within 5 months after year end):** Members vote on budget.

(c) Budget should include:

- (i) **Income projections:** Membership fees, donations, fundraising, grants, other revenue;
- (ii) **Working Group budgets:** Allocation for each Working Group by category;
- (iii) **State Branch budgets:** Allocation for each State Branch (if established);
- (iv) **Central operations:** Committee, governance infrastructure, professional services, insurance;
- (v) **Campaigns:** Electoral campaign budgets if elections planned;
- (vi) **Contingency reserve:** Unallocated funds for emergencies or opportunities (recommended 10-20% of budget);
- (vii) **Capital expenditures:** Major purchases or investments if planned.

(2) **Budget Approval:**

(a) Annual budget must be approved by **Eligible to Vote members** at AGM by simple majority vote.

(b) Budget should be:

- (i) Included in AGM notice and materials (21 days advance);
- (ii) Accompanied by Treasurer explanation and Committee recommendation;
- (iii) Available for member questions and discussion at AGM;
- (iv) Voted on as single motion (unless members request line-item votes).

(c) If budget is not approved:

- (i) Committee must revise based on member feedback;
- (ii) Revised budget presented at next General Meeting (SGM if necessary);
- (iii) In interim, spending continues at prior year's levels on monthly basis (1/12 of prior budget per month) for essential operations only.

(3) **Budget Amendments:**

(a) Committee may amend approved budget during the year by Committee vote if:

- (i) Circumstances change significantly (unexpected income, emergencies, opportunities);
- (ii) Budget line reallocation needed (moving funds between categories);
- (iii) Total budget increase is less than 20% of approved budget.

(b) Budget amendments exceeding 20% of total budget require:

- (i) Explanation to members via community space and email;
- (ii) Approval at next General Meeting (AGM or SGM).

(c) All budget amendments must be:

- (i) Documented with rationale;
- (ii) Reported to next AGM;
- (iii) Included in annual financial statements.

(4) **Budget Monitoring:**

(a) Treasurer must monitor budget performance monthly and report to Committee on:

- (i) Income vs. projections;
- (ii) Expenditure vs. budget by category;
- (iii) Working Group and State Branch spending vs. allocations;
- (iv) Variance explanations (why over/under budget);
- (v) Forecast for year-end based on current trends;
- (vi) Recommendations for corrective action if needed.

(b) Committee should:

### Rule 60: Policy Development Framework

(1) **Policy Development Principles:**

(b) Policy development is **collaborative and iterative**, involving:

- (i) Policy Working Group research and drafting;
- (ii) Community discussion and feedback in community space;
- (iii) Consultation with affected communities and subject matter experts;
- (iv) Committee review and refinement;
- (v) Member input and democratic oversight;
- (vi) Continuous evaluation and revision based on evidence and outcomes.

(2) **Policy Working Group Responsibilities:**

(a) The Policy Working Group, established under Rule 22(3)(a)(ii), is responsible for:

- (i) Researching policy issues and identifying systemic problems;
- (ii) Developing policy proposals aligned with Root Axiom and Association principles;
- (iii) Conducting Root Axiom Impact Assessments for proposed policies;
- (iv) Consulting with affected communities, experts, and stakeholders;
- (v) Drafting policy position documents and explanatory materials;
- (vi) Presenting policy proposals to Committee for approval;
- (vii) Maintaining policy register and tracking policy review schedules;
- (viii) Coordinating with Communications Working Group on policy messaging;
- (ix) Responding to emerging policy issues and political developments.

(b) Policy Working Group operates under NCAP for:

- (i) Research projects and background papers;
- (ii) Draft policy development and community consultation;
- (iii) Minor policy refinements and updates;
- (iv) Routine policy analysis and briefings.

(c) Policy Working Group must escalate to Committee for affirmative approval:

- (i) Adoption of new major policy positions;
- (ii) Significant modifications to existing policies;
- (iii) Policy positions with substantial electoral or strategic implications;
- (iv) Controversial policies likely to generate internal or external debate;
- (v) Policy positions requiring substantial resource commitments.

(3) **Root Axiom Impact Assessment:**

(a) Every policy proposal must include **Root Axiom Impact Assessment** analyzing:

**(i) Inefficiency Analysis:**

- What systemic inefficiencies does this policy address?
- What inefficiencies might this policy create?
- Are there more efficient alternatives?
- What is the administrative burden and complexity?
- What are opportunity costs?

**(ii) Suffering Analysis:**

- Whose suffering does this policy reduce or prevent?
- What unnecessary suffering currently exists that this addresses?
- Could this policy create new suffering or burdens?
- How are impacts distributed across different communities?
- What are second-order effects on wellbeing?

**(iii) Evidence Assessment:**

- What empirical evidence supports this approach?
- What have other jurisdictions or programs demonstrated?
- What research, data, or case studies inform this policy?
- What assumptions are we making and are they valid?
- How will we measure success or failure?
- What are alternative approaches and their evidence base?

**(iv) Value Gates Application:**

- **Transparency:** Does policy increase openness and accountability or create opacity?
- **Evidence:** Is policy grounded in data and research or ideology and assumption?
- **Efficiency:** Does policy minimize waste and maximize impact per resource unit?
- **Adaptability:** Does policy include feedback mechanisms, review provisions, sunset clauses?
- **Fairness:** Does policy reduce unjust inequality and expand opportunity?

(b) Root Axiom Impact Assessment should be:

- (i) Written in accessible language (avoiding jargon where possible);
- (ii) Honest about trade-offs and limitations;
- (iii) Transparent about uncertainties and assumptions;
- (iv) Realistic about implementation challenges;
- (v) Published with policy proposal for member review.

(4) **Policy Approval Process:**

(a) **For new major policy positions:**

**(i) Development phase:**

- Policy Working Group researches issue and develops draft proposal;
- Draft includes policy position, rationale, Root Axiom Impact Assessment, implementation considerations;
- Draft posted to community space for member discussion (minimum 14 days);
- Consultation with affected communities and experts conducted;
- Feedback incorporated and draft revised.

**(ii) Approval phase:**

- Revised proposal submitted to Committee with summary of consultation feedback;
- Committee reviews proposal at meeting with adequate discussion time;
- Committee may approve, request revisions, or reject by majority vote;
- Approved policies added to official policy register;
- Policy announcement coordinated with Communications Working Group.

**(iii) Implementation phase:**

- Relevant Working Groups (Campaigns, Communications, State Branches) develop messaging and advocacy strategy;
- Policy incorporated into electoral materials, candidate briefings, public communications;
- Policy implementation monitored and evaluated;
- Policy reviewed according to schedule under Rule 62.

(b) **For minor policy updates or refinements:**

- Policy Working Group may make minor clarifications or updates via NCAP;
- Substantive changes require Committee approval;
- All changes documented and published to policy register.

(c) **For emergency or time-sensitive policy positions:**

- Policy Working Group develops rapid response policy brief;
- Committee may approve via emergency meeting or asynchronous vote under Rule 54(6);
- Full Root Axiom Impact Assessment may be conducted retrospectively if time constraints prevent prior completion;
- Emergency policy positions subject to review within 90 days.

(5) **Policy Register and Accessibility:**

(a) The Association must maintain **public policy register** including:

- (i) All current policy positions with full text;
- (ii) Root Axiom Impact Assessments;

- (v) Status (active, under review, sunset, superseded);
- (vi) Version history and change log.

- (i) Published on Association website;
- (ii) Accessible to all members and public;
- (iii) Searchable by topic, issue area, date;
- (iv) Maintained by Policy Working Group with Secretary oversight;
- (v) Updated within 7 days of any policy change.

(c) Policy documents should be:

- (i) Written in accessible language for general public;
- (ii) Formatted consistently for easy navigation;
- (iii) Include executive summaries for longer policies;
- (iv) Link to supporting research and evidence;
- (v) Provide contact for questions or feedback.

### Rule 61: Ideological Framework and Policy Principles

(1) **Libertarian Socialist Foundation:**

(a) The Association's ideological framework synthesizes **libertarian** and **socialist** principles to maximize both individual freedom and collective security.

(b) **Libertarian elements:**

- (i) **Individual freedom and autonomy:** Policies maximize personal choice, bodily autonomy, and self-determination;
- (ii) **Minimal bureaucracy:** Universal programs eliminate surveillance, means-testing, and administrative overhead;
- (iii) **Decentralized governance:** NCAP and distributed decision-making rather than hierarchical control;
- (iv) **Voluntary association:** No compulsory participation, diversity mandates, or quotas;
- (v) **Civil liberties:** Strong protections for free speech, privacy, due process, and personal autonomy;
- (vi) **Market efficiency:** Markets allowed to operate where they function well, with democratic oversight of natural monopolies and essential services.

(c) **Socialist elements:**

- (i) **Economic security for all:** Universal Basic Income, healthcare, housing, education ensuring material needs met;
- (ii) **Collective provision:** Universal services funded collectively (progressive taxation) and provided free at point of use;
- (iii) **Systemic justice:** Address root causes of inequality (economic systems, colonialism, structural discrimination) not just symptoms;
- (iv) **Democratic ownership:** Worker cooperatives, public ownership of natural monopolies, community control of essential infrastructure;
- (v) **Solidarity and mutual aid:** "From each according to ability, to each according to need" via redistribution through taxation and universal services;
- (vi) **Environmental stewardship:** Collective responsibility for ecological sustainability and climate justice.

(d) **Synthesis:**

The Association rejects false dichotomy between freedom and security:

- (i) **Universal floor enables freedom:** UBI and universal services provide secure foundation from which individuals can exercise genuine choice;
- (ii) **Efficient collective provision reduces bureaucracy:** Universal programs eliminate means-testing surveillance that restricts freedom;
- (iii) **Democratic control protects autonomy:** Public ownership of essential services prevents corporate exploitation while preserving choice;
- (iv) **Markets within boundaries:** Economic freedom for entrepreneurship and innovation, democratic constraints on exploitation and externalities;
- (v) **Individual and collective thrive together:** Personal autonomy and social solidarity are complementary, not contradictory.

(2) **Raise the Floor, Don't Lower the Ceiling:**

(a) **Core principle:**

Association policy prioritizes **raising the floor for disadvantaged people** rather than reducing advantages of those already secure.

(b) **Floor-raising approach:**

- (i) **Universal Basic Income:** Ensures everyone has adequate income regardless of employment, ability, or circumstances;
- (ii) **Universal healthcare:** Medicare-for-all ensuring comprehensive healthcare access without cost barriers;
- (iii) **Public housing abundance:** Sufficient quality public/social housing that housing insecurity becomes impossible;
- (iv) **Free public transport:** Excellent public transport making car ownership optional choice not necessity;
- (v) **Universal education:** Free high-quality education from early childhood through tertiary and lifelong learning;
- (vi) **Environmental sustainability:** Clean air, water, livable climate as universal right not luxury.

(c) **Ceiling-lowering approaches to avoid:**

- (i) Wealth caps, maximum income limits, confiscatory taxation on success;
- (ii) Banning or severely restricting private schools, healthcare, or services;
- (iii) Forced redistribution that punishes achievement rather than addressing systemic barriers;
- (iv) Rent control without building abundant housing (restricts supply);
- (v) Maximum CEO pay ratios without ensuring worker bargaining power;
- (vi) Property ownership limits without providing universal housing security.

(d) **Rationale - Root Axiom alignment:**

- (i) **Floor-raising eliminates suffering** of poverty, insecurity, and deprivation without creating new suffering for those currently secure;
- (ii) **Ceiling-lowering creates resentment** and political backlash, making change harder;
- (iii) **Floor-raising is politically durable** (broad coalition supports universal benefits);
- (iv) **Ceiling-lowering invites capital flight** and economic disruption;
- (v) **Floor-raising solves actual problems** (poverty, insecurity) rather than attacking success;
- (vi) **Floor-raising enables positive-sum economy** where everyone can thrive.

(e) **Exceptions where restrictions may be justified:**

- (i) **Negative externalities:** Carbon pricing, pollution taxes (those causing harm should pay);
- (ii) **Monopoly power:** Breaking up monopolies that harm consumers and innovation;
- (iii) **Rent-seeking:** Eliminating unearned income from land speculation (Land Value Tax), IP monopolies, regulatory capture;
- (iv) **Political corruption:** Campaign finance reform, lobbying restrictions to reduce wealthy capture of democracy;
- (v) **Public goods:** Democratic control of natural monopolies (water, electricity, transport infrastructure) to prevent exploitation.

Even these should be framed as **removing obstacles to universal flourishing** rather than punishing success.

(3) **Universal Over Means-Tested:**

(a) **Universal programs are superior to means-tested programs:**

**(i) Administrative efficiency:**

- No eligibility determination infrastructure (everyone qualifies by default);
- No ongoing compliance monitoring or reporting;
- No fraud investigation or enforcement bureaucracy;
- Dramatically lower administrative costs (UBI: ~1% vs Centrelink: ~5-8%).

**(ii) Reduced suffering:**

- No stigma of receiving "welfare" or "handouts";
- No intrusive surveillance or reporting requirements;
- No risk of losing benefits due to bureaucratic errors;
- No mutual obligations or forced compliance;
- No welfare cliffs (poverty traps where earning income loses more in benefits).

**(iii) Political durability:**

- Universal programs have broad support (everyone benefits, everyone defends);
- Means-tested programs vulnerable to cuts (only beneficiaries defend);
- Example: Medicare (universal) is politically untouchable; Newstart/JobSeeker (means-tested) is constantly inadequate and attacked.

**(iv) Social cohesion:**

- Universal programs unite rather than divide (shared social infrastructure);
- Means-tested programs create resentment ("my taxes supporting those people");
- Universal framing: "We all contribute, we all benefit when needed."

(b) **Policy preference hierarchy:**

**(i) First preference - Universal:**

- UBI, Medicare, public education, public transport, public housing;
- Everyone receives benefit regardless of income, employment, or status;
- Funded through progressive taxation (wealthy pay more, everyone benefits).

**(ii) Second preference - Progressive universal:**

- Services are universal but contribution is progressive (e.g., HECS - everyone can access, repayment based on income);
- Maintains universality while ensuring fair contribution.

**(iii) Third preference - Categorical (avoid if possible):**

- Benefits targeted to specific groups (disability support, First Nations services);
- Justified only where universal approach would dilute impact or fail to address specific needs;
- Should still minimize means-testing and administrative burden.

**(iv) Last resort - Means-tested (avoid):**

- Benefits dependent on income/asset testing;
- Only acceptable where universal or categorical approaches genuinely unworkable;
- Should include pathway to transitioning to universal model.

(c) **Examples of universal policy approach:**

| Issue          | Means-Tested Approach (Avoid)                                               | Universal Approach (Prefer)                                                                     |
| -------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Income support | Newstart/JobSeeker with mutual obligations, compliance, means testing       | Universal Basic Income - everyone receives regardless of employment or income                   |
| Healthcare     | Medicare with means-tested gap payments, private health rebate carrot/stick | Universal Medicare covering dental, mental health, allied health - free at point of use for all |
| Housing        | Public housing waitlists, income limits, priority allocations               | Public housing abundance - anyone can access quality public housing, wealthy just choose not to |
| Transport      | Concession cards for low-income, seniors, students                          | Free or very cheap public transport for everyone, excellent service making cars optional        |
| Education      | HECS with income thresholds, Youth Allowance means testing                  | Free tertiary education for all, living allowance (UBI) for students                            |
| Childcare      | Subsidies based on family income, activity test                             | Universal free childcare/early learning for all families                                        |

(4) **Abundance Over Scarcity:**

(a) **Abundance mindset:**

The Association operates from assumption that **sufficient resources exist** if systems are efficient and fair:

- (i) Australia is one of wealthiest nations per capita in human history;
- (ii) GDP per capita sufficient to provide comfortable life for everyone if distributed efficiently;
- (iii) Technological productivity means less labor required for basic needs;
- (iv) Current poverty and insecurity are **policy choices**, not resource scarcity;
- (v) Evidence from Nordic countries and other jurisdictions proves universal programs are feasible and sustainable.

(b) **Scarcity mindset to reject:**

**(i) False scarcity narratives:**

- "We can't afford UBI/Medicare/public housing" (we can, it's about priorities);
- "There's not enough to go around" (there is, if distributed efficiently);
- "We must choose between quality services and affordability" (false dichotomy);
- "Welfare makes people lazy" (evidence shows opposite - security enables productivity).

**(ii) Zero-sum thinking:**

- "Your gain is my loss" (positive-sum economy possible);
- "Helping poor requires hurting rich" (universal floor raises all boats);
- "We must ration and restrict to ensure fairness" (abundance allows universal access).

(c) **Abundance enables generosity:**

- (i) Universal programs reflect belief that everyone deserves security and dignity;
- (ii) Collaborative rather than competitive approach to resource distribution;
- (iii) Investment mindset (spend now on UBI/education/health, gain productivity/wellbeing/reduced costs later);
- (iv) Long-term sustainability through prevention rather than expensive crisis intervention.

(5) **Equity of Opportunity, Not Equality of Outcome:**

(a) **Core distinction:**

**(i) Equity of opportunity (support):**

- Everyone has access to resources needed to thrive (UBI, education, healthcare, housing);
- Barriers to participation removed (cost, geography, disability, discrimination);
- Support provided based on need (disability accommodations, First Nations services addressing intergenerational trauma);
- Individual choice and agency respected within secure foundation.

**(ii) Equality of outcome (reject):**

- Mandating demographic representation through quotas;
- Forcing identical outcomes regardless of individual choices or circumstances;
- Punishing success or achievement to create statistical parity;
- Assuming all disparities are result of discrimination (ignoring agency, choice, cultural differences).

(b) **Application to diversity:**

**(i) No diversity mandates or quotas:**

- Association does not set targets for membership composition (ethnicity, gender, age, sexuality, disability);
- Association does not require Committee, Working Groups, or candidates meet demographic quotas;
- Association does not implement affirmative action or preferential selection based on identity.

**(ii) No barriers to participation:**

- Membership open to all who support Root Axiom and Association purposes;
- No restrictions based on demographics;
- Accessibility accommodations provided (disability access, language support, financial access via Free Membership);
- Welcoming culture with zero tolerance for harassment or discrimination.

**(iii) Natural diversity reflects genuine inclusion:**

- If Association creates genuinely welcoming environment and policies address systemic barriers, diverse people will join;
- If Association fails to attract diverse membership, this signals need to examine policies and culture, not manipulate composition;
- Diversity is **outcome** of justice/equity/inclusion, not **goal** to be engineered.

**(iv) Homogeneity is not inherently bad:**

- Political parties exist to aggregate people with shared values and policy goals;
- Ideological coherence is strength (clear vision, consistent messaging);
- Better to be homogeneous and effective than diverse and incoherent;
- Association may naturally attract particular demographics based on policy alignment, communication channels, existing networks - this is acceptable.

(c) **Root Axiom alignment:**

- **Diversity quotas create suffering:** Tokenism (stigma, isolation), resentment (qualifications questioned), reduced autonomy;
- **Diversity quotas create inefficiency:** Resources spent on demographic tracking rather than removing actual barriers;
- **Equity of opportunity eliminates both:** Remove barriers, provide resources, respect choices.

### Rule 62: Policy Review and Sunset Provisions

(1) **Continuous Policy Evaluation:**

(a) All Association policies are subject to regular review and evaluation to ensure:

- (i) Continued alignment with Root Axiom and Association principles;
- (ii) Evidence base remains current and valid;
- (iii) Policy remains effective in addressing identified problems;
- (iv) Unintended consequences identified and addressed;
- (v) Policy adapted to changing circumstances and new evidence.

(b) This reflects **Adaptability** value gate: systems must incorporate feedback mechanisms and iterative improvement.

(2) **Policy Review Schedule:**

(a) All policies must be reviewed according to the following schedule:

**(i) Major policy positions:** Reviewed every **3 years** from adoption date;

**(ii) Electoral/campaign policies:** Reviewed after each election cycle where policy was active;

**(iii) Time-sensitive policies:** Reviewed annually or when triggering events occur;

**(iv) Operational/administrative policies:** Reviewed every **5 years** or as needed.

(b) Review schedule published in policy register and tracked by Policy Working Group.

(c) Overdue reviews flagged in Policy Working Group monthly reports to Committee.

(3) **Policy Review Process:**

(a) Policy Working Group conducts review including:

- (i) Assessment of whether policy achieved intended outcomes;
- (ii) Evidence review (new research, data, case studies from other jurisdictions);
- (iii) Feedback from affected communities, experts, members;
- (iv) Analysis of unintended consequences or implementation challenges;
- (v) Consideration of alternative approaches;
- (vi) Updated Root Axiom Impact Assessment;
- (vii) Recommendation to renew unchanged, modify, or sunset policy.

(b) Review report presented to Committee with recommendation.

(c) Committee decides by majority vote to:

- (i) **Renew policy unchanged** (resets review clock);
- (ii) **Modify policy** (amendments require approval per Rule 60(4));
- (iii) **Sunset policy** (remove from active policy register, archive);
- (iv) **Defer decision** and request additional analysis.

(4) **Automatic Sunset Provisions:**

(a) Policies automatically sunset (become inactive) if:

- (i) Review is **more than 12 months overdue** and Policy Working Group has not provided extension justification;
- (ii) Policy includes **explicit sunset date** that has passed;
- (iii) Policy is **superseded** by newer policy addressing same issue;
- (iv) **Evidence base is invalidated** by new research (Committee determination required).

(b) Sunset policies are:

- (i) Moved to "archived" status in policy register;
- (ii) No longer active Association positions;
- (iii) Not removed entirely (historical record maintained);
- (iv) May be revived if circumstances change.

(c) **Purpose of automatic sunset:**

- Prevents policy cruft and accumulation of outdated positions;
- Forces active renewal and re-evaluation;
- Ensures policies remain evidence-based and current;
- Reflects Adaptability value gate.

(5) **Emergency Policy Suspension:**

(a) Committee may **temporarily suspend** active policy by two-thirds majority vote if:

- (i) New evidence emerges contradicting policy's evidence base;
- (ii) Policy implementation causing unforeseen harm;
- (iii) Circumstances have changed making policy inappropriate;
- (iv) Legal or electoral compliance issues arise.

(b) Suspended policy:

- (i) Marked "under review" in policy register;
- (ii) Not actively advocated or communicated;
- (iii) Subject to expedited review within 60 days;
- (iv) Reinstated, modified, or sunset based on review.

(c) Suspension must be transparent:

- (i) Announced to membership with rationale;
- (ii) Review process and timeline communicated;
- (iii) Outcome of review reported publicly.

### Rule 63: First Nations Engagement and JEDI Framework

(1) **Consultation Over Symbolism:**

(a) The Association's approach to First Nations engagement is informed by **direct consultation with First Nations community representatives** rather than prescriptive symbolic gestures.

(b) The Association reserves Acknowledgements of Country and Welcomes to Country for:

- (i) Direct personal interactions (meetings, events, gatherings) where expression is in keeping with intended spirit;
- (ii) Contexts where First Nations representatives have indicated appropriateness;
- (iii) Situations where acknowledgement is meaningful rather than performative.

(c) The Association does **not** mandate Acknowledgements of Country for:

- (i) Written documents or website content (where acknowledgement becomes static text divorced from personal connection);
- (ii) Automated or templated communications;
- (iii) Contexts where First Nations consultation indicates acknowledgement may be inappropriate or tokenistic.

(2) **Avoiding Tokenism - Root Axiom Application:**

(a) **Tokenism creates suffering:**

- (i) Empty gestures without substantive action harm First Nations communities by suggesting respect while maintaining inequality;
- (ii) "Acknowledge then ignore" approaches create cynicism and undermine genuine reconciliation;
- (iii) First Nations people experience frustration with performative recognition.

(b) **Consultation eliminates inefficiency:**

- (i) Asking First Nations representatives what they actually want is more effective than assuming universal preferences;
- (ii) Different First Nations communities may have different protocols and preferences;
- (iii) One-size-fits-all approaches ignore diversity of First Nations cultures.

(c) **Meaningful action over performative gestures:**

- (i) Association prioritizes substantive policy positions addressing systemic inequality;
- (ii) Resources spent on symbolic gestures redirected to material change;
- (iii) First Nations communities benefit more from policy advocacy than acknowledgement text.

(3) **JEDI Over DEI:**

(a) The Association adopts **JEDI (Justice, Equity, Diversity, Inclusion)** framework rather than DEI:

**DEI (Diversity, Equity, Inclusion) limitations:**

- Focuses on representation and inclusive spaces;
- May not address root causes of inequality;
- Can become performative (diverse photos without structural change);
- Often satisfied with surface-level metrics (headcount, quotas).

**JEDI (Justice, Equity, Diversity, Inclusion) advantages:**

- Adds **Justice** as foundational element;
- Actively dismantles oppressive systems;
- Focuses on structural change rather than optics;
- Measures impact on material conditions, not just representation.

(b) **Root Axiom alignment:**

- (i) **Justice** identifies and eliminates systemic sources of suffering (oppressive structures, policies, power imbalances);
- (ii) **Equity** eliminates unnecessary suffering by addressing unequal access to resources and opportunities;
- (iii) **Diversity** is outcome of justice and equity, not goal in itself;
- (iv) **Inclusion** means meaningful participation in decision-making, not just presence.

(c) **JEDI application to First Nations policy:**

- (i) **Justice:** Support constitutional recognition, truth-telling, treaty processes, land rights, sovereignty;
- (ii) **Equity:** Address gaps in health, education, employment, housing, incarceration through systemic policy change;
- (iii) **Diversity:** First Nations perspectives integrated into policy development, not consulted as afterthought;
- (iv) **Inclusion:** First Nations people in leadership and decision-making roles.

(4) **Substantive First Nations Policy Positions:**

(a) The Association demonstrates respect through **policy** addressing systemic inequality:

- (i) Constitutional recognition with substantive power (not just symbolic preamble);
- (ii) Truth-telling and treaty processes at federal and state levels;
- (iii) Raise Age of Criminal Responsibility to at least 14 (addressing disproportionate youth incarceration);
- (iv) Housing and homelessness initiatives addressing First Nations overrepresentation;
- (v) Land rights and native title reform;
- (vi) Health equity (closing the gap with adequate funding and First Nations-led services);
- (vii) Education equity and culturally appropriate curricula;
- (viii) Economic empowerment through Indigenous procurement and business support;
- (ix) Environmental justice (First Nations leadership in land and water management).

(b) These policies address **root causes of suffering** rather than symptoms.

(5) **Avoiding Performative Anti-Racism:**

(a) The Association is explicitly **against**:

- (i) Diversity quotas without addressing why diversity is lacking (tokenism);
- (ii) Unconscious bias training as substitute for structural change (individual focus rather than systemic);
- (iii) Rainbow-washing or virtue signaling (appearing progressive without substantive policy);
- (iv) Celebration of diversity while maintaining inequitable systems;
- (v) Box-ticking compliance with diversity metrics without material improvement.

(b) The Association is explicitly **for**:

- (i) Systemic analysis of why inequality exists (power structures, resource distribution, policy frameworks);
- (ii) Structural reforms that redistribute power and resources;
- (iii) Accountability mechanisms measuring material outcomes, not inputs;
- (iv) Community-led solutions where affected communities design and implement changes;
- (v) Evidence-based policy grounded in data on actual harm and inequality.

(6) **Consultation and Engagement Process:**

(a) When developing policy affecting First Nations communities, the Association must:

- (i) Consult directly with First Nations representatives and organizations;
- (ii) Prioritize First Nations voices in policy formation;
- (iii) **Compensate First Nations advisors** for their time and expertise (labor should not be extracted for free);
- (iv) Defer to First Nations leadership on matters primarily affecting their communities;
- (v) Build ongoing relationships rather than one-off consultations.

(b) Consultation is **not**:

- (i) A checkbox to legitimize pre-determined policy;
- (ii) Extracting knowledge without compensation or credit;
- (iii) Selecting only voices that agree with pre-existing position;
- (iv) Performative engagement without genuine openness to changing course.

(c) Consultation **is**:

- (i) Genuinely seeking guidance and expertise;
- (ii) Being willing to change or abandon policy based on feedback;
- (iii) Ongoing relationship-building and learning;
- (iv) Recognizing First Nations people as experts on their own experiences and needs.

(7) **Diversity as Outcome, Not Mandate:**

(a) **No diversity quotas or targets:**

The Association does **not** set diversity quotas, targets, or mandates for:

- (i) Membership composition (ethnicity, age, gender, sexuality, disability);
- (ii) Committee composition;
- (iii) Working Group membership;
- (iv) Candidate selection.

(b) **No barriers ≠ guaranteed diversity:**

The Association acknowledges that:

- (i) Source Party membership may skew toward certain demographics based on natural alignment;
- (ii) This is natural sorting based on policy alignment, communication channels, cultural fit, existing networks;
- (iii) Lack of demographic diversity does **not** indicate discrimination if barriers have been removed;
- (iv) Forcing demographic diversity through quotas would be tokenistic and contrary to principles.

(c) **What matters is absence of barriers:**

The Association ensures:

- (i) No membership restrictions based on demographics;
- (ii) No cultural gatekeeping or unstated norms that exclude;
- (iii) Accessible participation (online/hybrid, financial via Free Membership, disability accommodations);
- (iv) Welcoming culture (respectful, zero tolerance for harassment/discrimination);
- (v) Policy alignment is only criterion (support Root Axiom and Association purposes).

(d) **Diversity as signal, not goal:**

**(i) If Association naturally attracts diverse membership:**

- Suggests policies resonate across communities;
- Indicates inclusive culture is working;
- Demonstrates barriers successfully removed.

**(ii) If Association fails to attract specific communities:**

- May signal policies don't address their priorities;
- May indicate cultural barriers still exist;
- May suggest communication not reaching them;
- May reflect those communities' strategic choices.

**(iii) Appropriate response:**

- Examine whether policies address underrepresented communities' concerns;
- Review whether cultural norms create barriers;
- Seek feedback and remove identified barriers;
- **Do not** set quotas or recruit "token" members for optics.

(e) **Homogeneity is not inherently bad:**

- Political parties exist to aggregate people with shared values;
- Ideological coherence is strength (clear vision, consistent messaging);
- Better to be homogeneous and effective than diverse and incoherent;
- Association may naturally attract particular demographics - this is acceptable feature, not bug.

(f): Genuine Care Without Performativity

(a) The Association's approach to inclusion is:

- (i) **We care** about members' experiences, identities, and challenges;
- (ii) **We see** differences and how they shape lived experiences;
- (iii) **We support** members through barriers they face;
- (iv) **But we don't make it a thing** (no performative celebration, no tokenism, no special treatment that signals "you're different");
- (v) **We just include** (everyone belongs here if they support Root Axiom).

(b) This means:

- (i) **Listen** when members share experiences of discrimination or barriers;
- (ii) **Act** to remove barriers and support members;
- (iii) **Advocate** for policies addressing systemic inequality;
- (iv) **Don't** make a big deal about someone's identity as if it's novel or requires special accommodation beyond removing genuine barriers;
- (v) **Don't** collect diversity statistics or celebrate demographic composition;
- (vi) **Do** celebrate policy wins that reduce suffering for everyone, including marginalized communities.

(c) **Root Axiom alignment:**

- Caring about members' experiences eliminates suffering (isolation, discrimination, exclusion);
- Removing barriers eliminates inefficiency (people can contribute fully);
- Avoiding performativity eliminates suffering (tokenism, "diversity hire" stigma);
- Universal policies eliminate both (systemic solutions rather than identity-targeted bandaids).

(8) **Intersectionality Without Identity Politics:**

(a) The Association recognizes **intersectionality** (people experience multiple overlapping systems of oppression) without embracing identity politics:

**Intersectionality (acknowledge):**

- People face multiple intersecting barriers (racism + sexism, ableism + poverty, etc.);
- Experiences compound and interact in complex ways;
- Policy should address systems creating suffering, recognizing differential impacts.

**Identity politics (avoid):**

- Defining people primarily by demographic categories;
- Assuming all members of identity group share same politics;
- Creating hierarchy of oppression;
- Focusing on representation over material change;
- Fragmenting solidarity into competing identity claims.

(b) **Source Party approach - focus on systems, not identities:**

**(i) Universal solutions with differentiated benefits:**

- UBI benefits everyone but lifts most those furthest from secure floor;
- Universal public transport benefits everyone but frees most those currently transport-poor;
- Universal healthcare benefits everyone but helps most those currently unable to afford care.

**(ii) No need to target by identity:**

- Systems-focused policies automatically help those most harmed;
- Universal programs avoid stigma and division;
- Broad coalitions easier to build around universal benefits.

## PART 11: ELECTIONS AND CAMPAIGNS

### Rule 64: Electoral Strategy and Priorities

(1) **Electoral Objectives:**

(a) The Association participates in electoral politics to:

- (i) Advance the Root Axiom by electing candidates who will implement policies eliminating systemic inefficiency and unnecessary suffering;
- (ii) Build public awareness of Association policy positions and values;
- (iii) Shift political discourse toward evidence-based, systems-focused solutions;
- (iv) Demonstrate viability of libertarian socialist principles in practice;
- (v) Gain platform to advocate for universal programs and floor-raising policies;
- (vi) Hold major parties accountable by providing credible alternative.

(b) Electoral success is measured by:

- (i) **Primary:** Policy influence and discourse shift (changing conversation, forcing major parties to adopt positions);
- (ii) **Secondary:** Vote share and electoral performance (building support base, demonstrating momentum);
- (iii) **Tertiary:** Seats won and direct legislative power (ideal outcome, but not only measure of success);
- (iv) **Long-term:** Building sustainable political movement capable of governing.

(c) The Association recognizes that:

- (i) Electoral breakthrough takes time (years to decades, not single cycles);
- (ii) Early campaigns are primarily about building brand, testing messages, recruiting talent;
- (iii) Losing elections can still advance Root Axiom if they shift discourse or build capacity;
- (iv) Electoral success without governing capacity is hollow (must build organizational depth).

(2) **Strategic Approach:**

(a) **Build from strong foundations:**

- (i) Contest seats where Association has genuine community connections and organizing base;
- (ii) Prioritize quality over quantity (one well-resourced campaign better than ten underfunded);
- (iii) Target winnable seats or high-visibility opportunities (avoid spreading too thin);
- (iv) Invest in building lasting relationships in electorates, not just campaign-period presence.

(b) **Evidence-based targeting:**

- (i) Analyze electoral data to identify opportunities (demographics, previous results, major party vulnerabilities);
- (ii) Assess Association capacity honestly (membership, volunteers, funds, local networks);
- (iii) Consider preferential voting dynamics (where can Association win preferences?);
- (iv) Evaluate strategic value beyond winning (high-profile races, marginal seats, building blocks for future).

(c) **Long-term movement building:**

- (i) Every campaign builds infrastructure for next campaign (data, volunteers, donor base, brand recognition);
- (ii) Candidates who lose remain engaged (continue organizing, build toward next attempt);
- (iii) Electoral cycles are part of continuous organizing, not isolated events;
- (iv) Success in local government can build toward state, state toward federal.

(3) **Balancing Pragmatism and Principle:**

(a) **The Association will not:**

- (i) Abandon core policy positions for electoral convenience;
- (ii) Make false promises or commit to policies not aligned with Root Axiom;
- (iii) Engage in personal attacks, misinformation, or unethical campaign tactics;
- (iv) Accept donations or support that compromise independence or values;
- (v) Preference major parties over independent or minor party candidates aligned with Association values.

(b) **The Association will:**

- (i) Communicate policies in accessible language tailored to diverse audiences;
- (ii) Emphasize different aspects of platform based on electorate priorities (transport in car-dependent suburbs, housing in affordability crisis areas);
- (iii) Build coalitions with aligned organizations and candidates where strategic;
- (iv) Learn from successful campaigns (international and domestic) and adapt tactics;
- (v) Prioritize honesty and transparency even when electorally costly in short term.

(c) **"Trojan Horse Socialism" approach:**

- (i) Use examples of successful universal programs (Singapore housing, Nordic healthcare, Hong Kong MTR) to demonstrate feasibility without triggering ideological resistance;
- (ii) Frame policies in terms of efficiency, freedom, and practical problem-solving rather than ideological labels;
- (iii) Emphasize "raise the floor" message (ensuring everyone has enough) over "tax the rich" rhetoric (though progressive taxation is part of solution);
- (iv) Appeal to evidence and outcomes, not partisan loyalty or ideological purity;
- (v) Build broad coalition across traditional left-right spectrum by addressing real suffering.

### Rule 65: Candidate Selection and Support

(1) **Candidate Selection Authority:**

(a) **State and local candidates:**

- (i) Selected by State Branch preselection process under Rule 32(2);
- (ii) Approved by National Committee under Rule 32(1)(b);
- (iii) Final authority rests with National Committee to ensure alignment with Association values and standards.

(b) **Federal candidates:**

- (i) Selected by National Committee in consultation with relevant State Branch (if established);
- (ii) May be delegated to State Branch on case-by-case basis;
- (iii) Committee retains oversight and approval authority.

(2) **Candidate Requirements:**

All candidates endorsed by the Association must:

(a) **Eligibility and compliance:**

- (i) Meet legal eligibility requirements for the office sought;
- (ii) Be Paid Members in good standing;
- (iii) Reside in or have strong connection to the electorate;
- (iv) Pass background checks (criminal history, financial probity, public statements review);
- (v) Disclose any potential conflicts of interest, controversial history, or opposition research vulnerabilities.

(b) **Alignment and values:**

- (i) Demonstrate deep understanding of and commitment to Root Axiom;
- (ii) Fluency in Association policy framework and Value Gates;
- (iii) Personal integrity and character consistent with Association values;
- (iv) Commitment to evidence-based decision-making and adapting based on data;
- (v) No history of racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia, ableism, or other bigotry.

(c) **Capability and commitment:**

- (i) Communication skills and ability to engage diverse audiences;
- (ii) Campaigning capacity (time availability, energy, resilience);
- (iii) Team-building and volunteer coordination capability;
- (iv) Willingness to participate in candidate training and preparation;
- (v) Commitment to post-election accountability and continued organizing (win or lose).

(d) **Campaign agreement:**

- (i) Sign candidate agreement committing to Association policy positions;
- (ii) Commit to coordinating campaign with Campaigns Working Group and relevant State Branch;
- (iii) Agree to financial transparency and compliance with electoral law;
- (iv) Accept post-election reporting and accountability requirements;
- (v) Understand that endorsement may be withdrawn if conduct violates Association standards.

(3) **Candidate Support and Resources:**

(a) The Association provides endorsed candidates with:

**(i) Campaign infrastructure:**

- Campaign manager or coordinator (if resources allow);
- Volunteer recruitment and coordination support;
- Campaign plan template and strategic guidance;
- Access to Association branding, materials, and messaging framework.

**(ii) Policy and communications support:**

- Policy briefings and position papers from Policy Working Group;
- Media training and communications coaching from Communications Working Group;
- Talking points and Q&A preparation;
- Rapid response support for controversies or attacks.

**(iii) Financial resources:**

- Campaign budget approved by Committee and allocated from Association funds or fundraising;
- Support with fundraising (donor lists, fundraising events, online campaigns);
- Compliance support for electoral funding and disclosure requirements;
- Reimbursement for approved campaign expenses within budget.

**(iv) Technology and data:**

- Access to voter data and electoral analysis;
- Campaign website and social media support from Technology Working Group;
- Email and SMS campaign tools;
- Volunteer management and contact databases.

**(v) Training and development:**

- Candidate boot camp or intensive training program;
- Media training, public speaking coaching, debate preparation;
- Door-knocking and phone-banking training;
- Policy deep-dives and electorate analysis.

(b) Level of support scaled to:

- (i) Strategic importance of seat (winnable vs. long-shot, high-visibility vs. low-profile);
- (ii) Association capacity and resources available;
- (iii) Candidate's own fundraising and volunteer mobilization;
- (iv) Whether part of coordinated campaign (multiple candidates in same election) or standalone.

(4) **Independent Candidates and Endorsements:**

(a) The Association may **endorse candidates who are not Association members** if:

- (i) Candidate's policy positions substantially align with Association platform;
- (ii) Endorsement advances Root Axiom and Association strategic goals;
- (iii) Candidate requests or consents to endorsement;
- (iv) Committee approves endorsement by majority vote.

(b) Endorsement of non-member candidates:

- (i) Does not commit Association resources beyond public statement of support;
- (ii) May include volunteer assistance or coordination if mutually agreed;
- (iii) Should be transparent about basis for endorsement and any conditions;
- (iv) Can be withdrawn if candidate positions or conduct change.

(c) **Strategic endorsements:**

- (i) Association may endorse independent or minor party candidates over major party candidates in preferential voting system;
- (ii) Endorsements can build coalitions and demonstrate Association is not isolated;
- (iii) Preferencing decisions in elections where Association not contesting should align with policy positions and Root Axiom.

### Rule 66: Campaign Planning and Execution

(1) **Campaign Planning Process:**

(a) For each election where Association is fielding candidates, Campaigns Working Group in coordination with relevant State Branch must develop:

**(i) Campaign strategy document** including:

- Electoral analysis (demographics, previous results, major party positioning, opportunities);
- Campaign narrative and key messages;
- Target voter segments and persuasion strategy;
- Volunteer recruitment and mobilization plan;
- Fundraising targets and strategy;
- Media and communications plan;
- Timeline and key milestones;
- Budget and resource allocation;
- Risk assessment and contingency plans.

**(ii) Candidate campaign plans** for each individual candidate including:

- Electorate-specific analysis and targeting;
- Candidate positioning and biography;
- Local issues and policy emphasis;
- Door-knocking and direct voter contact plan;
- Local media and community engagement;
- Volunteer team structure and roles;
- Budget and expenditure plan;
- Integration with broader Association campaign.

(b) Campaign plans must be:

- (i) Approved by Committee before campaign launch;
- (ii) Grounded in evidence and realistic assessment of capacity;
- (iii) Aligned with Association branding and messaging framework;
- (iv) Compliant with electoral law and Association financial policies;
- (v) Flexible and adaptable based on campaign developments.

(2) **Campaign Coordination:**

(a) **Centralized coordination:**

- (i) Campaigns Working Group provides overall strategic direction and coordination across campaigns;
- (ii) Communications Working Group ensures consistent messaging and branding;
- (iii) National Committee provides oversight and resolves conflicts or issues;
- (iv) Regular campaign coordination meetings during election period.

(b) **Candidate autonomy:**

- (i) Candidates have operational autonomy within approved campaign plan and budget;
- (ii) Local campaign teams make day-to-day tactical decisions;
- (iii) Candidates adapt messaging to local context while maintaining alignment with core policy;
- (iv) Escalate major decisions or controversies to Campaigns WG or Committee.

(c) **Shared resources:**

- (i) Volunteers may be shared across campaigns in same region;
- (ii) Campaign materials, design assets, and resources pooled where possible;
- (iii) Data and voter contact information shared (with privacy compliance);
- (iv) Successful tactics and learnings shared in real-time during campaign.

(3) **Campaign Communications:**

(a) **Messaging principles:**

- (i) **Positive and aspirational:** Focus on vision and solutions, not just attacks on opponents;
- (ii) **Evidence-based:** Support claims with data, research, case studies;
- (iii) **Accessible:** Avoid jargon, use plain language, explain complex policies simply;
- (iv) **Authentic:** Reflect genuine candidate personality and local community voice;
- (v) **Consistent:** Align with Association brand and core messages while allowing local variation.

(b) **Communication channels:**

- (i) **Direct voter contact:** Door-knocking, phone banking, SMS, targeted mail (most effective, resource-intensive);
- (ii) **Traditional media:** Press releases, media interviews, letters to editor, community radio;
- (iii) **Social media:** Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, TikTok, YouTube (cost-effective reach, younger demographics);
- (iv) **Digital advertising:** Targeted online ads, search engine marketing (if budget allows);
- (v) **Community engagement:** Forums, debates, street stalls, local events, community groups;
- (vi) **Earned media:** Campaign launches, policy announcements, endorsements, controversies.

(c) **Message discipline:**

- (i) Candidates must stay on message and aligned with approved policy positions;
- (ii) Major policy announcements coordinated with Committee and Communications WG;
- (iii) Responses to controversies or attacks cleared with campaign team before public statement;
- (iv) "Off script" moments acceptable if aligned with values, but should be flagged to team.

(4) **Volunteer Mobilization:**

(a) **Volunteer recruitment:**

- (i) Recruit from Association membership as foundation;
- (ii) Recruit from candidate's personal networks, local community connections;
- (iii) Recruit via campaign events, social media, community engagement;
- (iv) Welcome volunteers regardless of membership status (non-members can volunteer for campaigns);
- (v) Convert engaged volunteers into Association members post-campaign.

(b) **Volunteer management:**

- (i) Clear roles and task assignments (door-knocking, phone banking, data entry, event support, social media);
- (ii) Training provided for all volunteer roles;
- (iii) Volunteer coordinator manages scheduling, communications, recognition;
- (iv) Regular team meetings and social events (build morale and community);
- (v) Appreciation and recognition (thank you events, personal acknowledgment, post-campaign celebration).

(c) **Volunteer retention:**

- (i) Positive volunteer experience increases likelihood of continued engagement;
- (ii) Stay connected with volunteers post-campaign via newsletters, events, next campaign opportunities;
- (iii) Invite volunteers to join Working Groups or State Branches;
- (iv) Recognize volunteer contributions publicly (with consent);
- (v) Build lasting relationships, not transactional campaign labor extraction.

(5) **Campaign Finance and Compliance:**

(a) **Budget management:**

- (i) Campaign budgets approved by Committee based on strategic priorities and available resources;
- (ii) Expenditures tracked in real-time by campaign treasurer or finance coordinator;
- (iii) Major expenditures (advertising buys, contractor engagements) require approval per spending authority framework in Rule 50;
- (iv) Regular financial reports to Campaigns WG and Committee during campaign;
- (v) Post-campaign financial reconciliation and reporting.

(b) **Fundraising:**

- (i) Campaign-specific fundraising (donations earmarked for specific candidate or election);
- (ii) Fundraising events (dinners, auctions, online campaigns);
- (iii) Small-dollar online donation campaigns;
- (iv) Compliance with electoral funding laws (donation caps, disclosure thresholds, prohibited donors);
- (v) Transparent reporting of campaign income and expenditure.

(c) **Electoral law compliance:**

- (i) **Critical priority:** Non-compliance can result in fines, prosecution, loss of registration;
- (ii) All campaign materials include required authorization statements;
- (iii) Donation and expenditure records maintained meticulously;
- (iv) Electoral commission returns lodged accurately and on time;
- (v) Campaign finance coordinator or Treasurer ensures compliance throughout campaign;
- (vi) Legal advice sought proactively on compliance questions.

### Rule 67: Campaign Ethics and Standards

(1) **Ethical Campaign Conduct:**

(a) Association campaigns must adhere to high ethical standards:

**(i) Honesty and accuracy:**

- No false or misleading claims about Association policies, candidate records, or opponent positions;
- Fact-check claims before publication;
- Correct errors promptly and transparently when they occur;
- Base attacks on opponents on factual record, not innuendo or fabrication.

**(ii) Respect and civility:**

- Focus on policy differences, not personal attacks;
- No racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia, ableism, or other bigotry in campaign materials or rhetoric;
- Respect opponents as people even while disagreeing with their positions;
- Model the political culture Association wants to create.

**(iii) Transparency:**

- Disclose funding sources and major donors (beyond legal requirements);
- Be transparent about Association structure, membership, and decision-making;
- Acknowledge uncertainties and limitations in policy proposals;
- Admit mistakes and learn from them publicly.

**(iv) Democratic engagement:**

- Encourage voter participation and democratic engagement regardless of who people vote for;
- Provide accurate information about voting procedures, registration, accessibility;
- Respect electoral processes and outcomes;
- Do not suppress votes or engage in voter intimidation.

(b) **Prohibited campaign tactics:**

- (i) Voter suppression or intimidation;
- (ii) Deliberate misinformation or disinformation;
- (iii) Coordination with prohibited donors or foreign entities;
- (iv) Bribing voters or offering inducements for votes;
- (v) Impersonating opponents or creating fake endorsements;
- (vi) Hacking, doxxing, or other illegal information gathering;
- (vii) Astroturfing (fake grassroots campaigns) or bot networks;
- (viii) Any illegal activity or violation of electoral law.

(2) **Negative Campaigning:**

(a) **Negative campaigning is permitted when:**

- (i) Based on factual record of opponent's votes, statements, or actions;
- (ii) Focused on policy positions, not personal characteristics;
- (iii) Relevant to voters' decision-making (legitimate public interest);
- (iv) Proportionate and not exaggerated;
- (v) Necessary to highlight meaningful differences between candidates.

(b) **Negative campaigning crosses line when:**

- (i) Based on falsehoods, fabrications, or misleading editing;
- (ii) Attacks irrelevant personal matters (family, appearance, private life);
- (iii) Uses bigoted or discriminatory rhetoric;
- (iv) Relies on innuendo, conspiracy theories, or guilt by association;
- (v) Becomes dominant campaign message (mostly attacking, not offering alternative).

(c) **Balance:**

- Association campaigns should be **primarily positive** (vision, solutions, values) with **targeted negative** (holding opponents accountable for record).
- Ratio of positive to negative should heavily favor positive.
- Negative campaigning should feel reluctant and necessary, not gleeful or vindictive.

(3) **Candidate Conduct Standards:**

(a) Candidates represent Association and must conduct themselves accordingly:

**(i) Public behavior:**

- Professional and respectful in all public interactions;
- No harassment, bullying, or abusive behavior toward opponents, media, voters, or volunteers;
- Respectful disagreement without personal animosity;
- Model inclusive and welcoming demeanor.

**(ii) Social media:**

- Exercise judgment and restraint in social media posts;
- Avoid inflammatory or offensive content;
- No "posting through" controversies - take time to respond thoughtfully;
- Maintain campaign social media separately from personal if necessary.

**(iii) Alcohol and substances:**

- No public intoxication or substance use that impairs judgment during campaign;
- Campaign events may include alcohol but candidates should model responsible consumption.

**(iv) Romantic/sexual conduct:**

- No sexual harassment or inappropriate advances toward volunteers, staff, or voters;
- Exercise extreme discretion in romantic relationships during campaign;
- Disclose potential conflicts if romantic involvement with campaign team member.

**(v) Financial propriety:**

- No misuse of campaign funds for personal benefit;
- Transparent expense claims and reimbursements;
- No conflicts of interest between campaign and personal financial interests.

(b) **Violation consequences:**

If candidate violates conduct standards:

- (i) **Minor violations:** Counseling, warning, additional oversight;
- (ii) **Moderate violations:** Public apology, remedial action, reduced autonomy;
- (iii) **Serious violations:** Suspension of campaign support, endorsement withdrawn;
- (iv) **Severe violations:** Immediate withdrawal of endorsement, public dissociation, potential membership termination.

Decision made by Committee based on severity, context, candidate's response, and impact on Association.

### Rule 68: Post-Election Accountability and Learning

(1) **Post-Election Reporting:**

(a) Within **30 days** of any election where Association fielded candidates, detailed post-election reports must be submitted:

**(i) Candidate reports** (from each candidate or campaign manager):

- Electoral results (votes, percentage, preferences, comparison to targets);
- Campaign financial summary (income, expenditure, compliance);
- Volunteer engagement metrics (number of volunteers, hours contributed, activities);
- Media coverage summary (earned media, paid advertising, social media reach);
- Voter contact statistics (doors knocked, calls made, conversations, conversion rates);
- Campaign narrative (what worked, what didn't, unexpected challenges, key moments);
- Lessons learned and recommendations for future campaigns;
- Personal candidate reflections and experience.

**(ii) Consolidated campaign report** (from Campaigns Working Group):

- Overall electoral performance across all Association candidates;
- Strategic assessment (did campaign achieve objectives?);
- Comparative analysis (performance vs. expectations, benchmarks, previous elections);
- Resource utilization (budget, volunteers, time);
- Coalition building and preference flows;
- Policy resonance (which messages worked, which fell flat);
- Organizational capacity assessment (what Association can sustain);
- Recommendations for future electoral strategy.

(b) Post-election reports must be:

- (i) Honest and self-critical (acknowledge failures as well as successes);
- (ii) Evidence-based (grounded in data, not just impressions);
- (iii) Forward-looking (actionable recommendations);
- (iv) Shared with Committee, relevant State Branches, and (in summary form) membership.

(2) **Learning and Adaptation:**

(a) Committee and Campaigns Working Group must systematically learn from each electoral cycle:

**(i) What worked:**

- Successful messaging, tactics, volunteer mobilization, coalition building;
- Document and codify for replication in future campaigns;
- Share with other State Branches and candidates;
- Build institutional knowledge and capacity.

**(ii) What didn't work:**

- Failed strategies, ineffective messaging, resource misallocations;
- Analyze root causes (was it execution, strategy, or external factors?);
- Adjust approach for future campaigns;
- Avoid repeating mistakes.

**(iii) Emerging opportunities:**

- Demographic shifts, policy issues gaining salience, major party vulnerabilities;
- New technologies or campaign methods;
- Coalition or partnership possibilities;
- Electoral reform or redistribution creating new opportunities.

(b) **Continuous improvement culture:**

- Elections are experiments that generate data for next iteration;
- Losing campaigns can still succeed at learning and capacity building;
- Failure is acceptable and expected (most new parties lose for years before breakthrough);
- What matters is trajectory (are we improving?) and learning (are we adapting?).

(3) **Candidate Retention and Development:**

(a) **Candidates who lose elections should:**

- (i) Be thanked and recognized for service;
- (ii) Remain engaged with Association (continue as member, volunteer, or organizer);
- (iii) Consider running again in future (persistence matters);
- (iv) Transition to other Association roles (Committee, Working Group Coordinator, State Branch);
- (v) Mentor future candidates and share experience.

(b) **Association should:**

- (i) Maintain relationship with candidates post-election;
- (ii) Provide debriefing and emotional support (campaigns are intense and losing is difficult);
- (iii) Help candidates transition back to normal life;
- (iv) Incorporate candidate feedback into organizational improvement;
- (v) Build pipeline of experienced candidates for future elections.

(c) **Building candidate depth:**

- Association needs bench of potential candidates for multiple electorates and levels of government;
- Identify promising members early and provide development opportunities;
- Candidate training programs, mentorship, campaign shadowing;
- Not everyone needs to run immediately - build capacity over time.

(4) **Electoral Success and Governing Preparation:**

(a) If Association candidates are **elected to office:**

**(i) Immediate priorities:**

- Transition planning (staffing, office setup, legislative procedures);
- Policy implementation strategy within constraints of office;
- Media and communications strategy as elected representative;
- Relationship with Association (elected member remains member but has independent responsibilities);
- Accountability framework (how elected member reports to Association and constituents).

**(ii) Governing capacity:**

- Association must develop capacity to support elected members (policy research, constituent services, legislative drafting);
- Working Groups provide policy advice and research support;
- Communications WG supports public communications and media;
- Elected members participate in Association governance but manage time constraints.

**(iii) Maintaining values:**

- Elected members must remain aligned with Root Axiom and Association values;
- Accountability mechanisms (regular reporting, policy consultation, community feedback);
- Balance representing all constituents with advocating Association policy;
- Model collaborative, evidence-based, transparent governance.

(b) **Preparing for growth:**

- Even before electoral success, Association should build governance capacity;
- Policy depth, legislative drafting skills, media capability, constituent service systems;
- By the time Association wins seats, should have infrastructure to support effective governing.

### Rule 69: Preferential Voting and Coalition Strategy

(1) **Preferential Voting System:**

(a) Australia uses preferential voting (instant runoff) in most elections:

- (i) Voters rank candidates in order of preference;
- (ii) If no candidate wins majority of first preferences, lowest-polling candidate eliminated and preferences distributed;
- (iii) Process continues until one candidate has majority;
- (iv) This means Association can contest elections without "spoiler" effect.

(b) **Strategic implications:**

- (i) Association can run candidates without helping least-preferred major party win (unlike first-past-the-post);
- (ii) Even if Association doesn't win, can influence outcome via preference flows;
- (iii) "Vote 1 Association, preferences to aligned candidates" is viable strategy;
- (iv) Building first-preference vote share demonstrates support and shifts discourse even without winning seats.

(2) **How-to-Vote Cards and Preference Recommendations:**

(a) For each election, Association must decide:

- (i) Which candidates to preference above others (how-to-vote card order);
- (ii) Whether to make preference recommendations or leave open to voters;
- (iii) Whether to negotiate preference swaps with other candidates/parties.

(b) **Preference decision framework:**

**(i) Criteria for preferencing:**

- Policy alignment with Association platform (most important);
- Candidate integrity and ethics;
- Electorate-specific factors and voter sentiment;
- Strategic considerations (denying seats to worst opponents);
- Reciprocal preference deals (if advantageous and not compromising values).

**(ii) General approach:**

- **First preference:** Association candidate (obviously);
- **Second tier:** Independent or minor party candidates with strong policy alignment (Greens, progressive independents, single-issue candidates aligned on key issues);
- **Third tier:** Major party candidates with some redeeming features or less-bad record;
- **Last preferences:** Candidates with policies antithetical to Association values (far-right, climate deniers, anti-immigrant, etc.).

**(iii) Never preference above Association:**

- No preference deals that put other candidates above Association candidate;
- Association candidate is always first preference on Association how-to-vote cards;
- But Association can recommend preferences for voters in seats where Association not contesting.

(c) **Preference negotiation:**

**(i) Association may negotiate with other parties/candidates:**

- "We'll preference you above [major party] if you preference us above [other major party]";
- Can be mutually beneficial in preferential system;
- Enables minor parties and independents to build collective strength against major parties.

**(ii) Conditions for preference deals:**

- Must not compromise Association policy positions or values;
- Must be transparent (disclose deals to members and public);
- Must provide strategic benefit (not just courtesy preferences);
- Approved by Committee before finalized.

**(iii) No preference deals with:**

- Far-right parties or candidates;
- Parties/candidates with racist, anti-immigrant, or bigoted platforms;
- Candidates under investigation for corruption or serious misconduct;
- Anyone whose policies fundamentally contradict Root Axiom.

(3) **Coalition and Alliance Strategy:**

(a) **Association may form electoral coalitions** with aligned parties or candidates:

**(i) Types of coalitions:**

- **Preference swaps:** Mutual preferencing agreements;
- **Joint campaigns:** Coordinated messaging, shared resources, joint events;
- **Seat agreements:** "You run here, we run there" to avoid splitting vote;
- **Governing coalitions:** If multiple aligned parties/independents elected, form bloc in legislature.

**(ii) Coalition criteria:**

- Substantial policy alignment on core issues (UBI, universal healthcare, climate action, etc.);
- Shared values around evidence-based policy and democratic governance;
- Mutual respect and good faith relationship;
- Strategic benefit outweighs any compromises required.

**(iii) Coalition risks:**

- Association brand and identity must remain distinct;
- No mergers or absorption into larger parties;
- Maintain independence on policy positions;
- Exit option if coalition partner betrays values or becomes liability.

(b) **Crossbench strategy (if elected):**

**(i) If Association elects members to parliament:**

- Operate as independent or part of crossbench bloc;
- No automatic allegiance to major parties;
- Vote based on policy merit and Root Axiom alignment;
- Build coalitions issue-by-issue, not permanent party alignment.

**(ii) Balance of power scenarios:**

- If Association holds balance of power (hung parliament), maximum leverage for policy influence;
- Negotiate policy commitments in exchange for confidence and supply;
- Extract maximum concessions toward Association policy goals;
- But remain flexible and willing to work with either side if they meet conditions.

**(iii) Governing coalition decisions:**

- Entering government coalition is major strategic decision requiring member approval at Special General Meeting;
- Should only occur if substantial Association policy agenda can be implemented;
- Maintain distinct identity and ability to withdraw if betrayed;
- Communicate clearly to members what compromises are acceptable and which are non-negotiable.

## PART 12: RECORDS AND TRANSPARENCY

### Rule 70: Documentation Philosophy and Requirements

(1) **Documentation as Foundation:**

(a) The Association operates on principle that **documented systems scale, undocumented systems fail**.

(b) This principle manifests as three core documentation rules:

- (i) **Rule 21A:** "If not documented, doesn't exist" - decisions, processes, and agreements must be recorded;
- (ii) **Rule 21B:** "If not documented, not expected" - roles and responsibilities must be explicitly defined;
- (iii) **Rule 70:** "If not accessible, not transparent" - documentation must be findable and usable.

(c) **Root Axiom alignment:**

- Documentation eliminates **inefficiency** of: re-explaining decisions, duplicating work, losing institutional knowledge, onboarding friction, dispute over "what was decided";
- Documentation eliminates **suffering** of: members feeling excluded, volunteers confused about roles, leaders burned out re-explaining, newcomers unable to contribute;
- Documentation enables **evidence-based** improvement: can't learn from what you can't review.

(2) **What Must Be Documented:**

All of the following must be documented in writing and stored in accessible systems:

(a) **Governance and decisions:**

- (i) Committee meeting agendas, minutes, and decisions (Rule 46);
- (ii) General Meeting (AGM/SGM) notices, agendas, minutes, and votes (Rule 39);
- (iii) NCAP authorizations with complete audit trail (Rule 49);
- (iv) Working Group and State Branch decisions and reports (Rules 26, 32);
- (v) Constitutional amendments and rationale;
- (vi) Policy positions and Root Axiom Impact Assessments (Rule 60);
- (vii) Strategic plans and electoral strategies.

(b) **Financial records:**

- (i) All income and expenditure with receipts (Rule 56);
- (ii) Bank account transactions and reconciliations;
- (iii) Budgets, financial reports, and audit/review reports;
- (iv) NCAP-authorized spending with approval evidence;
- (v) Electoral funding and disclosure records (if registered);
- (vi) Contracts, agreements, and financial commitments.

(c) **Membership and people:**

- (i) Membership register with contact details, membership type, dates (Rule 9);
- (ii) Committee member appointments and terms;
- (iii) Working Group and State Branch rosters;
- (iv) Role definitions for all positions (Rule 21B);
- (v) Remuneration decisions and amounts (Rule 21(6));
- (vi) Discipline decisions and appeals (Rule 13) - confidential but documented.

(d) **Operations and processes:**

- (i) Operating procedures and handbooks (Secretary Operating Manual, Professional Standards, etc.);
- (ii) NCAP protocol details (Schedule B);
- (iii) Communications and brand guidelines;
- (iv) Technology infrastructure documentation;
- (v) Electoral campaign plans and post-election reports (Rule 68);
- (vi) Partnership and coalition agreements.

(e) **Legal and compliance:**

- (i) Constitution (this document) and all amendments;
- (ii) Incorporation documents and CAV annual statements;
- (iii) Electoral registration documents and returns;
- (iv) Legal advice and correspondence (confidential but documented);
- (v) Insurance policies and claims;
- (vi) Intellectual property registrations (logos, trademarks, domain names).

(3) **Documentation Standards:**

(a) All documentation must be:

- (i) **Accurate:** Reflect reality, not aspirations or impressions;
- (ii) **Complete:** Include essential context and rationale, not just outcomes;
- (iii) **Accessible:** Stored where authorized people can find it;
- (iv) **Searchable:** Organized and tagged for retrieval;
- (v) **Preserved:** Retained for required periods (minimum 7 years for financial, permanent for governance);
- (vi) **Versioned:** Changes tracked with dates and authors.

(b) **Good documentation includes:**

- What was decided or done;
- When it occurred;
- Who was involved (proposer, approver, participants);
- Why it was decided (rationale, Root Axiom alignment);
- How it will be implemented or measured;
- Links to related documents or discussions.

(c) **Bad documentation:**

- "We discussed some stuff and made some decisions" - no specifics;
- Minutes that just say "approved" without context or votes;
- Decisions stored in personal inboxes or private messages;
- Knowledge that exists only in one person's head;
- "You had to be there to understand" - inaccessible to future members.

(4) **Documentation Responsibilities:**

(a) **Primary responsibility:**

- (i) **Secretary:** Overall custodian of Association records, ensures documentation requirements met;
- (ii) **Treasurer:** Financial records, expenditure documentation, audit trails;
- (iii) **Working Group Coordinators:** Working Group decisions, activities, reports;
- (iv) **State Branch Coordinators:** Branch decisions, activities, reports;
- (v) **Committee members:** Collective responsibility for meeting minutes and decision documentation.

(b) **Everyone's responsibility:**

- All members should document their contributions and decisions within their areas;
- "I did this work but didn't document it" = work may not be recognized or may need redoing;
- Culture of documentation is collective, not just leadership burden.

(c) **Technology Working Group support:**

- Maintain documentation infrastructure (community space, file storage, version control);
- Ensure systems are accessible, backed up, and functional;
- Train members on documentation tools and practices;
- Automate documentation where possible (NCAP logs, meeting recordings, etc.).

(5) **When Documentation Is Not Required:**

(a) **Informal discussions:**

- Brainstorming, casual conversations, social chatter in community space don't require formal documentation;
- But if informal discussion leads to decision, decision should be documented.

(b) **Work in progress:**

- Draft documents, exploratory research, abandoned ideas don't need permanent documentation;
- But final outputs and decisions about what to pursue/abandon should be documented.

(c) **Personal notes and working files:**

- Individual members' personal notes, to-do lists, draft materials are personal;
- But anything affecting Association decisions or operations should be elevated to shared documentation.

(6) **Balancing Documentation with Action:**

(a) **Documentation should not paralyze action:**

- Don't let "we need to document this first" prevent necessary action;
- Act first, document after - what matters is that it gets documented;
- Documentation is servant of effective action, not master.

(b) **But documentation is not optional:**

- "Too busy to document" is not acceptable - make time or delegate;
- If you act, you must document - eventually;
- If you don't have time to document, you're doing too much - reassess priorities.

(c) **When to document:**

- **Concurrent preferred:** Meeting minutes during meeting, NCAP logs automatic - easiest and most accurate;
- **Retrospective acceptable:** Document hours, days, or weeks after if needed - better late than never;
- **What matters:** That it gets documented, not when it gets documented;
- **Memory decay caveat:** Longer you wait, harder it is to reconstruct accurately - don't procrastinate indefinitely.

(d) **Right-size documentation effort:**

- Small decisions: Brief note in appropriate channel or log;
- Medium decisions: NCAP submission or email with rationale;
- Major decisions: Formal motion, detailed minutes, Root Axiom Impact Assessment;
- Documentation effort should scale to decision significance.

(e) **Retrospective documentation guidelines:**

- Note that it's retrospective: "[Documented 2025-03-15, decision made 2025-02-28]";
- Be honest about what you remember vs. reconstruct;
- If multiple people involved, consult them to verify accuracy;
- Better to document approximately than not at all.

### Rule 72: Transparency and Member Access

(1) **Default to Transparency:**

(a) The Association operates on principle of **transparency by default, confidentiality by exception**.

(b) This means:

- Information should be shared with members unless specific reason for confidentiality;
- Burden of proof is on restricting access, not on granting it;
- If uncertain whether to share, lean toward sharing;
- Transparency builds trust, secrecy breeds suspicion.

(c) **Benefits of transparency:**

- Members feel informed and included;
- Decisions can be questioned and improved;
- Accountability is automatic (sunlight is disinfectant);
- Institutional knowledge is shared, not hoarded;
- Newcomers can get up to speed quickly;
- Public trust and credibility enhanced.

(d) **Costs of excessive secrecy:**

- Members feel excluded and distrustful;
- "Secret committee" perception damages morale;
- Knowledge silos create dependencies on individuals;
- Mistakes and dysfunction can hide;
- Onboarding is difficult;
- Public skepticism about motives.

(2) **What Must Be Public:**

The following must be accessible to the general public (not just members):

(a) **Constitution and governance:**

- Current Constitution with all amendments;
- Policy register with all current policy positions;
- Committee composition and contact information;
- Annual reports to AGM;
- AGM and SGM notices.

(b) **Financial transparency:**

- Annual financial statements approved at AGM;
- High-level budget (categories, not line items);
- Electoral funding and disclosure reports (if registered with electoral commission);
- Major contracts or partnerships (general description, not confidential terms).

(c) **Campaign and electoral:**

- Candidate profiles and platforms;
- Campaign materials and policy messaging;
- Electoral results and post-election reports (general findings);
- Preference framework (Traffic Light or similar).

(d) **Contact and participation:**

- How to join Association;
- How to contact Committee or Working Groups;
- Public events and meetings;
- How to provide feedback or raise concerns.

(3) **What Must Be Accessible to Members:**

Beyond public information, members should have access to:

(a) **All Paid Members:**

- Detailed monthly financial reports;
- Committee meeting summaries (decisions and rationale, not full deliberations);
- Working Group updates and reports;
- State Branch reports (if applicable);
- Policy development discussions and drafts;
- Strategic plans and priorities;
- NCAP activity summaries and patterns;
- Annual budget with detail.

(b) **Free Members:**

- Basic financial summaries (quarterly);
- Committee decision announcements;
- Policy register and major policy updates;
- High-level strategic priorities;
- General meeting materials.

(c) **Committee Members:**

- Everything Paid Members access, plus:
- Full Committee meeting minutes and materials;
- Confidential financial details;
- Legal advice and compliance matters;
- Personnel and discipline information (where appropriate to role);
- Sensitive strategic negotiations and partnerships.

(4) **Legitimate Confidentiality:**

Some information must be kept confidential to protect legitimate interests:

(a) **Personal privacy:**

- Member contact details and personal information;
- Discipline matters involving individual members;
- Personnel issues (if staff employed);
- Health or personal circumstances of members/candidates.

(b) **Legal and commercial:**

- Legal advice from solicitors (legal professional privilege);
- Litigation strategy for active cases;
- Commercial negotiations before concluded;
- Contract terms with confidentiality clauses;
- Intellectual property not yet protected or published.

(c) **Strategic sensitivity:**

- Candidate selections before announced;
- Campaign strategy details that would advantage opponents;
- Negotiation positions before talks concluded;
- Security or safety information.

(d) **Regulatory compliance:**

- Information required to be confidential by electoral law;
- Donor information beyond disclosure thresholds (privacy);
- Tax or compliance matters under investigation.

(5) **Member Requests for Information:**

(a) Any member may request access to Association records by written request to Secretary.

(b) **Request should specify:**

- What information or documents are sought;
- Purpose of request (helps Secretary provide most useful materials);
- Preferred format (digital, physical copy, access to view).

(c) **Secretary must respond within 14 days:**

- (i) **Grant access** to records member entitled to see;
- (ii) **Provide copies** or arrange viewing as appropriate;
- (iii) **Redact confidential portions** if document contains mix of public/confidential;
- (iv) **Explain denial** if access denied, with specific reason and constitutional/legal basis;
- (v) **Offer appeal** to Committee if member disputes denial.

(d) **Reasons access may be denied:**

- Information is legitimately confidential under Rule 72(4);
- Request is vexatious or excessively burdensome (asks for "everything");
- Information doesn't exist or can't be located despite reasonable search;
- Legal or regulatory prohibition on disclosure;
- Disclosure would harm Association, members, or third parties.

(e) **If access denied, member may:**

- Appeal to Committee for review of decision;
- Committee must consider appeal at next meeting;
- Committee decision is final unless constitutional violation alleged;
- If constitutional issue, may be raised at General Meeting under Rule 13(7) appeal process.

(6) **Proactive Transparency:**

(a) Rather than wait for requests, Association should proactively publish:

- Regular Committee decision summaries (after each meeting);
- Quarterly financial updates to members;
- Working Group and State Branch reports;
- NCAP activity summaries (monthly or quarterly);
- Strategic priorities and progress updates;
- Changes to policy, procedures, or governance.

(b) **Publishing platforms:**

- Community space announcements (meta channel for governance updates);
- Regular newsletter or email digest to members;
- Website updates for public information;
- Annual report consolidating all major activities and outcomes.

(c) **Transparency without information overload:**

- Provide summaries with links to detail for those interested;
- Don't bury members in every document - curate what's important;
- Make information findable, not mandatory reading;
- Use clear, accessible language, not bureaucratic jargon.

### Rule 73: Knowledge Management and Institutional Memory

(1) **Institutional Knowledge Problem:**

(a) Small organizations often suffer from **institutional knowledge loss**:

- Key member leaves, taking knowledge with them;
- New members can't access history of decisions;
- Same mistakes repeated because no one remembers what didn't work;
- Processes exist only in one person's head;
- Transitions between roles are chaotic.

(b) **Root Axiom impact:**

- Knowledge loss creates **inefficiency:** re-inventing wheels, duplicating work, slower onboarding;
- Knowledge loss creates **suffering:** new members feel lost, volunteers unsure what to do, leaders burned out re-explaining;
- Documentation and knowledge management eliminate both.

(2) **Building Institutional Memory:**

(a) **Document decisions with rationale:**

- Not just "what" was decided, but "why";
- Context that led to decision;
- Alternatives considered and why rejected;
- Expected outcomes and how to measure success.

(b) **Document what didn't work:**

- "We tried X in 2024 campaign, it failed because Y, don't do it again";
- Lessons learned reports after campaigns, projects, initiatives;
- Honest assessment of failures, not just successes;
- Create institutional wisdom, not just institutional history.

(c) **Maintain handbooks and procedures:**

- Secretary Operating Manual (what Secretary does, how to do it);
- Treasurer Handbook (financial procedures, software, reporting);
- Working Group Coordinator guides;
- Campaign planning templates and checklists;
- Onboarding guides for new members.

(d) **Archive historical documents:**

- Don't delete old constitutions, policies, or documents;
- Archive with clear dates and version numbers;
- Maintain access so future members can understand evolution;
- "Why do we do it this way?" answered by historical record.

(e) **Knowledge transfer during transitions:**

- Outgoing role-holders must brief incoming;
- Written transition notes documenting current state, pending issues, contacts, processes;
- Overlap period where possible (outgoing shadows incoming);
- Documentation reduces but doesn't eliminate need for human transfer.

(3) **Onboarding and Knowledge Accessibility:**

(a) **New member onboarding should include:**

- Welcome message with overview of Association (purposes, values, structure);
- Link to Constitution and policy register;
- Guide to community space channels and how to participate;
- Explanation of Working Groups and how to join;
- FAQ addressing common questions;
- Contact person for questions (Secretary or Membership WG Coordinator).

(b) **Make knowledge findable:**

- Searchable documentation systems;
- "Start here" guides for newcomers;
- Index or table of contents for key documents;
- Tags and keywords for topics;
- Don't assume newcomers will osmotically absorb tribal knowledge.

(c) **Encourage questions:**

- "No stupid questions" culture;
- Channel for new members to ask anything;
- Patient explanations without "you should know this" attitude;
- Document answers to common questions in FAQ;
- Remember: if one person asks, others probably wonder too.

(4) **Continuous Improvement through Documentation:**

(a) **Documentation enables learning:**

- Review past decisions and assess outcomes;
- Identify patterns of success and failure;
- Measure progress toward goals;
- Hold selves accountable for commitments;
- Adapt based on evidence.

(b) **Annual retrospectives:**

- Review year's decisions, campaigns, initiatives;
- What worked? What didn't? Why?
- What should we do more of? Less of? Differently?
- Update handbooks and procedures based on learnings;
- Codify improvements so they persist.

(c) **Documentation is investment:**

- Takes time upfront;
- Saves enormous time later;
- Compounds over time (more documentation = more valuable);
- Enables scaling (more members, more capacity);
- Essential for serious organization.

### Rule 74: Records Retention and Destruction

(1) **Retention Requirements:**

(a) Different types of records have different retention requirements based on legal obligations, governance needs, and historical value:

**(i) Permanent retention (never destroy):**

- Constitution and all amendments;
- Committee and General Meeting minutes;
- Major policy documents;
- Strategic plans and major decisions;
- Incorporation and registration documents;
- Significant legal agreements or contracts.

**(ii) 7 years minimum (legal requirement):**

- Financial records (transactions, invoices, receipts, bank statements);
- Tax records and returns;
- Employment records (if staff employed);
- Electoral funding and disclosure records;
- Contracts and agreements.

**(iii) While active + 2 years:**

- Working Group and project documents;
- Campaign materials and reports;
- Correspondence and routine communications;
- Draft documents and working files.

**(iv) While relevant:**

- Member contact information (delete promptly when membership ends);
- Discipline records (retain minimum necessary, destroy when person no longer member);
- Confidential personal information (minimize retention).

(b) Retention schedules should be documented and followed systematically, not ad hoc.

(2) **Secure Destruction:**

(a) When documents reach end of retention period, they should be **securely destroyed**:

- Physical documents: shred or burn, don't just bin;
- Digital documents: permanently delete, overwrite if sensitive;
- Personal information: special care to prevent identity theft or privacy breach.

(b) **Document destruction log:**

- Record what was destroyed, when, by whom, and under what authority;
- Ensures destruction was deliberate and authorized, not accidental loss;
- Provides evidence of compliance with privacy and record-keeping laws.

(c) **Never destroy:**

- Documents subject to legal hold (litigation, investigation, audit);
- Documents with historical significance;
- Records still within retention period;
- Anything you're uncertain about (consult Secretary or legal advisor).

(3) **Backup and Disaster Recovery:**

(a) **All critical digital records must be backed up:**

- Daily backups of most critical data (member lists, financial records, active documents);
- Weekly backups of less critical data;
- Monthly backups retained for at least 12 months;
- Annual backups retained permanently.

(b) **Backup storage:**

- Multiple locations (cloud and local);
- Different provider than primary system (reduces single point of failure);
- Encrypted if contains confidential information;
- Tested regularly to ensure backups actually work.

(c) **Disaster recovery plan:**

- If primary systems fail (hardware failure, cyberattack, natural disaster), how do we restore?
- Who has access to backups?
- How long would restoration take?
- What is acceptable data loss window (last 24 hours? last week?);
- Plan should be documented and tested periodically.

(d) **Physical document security:**

- Important physical documents (incorporation certificates, signed contracts) stored securely;
- Fireproof safe or bank safety deposit box for irreplaceable originals;
- Digital copies as backup.

- Maintain access to archived data from old system.

### Rule 76: NCAP Technical Implementation

(1) **NCAP System Requirements:**

(a) The NCAP system must provide:

**(i) Submission interface:**

- Form or command to submit authorization request;
- Required fields: Title, Description, Approver(s), Spending amount (if applicable), Timer duration;
- Optional fields: Category, Rationale, Links to related documents;
- Confirmation of submission with unique ID.

**(ii) Notification system:**

- Notify approver(s) immediately upon submission;
- Notify members in relevant channels (all members for major decisions, specific Working Group for WG decisions);
- Reminder notifications as timer approaches expiration;
- Notification of approval, objection, or expiration.

**(iii) Timer tracking:**

- Automatic countdown from submission;
- Clear display of time remaining;
- Automatic approval when timer expires with no objection (or Approved Gantry completes);
- Support for **Dynamic Timer Calculation** (Rule 49(3));
- Handling of **Gantry States** (Approved/Objection) and **Instant Resolution** triggers (Rule 49(5));
- Halt/Extend timer logic for objections (Ceiling mechanics).

**(iv) Objection mechanism:**

- Any member can object via button/command;
- Objection triggers **Objection Gantry** (extends timer to Ceiling) per Rule 49;
- Objector notified that objection was recorded;
- Submitter and approver notified of objection.

**(v) Audit trail and logging:**

- Every NCAP action logged with timestamp and actor;
- Submission, approval, objection, expiration all recorded;
- Complete history accessible for review;
- Searchable by date, category, approver, amount, keyword;
- Export capability for reporting and analysis.

**(vi) Integration with financial tracking:**

- NCAP approvals linked to expenditure records;
- Track budget against NCAP-authorized spending;
- Flag when spending approaches or exceeds NCAP limits;
- Generate financial reports from NCAP data.

(2) **NCAP User Experience:**

(a) **For submitters:**

- Simple form or command, minimal friction;
- Clear guidance on what information to provide;
- Immediate confirmation with tracking link;
- Status updates as approval progresses;
- Notification of outcome (approved/objected/escalated).

(b) **For approvers:**

- Clear notification with all relevant information;
- Context visible (what's being requested, why, by whom);
- One-click approval or option to discuss/defer;
- Ability to request clarification before approving;
- Override option if urgent (manual approval before timer expires).

(c) **For members (potential objectors):**

- Visibility into active NCAP requests;
- Channel or dashboard showing current timers;
- Clear "Objection" button with confirmation dialog;
- Explanation of what objection does (escalates to Committee, doesn't auto-block);
- Low friction to object if something seems wrong.

(d) **For Committee (when escalated):**

- Notification of objection with context;
- Access to full submission details and objection rationale;
- Discussion space to resolve;
- Final decision recorded and communicated.

(3) **NCAP Bot/Application Architecture:**

(a) **Implementation options:**

**(i) Discord bot (current approach):**

- Bot runs on server (cloud or self-hosted);
- Responds to slash commands or form submissions;
- Posts messages to designated channels;
- Uses Discord reactions/buttons for interaction;
- Stores data in database (SQLite, PostgreSQL, etc.);
- Written in Python, JavaScript, or other bot-friendly language.

**Advantages:** Integrated with primary communication platform, members already familiar with Discord, real-time notifications.

**Disadvantages:** Dependent on Discord, requires server hosting, bot maintenance.

**(ii) Web application (alternative/future):**

- Standalone web app accessible via browser;
- Form-based submissions;
- Email or webhook notifications to Discord/Slack;
- Database backend for tracking;
- More portable (not tied to Discord).

**Advantages:** Platform-agnostic, more professional UI possible, easier to migrate if communication platform changes.

**Disadvantages:** Separate system to maintain, less integrated with daily workflow, requires members to visit external site.

**(iii) Hybrid approach:**

- Web app for submissions and audit trail;
- Discord bot for notifications and quick actions;
- Best of both worlds (portability + integration).

(b) **Data model:**

The NCAP system should track:

```
NCAP_Submission:
  - id (unique identifier)
  - title (short description)
  - description (detailed explanation)
  - category (e.g., "Communications", "Financial", "Policy")
  - submitter (member who submitted)
  - approver(s) (who needs to approve)
  - timer_duration (hours until auto-approval)
  - amount (spending request, if applicable)
  - status (pending, approved, objected, escalated, expired)
  - submitted_at (timestamp)
  - approved_at (timestamp, null if not approved)
  - objected_at (timestamp, null if no objection)
  - objector (member who objected, null if none)
  - objection_reason (text explaining objection)
  - escalation_outcome (Committee decision if escalated)
  - links (URLs to related docs/discussions)

Financial_Transaction (if spending):
  - ncap_id (foreign key to NCAP_Submission)
  - amount (actual amount spent)
  - receipt (file/link to receipt)
  - date (when spent)
  - category (budget category)
```

(c) **Security and access:**

- NCAP system should authenticate members (verify Discord/member ID);
- Prevent spoofing (can't submit as someone else);
- Prevent duplicate objections (one objection per member per submission);
- Admin interface for Secretary/Technology WG to troubleshoot;
- Backup data regularly (database exports);
- Audit who accesses sensitive admin functions.

(4) **NCAP Monitoring and Analytics:**

(a) **Regular reporting:**

- Monthly summary of NCAP activity (number of submissions, approvals, objections, categories);
- Financial summary (total spending authorized via NCAP, breakdown by category);
- Trend analysis (are we using NCAP more/less? what categories most active?);
- Objection patterns (who objects? to what? are objections justified?).

(b) **Dashboard for visibility:**

- Real-time view of active NCAP timers;
- Historical view of past approvals and spending;
- Searchable archive;
- Accessible to all Paid Members for transparency.

(c) **Continuous improvement:**

- Review NCAP usage quarterly or annually;
- Adjust timers, thresholds, processes based on data;
- Solicit feedback from members on NCAP UX;
- Update Schedule B with refinements.

- **#ncap-archive** - Completed NCAP requests (read-only log).

**(iii) Working Group channels:**

- **#communications** - Communications Working Group coordination;
- **#policy** - Policy Working Group discussions;
- **#campaigns** - Campaigns Working Group planning;
- **#technology** - Technology Working Group infrastructure work;
- **#membership** - Membership Working Group recruitment/engagement;
- _[Additional channels for other Working Groups as established]_.

**(iv) State Branch channels:**

- **#victoria** - Victoria State Branch coordination;
- **#nsw** - NSW State Branch coordination (if established);
- _[Additional channels for other State Branches as established]_.

**(v) Project/Campaign channels (temporary):**

- **#campaign-melton-2026** - Specific campaign coordination;
- **#project-transport-engine** - Specific project work;
- _[Created as needed, archived when complete]_.

**(vi) Social/Off-topic channels:**

- **#off-topic** - Non-Association chat, memes, hobbies;
- **#media** - Share articles, videos, relevant news;
- **#events** - Meetups, social gatherings, conferences;
- Voice channels for meetings, hangouts.

(b) **Channel naming conventions:**

- Use lowercase and hyphens (e.g., #working-group-name, not #Working_Group_Name);
- Prefix for categorization (#wg-communications, #state-vic, #project-transport);
- Clear, descriptive names (not abbreviations or inside jokes);
- Consistent structure aids navigation.

(c) **Channel descriptions:**

- Every channel should have clear description/topic;
- Explains purpose and what belongs in channel;
- Links to relevant documents or resources;
- Pin important messages for quick reference.

(2) **Access Controls and Permissions:**

(a) **Role-based access:**

The community space should use roles to control access:

**(i) Public roles:**

- **@everyone** - Default role, can view public channels;
- **@Free Member** - Free Members, access to member channels;
- **@Paid Member** - Paid Members, full access to member areas;
- **@Committee** - Committee members, access to Committee channel;
- **@Working Group - [Name]** - Members of specific Working Groups;
- **@State Branch - [Name]** - Members of State Branches.

**(ii) Special roles:**

- **@Admin** - Technology WG managing server (full permissions);
- **@Moderator** - Designated members with moderation powers (kick/mute disruptive users);
- **@Bot** - NCAP bot and other automation.

(b) **Permission structure:**

**(i) Public channels (#general, #introductions, #announcements):**

- Viewable by @everyone (public);
- Postable by @Free Member and @Paid Member;
- #announcements read-only except Committee.

**(ii) Member channels (#meta, #questions, #resources):**

- Viewable/postable by @Free Member and @Paid Member;
- Some channels may restrict posting to @Paid Member only.

**(iii) Governance channels (#committee, #ncap-requests):**

- #committee viewable/postable by @Committee only;
- #ncap-requests viewable by @Paid Member, postable by NCAP bot and submitters.

**(iv) Working Group channels:**

- Viewable by all members (transparency);
- Postable by @Working Group - [Name] members and @Committee;
- Non-members can see discussions but channel focus on WG coordination.

**(v) State Branch channels:**

- Similar to Working Groups - viewable by all, postable by Branch members;
- Facilitates cross-branch learning and coordination.

(c) **Role assignment:**

- New members assigned @Free Member on join;
- @Paid Member role assigned when dues paid (Treasurer or Secretary updates);
- @Committee role assigned when elected;
- @Working Group roles assigned by Coordinator when member joins WG;
- @State Branch roles assigned by Branch Coordinator;
- Roles removed when membership lapses or person leaves role.

(3) **Community Space Moderation:**

(a) **Moderation principles:**

- Light touch - trust members to self-regulate;
- Intervene only when necessary (harassment, spam, violations of Professional Standards);
- Transparency - explain moderation actions publicly when possible;
- Appeals - members can appeal moderation decisions to Committee.

(b) **Moderator powers:**

- **Warning** - Private message explaining issue and requesting change;
- **Mute/timeout** - Temporary restriction on posting (hours to days);
- **Kick** - Remove from server (can rejoin if invited);
- **Ban** - Permanent removal (equivalent to membership termination under Rule 13).

(c) **Moderation process:**

- Member reports issue to moderator or posts in #meta;
- Moderator investigates (reviews messages, context);
- Moderator takes action (warning, mute, kick) or escalates to Committee;
- Serious violations (harassment, bigotry, threatening behavior) escalated immediately to Committee;
- Moderator logs action and rationale in private moderation channel;
- Member can appeal to Committee if they believe moderation was unjust.

(d) **Who moderates:**

- Committee members have moderation powers by default;
- Additional trusted members may be designated as @Moderators by Committee;
- Technology WG has admin access for technical management, not content moderation (unless also Committee/Moderator);
- Moderators should be diverse (not all same background/perspective) to avoid bias.

(4) **Community Space Best Practices:**

(a) **Keep it organized:**

- Don't create channels unnecessarily (channel bloat confuses newcomers);
- Archive inactive channels instead of deleting (preserves history);
- Use threads for in-depth discussions within channels (keeps main channel readable);
- Pin important messages (rules, guides, key decisions).

(b) **Encourage participation:**

- Welcome new members warmly;
- Ask questions, solicit input, create space for diverse voices;
- Recognize contributions publicly (shout-outs, thank-yous);
- Make it easy to get involved (clear pathways to Working Groups, campaigns);
- Model inclusive culture (respectful disagreement, listening, no gatekeeping).

(c) **Balance synchronous and asynchronous:**

- Community space is primarily asynchronous (post when convenient, respond when available);
- Use voice channels for synchronous meetings when needed;
- Don't expect instant responses (people have jobs, lives, time zones);
- Record or summarize important voice discussions for those who couldn't attend.

(d) **Reduce noise, signal ratio:**

- Off-topic chat belongs in #off-topic, not governance channels;
- Use reactions/emojis instead of "+1" posts;
- Thread lengthy discussions so they don't dominate;
- Edit messages to fix typos rather than delete and repost;
- Be concise and respectful of people's time.

### Rule 79: Data Security and Privacy

(1) **Data Security Responsibilities:**

(a) The Association handles sensitive member data and must protect it:

- Personal information (names, addresses, emails, phone numbers);
- Financial information (bank details, donation amounts, expenditure);
- Confidential communications (Committee discussions, discipline matters);
- Electoral data (voter files, contact lists).

(b) **Data security is everyone's responsibility:**

- Committee members must handle confidential information carefully;
- Technology WG implements technical safeguards;
- All members should follow good security practices (strong passwords, don't share accounts);
- Report security incidents immediately.

(2) **Technical Security Measures:**

(a) **Access controls:**

- Principle of least privilege (people only access what they need);
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA/2FA) for critical accounts (bank, hosting, admin panels);
- Strong, unique passwords for all accounts (use password manager);
- Regular review of access (remove permissions when people leave roles);
- Shared accounts avoided (each person has own login where possible).

(b) **Data encryption:**

- HTTPS for all websites (encrypt data in transit);
- Encrypted backups if containing confidential information;
- Secure file sharing (encrypted cloud storage, not email attachments for sensitive data);
- Use encrypted messaging (Signal, WhatsApp) for highly sensitive communications if needed.

(c) **System hardening:**

- Keep software updated (WordPress, Discord bots, operating systems);
- Use reputable, maintained tools (avoid abandoned or sketchy software);
- Regular backups tested for recoverability;
- Monitor for suspicious activity (unusual logins, system alerts);
- Separate production and development environments (don't test on live systems).

(3) **Privacy Compliance:**

(a) **Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) compliance:**

- Association collects personal information only for legitimate purposes;
- Members informed about what data collected and why;
- Data used only for stated purposes (no selling or sharing without consent);
- Data kept secure and accurate;
- Members can access and correct their personal information;
- Data deleted when no longer needed.

(b) **Privacy policy:**

- Association should publish privacy policy explaining:
  - What personal information is collected;
  - How it's used (membership admin, communications, electoral campaigns);
  - Who has access (Committee, relevant Working Groups);
  - How it's protected (security measures);
  - How to access or correct information;
  - How to opt out of communications.
- Privacy policy on website and provided to new members.

(c) **Data minimization:**

- Collect only what's necessary (don't ask for irrelevant information);
- Delete data when retention period expires (Rule 74);
- Be cautious about storing sensitive data (minimize where possible);
- If data breach occurs, fewer data = less damage.

(4) **Incident Response:**

(a) **Security incident types:**

- Data breach (unauthorized access to member information);
- Account compromise (password stolen, account hacked);
- Malware or ransomware;
- Accidental disclosure (sent confidential info to wrong person);
- Physical theft (laptop with data stolen).

(b) **Incident response process:**

- **Detect and contain:** Identify what happened, stop ongoing breach (change passwords, lock accounts);
- **Assess impact:** What data was exposed? How many people affected? How sensitive?
- **Notify:** Inform Committee immediately, notify affected members if their data compromised;
- **Remedy:** Fix vulnerability that caused breach, restore from backups if needed;
- **Report:** Notify relevant authorities if legally required (serious data breaches must be reported to OAIC);
- **Learn:** Document what happened and how to prevent recurrence.

(c) **Transparency about incidents:**

- If data breach affects members, inform them promptly and honestly;
- Explain what happened, what data was exposed, what Association is doing;
- Offer assistance (e.g., advice on changing passwords, monitoring for identity theft);
- Don't hide or minimize breaches - this destroys trust.

### Rule 80: Accessibility and Inclusion

(1) **Digital Accessibility:**

(a) The Association's digital infrastructure should be accessible to people with disabilities:

**(i) Website accessibility:**

- Compliant with WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards;
- Keyboard navigable (don't require mouse);
- Screen reader compatible (alt text for images, semantic HTML, ARIA labels);
- Color contrast meets standards (readable for color-blind users);
- Text resizable without breaking layout;
- Captions or transcripts for video/audio content.

**(ii) Document accessibility:**

- PDFs should be accessible (tagged, text-selectable, logical reading order);
- Provide alternative formats if requested (plain text, large print);
- Use clear, simple language (Plain Language principles);
- Structure documents with headings, lists, tables appropriately.

**(iii) Community space accessibility:**

- Avoid reliance on color alone to convey information;
- Use clear channel names and descriptions;
- Accommodate different time zones (async communication, record meetings);
- Provide text alternatives for visual content.

(b) **Testing and feedback:**

- Periodically test website with accessibility tools (WAVE, axe DevTools);
- Solicit feedback from members with disabilities;
- Prioritize fixing accessibility issues when identified;
- Include accessibility in design and development process (not afterthought).

(2) **Participation Barriers:**

(a) **Reduce barriers to participation:**

**(i) Financial barriers:**

- Free Membership option for those who can't afford dues;
- No requirement to attend in-person events (remote participation via video/audio);
- Provide stipends or expense reimbursement for volunteers if needed (e.g., travel to campaign).

**(ii) Time barriers:**

- Asynchronous participation options (NCAP, community space, written contributions);
- Meeting recordings or summaries for those who can't attend live;
- Flexible Working Group and State Branch structures (contribute as available);
- Don't penalize members who can't commit large amounts of time.

**(iii) Geographic barriers:**

- Online-first organization (remote participation is norm, not exception);
- State Branches coordinate locally but integrate nationally;
- Video conferencing for Committee meetings and General Meetings;
- In-person events are optional extras, not requirements.

**(iv) Language and literacy barriers:**

- Use plain language in constitution, policies, communications;
- Provide summaries of complex documents;
- Offer translation assistance if members request (multilingual volunteers);
- Visual aids and diagrams where helpful.

**(v) Disability accommodations:**

- Caption video content;
- Provide transcripts for audio;
- Accessible meeting venues if in-person events held;
- Flexibility for members with cognitive, physical, or mental health disabilities.

(b) **Proactive accommodation:**

- Ask members what they need (don't assume);
- Budget for accommodation costs (captioning, translation, venue accessibility);
- Normalize requesting accommodations (not special or burdensome);
- Improve systems based on feedback (if accommodation requested, make it standard).

(3) **Inclusive Design:**

(a) **Design for diversity from the start:**

- Don't design for "average user" then retrofit accessibility;
- Consider diverse users: disabilities, languages, time zones, tech literacy, economic situations;
- Universal design benefits everyone (captions help non-native speakers, keyboard nav helps power users).

(b) **Iterative improvement:**

- Accessibility and inclusion are ongoing, not one-time checklist;
- Regularly review systems and processes for barriers;
- Listen to feedback and act on it;
- Technology and standards evolve - keep up.

(c) **Document accessibility efforts:**

- Accessibility statement on website (what Association does, how to request accommodation);
- Include accessibility in annual reports;
- Celebrate improvements (show commitment to inclusion).

### Rule 81: Technology Working Group Responsibilities

(1) **Technology WG Scope:**

(a) The Technology Working Group is responsible for:

- Maintaining and improving Association's technology infrastructure;
- Implementing NCAP system and governance tools;
- Managing community space (Discord server admin);
- Website hosting and technical maintenance;
- Data security and backup systems;
- Evaluating new tools and platforms;
- Training and supporting members with technology;
- Documentation of systems and processes.

(2) **Technology WG Coordination:**

(a) **Relationship with other bodies:**

- Reports to Committee on infrastructure status, issues, proposals;
- Collaborates with Communications WG on website content and social media;
- Supports Campaigns WG with campaign technology needs;
- Assists Secretary with documentation systems;
- Works with Treasurer on financial software and NCAP integration.

(b) **Decision-making:**

- Technology WG operates under NCAP for routine decisions (software updates, minor configuration changes);
- Major changes (platform migrations, new systems) require Committee approval;
- Emergency actions (security incidents, outages) can be taken immediately and documented retrospectively.

(3) **Technology Skills and Knowledge:**

(a) **Desired Technology WG skills:**

- System administration (server management, backups, security);
- Web development (HTML/CSS/JavaScript, static sites or CMS);
- Bot development (Python, JavaScript for Discord bots);
- Database management (SQL, data modeling);
- DevOps (automation, CI/CD, monitoring);
- Security and privacy (encryption, access controls, compliance).

(b) **Not all skills required:**

- Technology WG can have members with different specializations;
- Learn as needed (documentation, online resources, community support);
- Prioritize reliability and security over cutting-edge features;
- Ask for help (external consultants, open source communities).

(4) **Technology Documentation:**

(a) **Technology WG must document:**

- System architecture (how everything connects);
- Setup and configuration (how to recreate systems from scratch);
- Admin procedures (how to perform common tasks);
- Incident response playbooks (what to do when things break);
- Code repositories (with clear READMEs and comments);
- Credentials and access (securely stored, multiple people have emergency access).

(b) **Documentation enables succession:**

- If Technology WG Coordinator leaves, someone else can take over;
- Systems don't depend on one person's knowledge;
- Onboarding new Technology WG members is faster;
- Association isn't held hostage by technical lock-in.
